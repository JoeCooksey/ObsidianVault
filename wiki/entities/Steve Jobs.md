# Steve Jobs

**Steven Paul Jobs** (1955–2011) — co-founder and CEO of Apple; co-founder of Pixar; the defining figure of the personal computing and consumer electronics revolutions. Widely considered the greatest product visionary in tech history. His biography by Walter Isaacson (2011), written at Jobs's own request, remains the definitive account of his life.

## Career Timeline
- **1976** — Co-founded Apple Computer with Steve Wozniak (and Ron Wayne) in the family garage in Cupertino
- **1977** — Apple II: first mass-market personal computer
- **1980** — Apple IPO; Jobs becomes millionaire at 25
- **1984** — Macintosh: first GUI-driven personal computer for consumers
- **1985** — Ousted from Apple by John Sculley (CEO Jobs himself recruited)
- **1985** — Founded NeXT Computer
- **1986** — Bought Pixar from George Lucas for $5M
- **1995** — Toy Story: world's first fully CGI feature film
- **1996** — Pixar IPO makes Jobs a billionaire; Apple acquires NeXT for $429M
- **1997** — Returns to Apple as CEO; launches "Think Different" campaign
- **1998** — iMac: saves Apple from near-bankruptcy
- **2001** — iPod: 1,000 songs in your pocket
- **2003** — iTunes Store: 99¢/song; renegotiated the music industry
- **2003** — Diagnosed with pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor; delayed surgery 9 months
- **2007** — iPhone: the most consequential consumer product launch since the Model T
- **2008** — App Store: transformed software distribution
- **2010** — iPad: post-PC device category
- **2011** — Resigned as CEO August 24; died October 5

## Defining Characteristics

### Reality Distortion Field
Jobs's most documented trait — an almost supernatural ability to make people believe the impossible was achievable. Coin by Bud Tribble (1981). Apple engineers built hardware in six months that they initially insisted would take two years. Double-edged: drove extraordinary achievement but also led to willful denial of medical reality. See: [[Reality Distortion Field]]

### Black-and-White Worldview
No middle ground. Products were "insanely great" or "shit." People were "geniuses" or "bozos." This binary lens was simultaneously the engine of his perfectionism and the source of his documented cruelty to colleagues.

### Humanities × Sciences
Jobs's central belief: the greatest innovations live at the intersection of technology and liberal arts. He cited his calligraphy class at Reed College as the direct origin of the Mac's beautiful typography — a liberal arts course that shaped the most important personal computer of its era.

### Product Absolutism
He ran Apple as a product company, not a profit company. When given a choice between a better product and a higher margin, he consistently chose the product. His logic: great products drive great profits; profit-first reasoning drives mediocre products.

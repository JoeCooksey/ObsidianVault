# Rome & Vatican City — Family Tourist Guide (May 11–19, 2026)

**Trip profile:** 3 people — Joe + parents (aged 60 and 52). 9 nights based in Rome. Vatican City as the centrepiece stop. May is ideal: mild 20–25°C days, 10–12 hours of sun, smaller crowds than July–August.

---

## 1. Before You Go — Logistics

### Flights & Airport
- Rome is served by **Fiumicino (FCO)** — Leonardo da Vinci International. Most intercontinental flights land here.
- Ciampino (CIA) handles low-cost European carriers.
- **FCO → Rome Centre**: Leonardo Express train (30 min, €14/person) or taxi (fixed €50 from FCO, no meter games).

### Where to Stay — Best Neighbourhoods

| Neighbourhood | Why it works for this trip | Typical price tier |
|---|---|---|
| **Historic Centre (Centro Storico)** | Walk to Pantheon, Piazza Navona, Trevi — zero transport needed | €€€ |
| **Monti** | 5-min walk to Colosseum; quieter, fewer tourists, great trattorias | €€ |
| **Prati** (Vatican side) | 10-min walk to Vatican; calm, residential, excellent restaurants | €€ |
| **Trastevere** | Charming medieval streets; evening atmosphere; slightly further | €€ |

**Recommendation for this family:** Prati neighbourhood or Historic Centre. Prati keeps Vatican day simple (walk over) and the area is flat and easy to navigate with older parents.

**Vetted property types:** Boutique hotels with lifts are essential — confirm lift access when booking. Look for hotels with included breakfast to simplify mornings.

### What to Book *Before Arriving* (Critical)
1. **Vatican Museums + Sistine Chapel tickets** — book minimum 4–6 weeks in advance; May is peak season and slots sell out. Pre-book at the official Vatican Museums site or a trusted vendor (Viator, GetYourGuide).
2. **Colosseum entry** — book online; same-day queues can be 2+ hours.
3. **Borghese Gallery** — entry is strictly by timed reservation (capacity-limited by Italian law); book immediately.
4. **Vatican Gardens tour** (optional) — limited capacity, books out weeks ahead.
5. **Pompeii or day trip tours** if chosen.

## 2. May in Rome — Weather & Packing

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Daytime temperature | 20–25°C (68–77°F) |
| Evening temperature | ~14°C (57°F) |
| Sunshine hours/day | 10–12 hours |
| Rain days | ~11 in May (brief showers, not all-day) |

**What to pack:**
- Light cotton trousers/jeans + t-shirts for daytime
- Light cardigan or layer for evenings and air-conditioned museums
- Comfortable walking shoes — this is the single most important item
- Compact umbrella (essential, not optional)
- SPF 30+ sunscreen + sunglasses
- Scarf or wrap (women): doubles as Vatican modesty cover for shoulders

**Vatican dress code (non-negotiable):**
- Knees and shoulders must be covered for ALL visitors entering St. Peter's Basilica and Vatican Museums
- Men: long trousers, shirt with sleeves
- Women: skirts/trousers below the knee; sleeves covering shoulders (or carry a scarf to wrap)
- No sleeveless tops, crop tops, shorts, miniskirts, or hats for men inside
- Comfortable closed-toe shoes essential if climbing the dome (hundreds of narrow steps)

## 3. Senior-Friendly Planning Principles

Your parents (60 and 52) are perfectly capable of a full Rome experience — these principles make it excellent rather than exhausting:

1. **Max 2–3 major sights per day.** Quality over quantity. Rome fatigue is real — cobblestones and heat accumulate.
2. **Morning sightseeing, afternoon rest.** Book museums for 9–10am. Use 1–3pm for lunch + rest (many Italians do the same). Resume evening walks when it cools.
3. **Alternate heavy walking days with easy days.** Day at Colosseum (lots of uneven ground) → followed by leisurely Trastevere stroll.
4. **Book seated guided tours.** Golf cart tours, minibus day trips, and private guides cut walking distance dramatically while adding depth.
5. **Transport tactically.** Rome's metro is fast for the Colosseum (Line B, Colosseo stop) and Vatican (Line A, Ottaviano stop). Taxis and the app **Free Now** (or itTaxi) are affordable for cross-city jumps.
6. **Accessibility notes:** Vatican Museums has wheelchair-friendly routes. Colosseum has lift access to upper levels — confirm when booking. Most major churches and piazzas are ground-level accessible.
7. **Carry water always.** Rome has hundreds of free drinking fountains (nasoni) throughout the city — safe to drink, cold, and refreshing.

## 4. Day-by-Day Itinerary (May 11–19)

### Day 1 — Sunday May 11 | Arrival & Orientation
**Theme:** Arrive, settle in, gentle introduction to Rome.

- Arrive, check in, freshen up
- Late afternoon: gentle walk around your neighbourhood — find your nearest café and gelateria
- **Piazza Navona** (baroque fountain square — no tickets, just wander) — ideal first Rome impression
- **Campo de' Fiori** — lively square; browse if market is still running
- Dinner: Find a neighbourhood trattoria; order *cacio e pepe* (Roman pasta classic) and local house wine
- Early night — you need sleep after travel

**No museums, no queues. Just absorb Rome.** The city itself is the attraction today.

---

### Day 2 — Monday May 12 | Ancient Rome
**Theme:** Colosseum + Roman Forum + Palatine Hill + Capitoline Museums

- **9:00am:** Colosseum (pre-booked skip-the-line). Arrive early — it gets crowded and hot by midday. Book a tour with an arena floor option if available — far more dramatic than upper levels alone.
- **11:00am:** Walk directly into **Roman Forum** (included with Colosseum ticket). The heart of the ancient city — Senate, temples, triumphal arches. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
- **12:30pm:** Climb **Palatine Hill** (also included) for panoramic views over the Forum. This is the hill where Rome's emperors lived.
- **1:30pm:** Lunch near the area — try Pizzarium or a trattoria in Testaccio neighbourhood (authentic Roman working-class food).
- **3:30pm:** **Capitoline Museums** (world's oldest public museums; outstanding Roman statuary; Marcus Aurelius equestrian statue; breathtaking terrace view of the Forum). Book in advance or buy on-site — usually no huge queue.
- **Evening:** Wander to **Circus Maximus** (free, just walk through), then dinner in Testaccio

**Senior tip:** Capitoline Museums has a lift. The Forum is the most tiring part (uneven stone, uphill) — take it slowly and carry water.

---

### Day 3 — Tuesday May 13 | Vatican City *(The Main Event)*
**Theme:** Vatican Museums → Sistine Chapel → St. Peter's Basilica → St. Peter's Square

This is the centrepiece of the whole trip. Dedicate the full day and arrive rested.

**Recommended approach: Pre-booked guided tour with skip-the-line entry.**
- A guided group tour (3–4 hours) gives you a dedicated entrance, an expert commentary through the museums, and guaranteed Sistine Chapel access.
- Cost: approx €50–80/person for a reputable guided tour. Worth every cent vs. hours queuing.

**Vatican Museums sequence (highlights):**
1. Pinecone Courtyard (outdoor, first stop)
2. Gallery of Maps (54 painted maps of Italian regions — spectacular)
3. Gallery of Tapestries
4. Raphael Rooms (School of Athens — one of the greatest paintings ever made)
5. **Sistine Chapel** (Michelangelo's ceiling — Genesis scenes; and the newly restored Last Judgment wall, restoration completed March 26, 2026)

**Note on Sistine Chapel:** Photography is officially prohibited (enforced inconsistently). Be quiet and respectful — guards will remove disruptive visitors.

- After Vatican Museums: Cross into **St. Peter's Basilica** (free entry, separate from museums; join the security line)
  - Inside: Michelangelo's *La Pietà* (right nave, behind glass) — one of the most moving sculptures in human history
  - High altar and Bernini's bronze baldachin
  - Free to explore; treasure museum is optional (€8)

- **Dome climb (optional but spectacular):**
  - Lift to first terrace (€8), then stairs to top (€6 without lift) — narrowing spiral staircase
  - 360° panoramic view of Rome from the top of the dome
  - Physical note for 60-year-old parent: 320+ steps after the lift section; assess fitness and pace. Not accessible by wheelchair. Perfectly doable for a fit 60-year-old, just slow and steady.

- **St. Peter's Square:** Stand at the centre to see Bernini's colonnade wrap around you — designed to symbolise the Church embracing the world.

**Afternoon:** Rest at a café in Prati; the Vatican area is exhausting. Gelato mandatory.
**Evening:** Dinner in Prati (excellent neighbourhood for traditional Roman food without tourist markup).

**Dress code reminder:** Bring your modesty layers. Guards at St. Peter's will turn away visitors showing shoulders or knees. Women: scarf or shawl. Men: no shorts.

---

### Day 4 — Wednesday May 14 | Historic Centre
**Theme:** Pantheon → Trevi Fountain → Spanish Steps → Piazza del Popolo

- **9:00am:** **Pantheon** (book timed entry online — €5; free for under-18s). Best ancient building ever built. The dome's unreinforced concrete has stood 2,000 years and still has the world's largest unreinforced concrete dome. Arrive early before tour groups swarm.
- **10:30am:** Wander the streets around the Pantheon — best area for coffee (Sant'Eustachio il Caffè, a Roman institution, 5-minute walk).
- **11:30am:** **Trevi Fountain** — busiest in the morning; still impressive. Toss a coin (tradition: one coin = return to Rome; two = fall in love in Rome). Pro tip: Return at 10pm when crowds thin dramatically — magical at night.
- **12:30pm:** Lunch near Piazza Navona.
- **2:30pm:** **Spanish Steps** (138 steps; sit and people-watch). Walk up via the steps or take a side street — flat approach from the top is available for anyone struggling with steps.
- **3:30pm:** Stroll down to **Via Condotti** (Rome's luxury shopping street — even just window shopping is fun).
- **4:30pm:** Walk or taxi to **Piazza del Popolo** — twin churches, obelisk, impressive piazza; free.
- **Evening:** Aperitivo hour (5–7pm) at any bar — Italy's best pre-dinner tradition. Negroni or Spritz with free snacks is the norm. Then dinner.

---

### Day 5 — Thursday May 15 | Day Trip: Tivoli
**Theme:** Roman Emperor's escape villa + Renaissance water gardens

Tivoli is 30 km east of Rome — 40–60 minute regional train (€4 one-way). A perfect day trip for the family: beautiful, manageable, and not as physically demanding as Rome's cobblestone centre.

**Two World Heritage Sites in one day:**

**Villa d'Este and Tivoli Gardens (morning):**
- 16th-century Renaissance villa with spectacular terraced gardens
- 500+ fountains, mechanical water organs, grand avenues — genuinely breathtaking
- Gardens are extensive but mostly flat terraces; some steps between levels
- Entry: ~€12–15

**Hadrian's Villa / Villa Adriana (afternoon):**
- Emperor Hadrian's vast 2nd-century estate — effectively a private city with pools, temples, theatres
- More ruins-style walking; flatter than Rome's hills
- Entry: ~€8

**Logistics:** Take the train from Roma Tiburtina station. Taxi between the two villas in Tivoli (~€10–15). Return by 7pm for dinner in Rome.

**Senior rating:** ★★★★★ — highly recommended for parents. Less crowded than Rome, beautiful scenery, manageable walking, no intense queuing.

---

### Day 6 — Friday May 16 | Borghese Gallery + Trastevere Evening
**Theme:** World-class sculpture gallery (morning) + Rome's most charming neighbourhood (evening)

- **9:00am:** **Borghese Gallery** (strictly pre-booked timed entry — 2-hour slot, limited visitors, one of the most peaceful museum experiences in Italy)
  - Bernini's sculptures (*Apollo and Daphne*, *The Rape of Proserpina*, *David*) — widely considered the greatest baroque sculptures in existence. Even if you've never been moved by sculpture, you will be here.
  - Canova's *Pauline Bonaparte as Venus Victrix* — extraordinary
  - Entry: ~€15 + €2 booking fee; guided audio available
- **11:30am:** Walk through **Villa Borghese gardens** (Rome's central park — free, beautiful, peaceful)
- **1:00pm:** Lunch in Parioli or taxi/bus to Trastevere
- **Afternoon rest** — hotel or café
- **4:30pm:** **Trastevere** neighbourhood (cross the Tiber on foot or by tram)
  - Medieval streets, ivy-covered buildings, authentic Roman atmosphere
  - **Basilica di Santa Maria in Trastevere** — 12th-century mosaics; one of Rome's oldest churches; free
  - Browse the neighbourhood at leisure — this is Rome as it actually lives, not Rome for tourists
- **Evening:** Dinner in Trastevere — the neighbourhood's restaurants are excellent and the atmosphere is perfect. Try *supplì* (Roman fried rice balls) as a starter.

---

### Day 7 — Saturday May 17 | Day Trip: Ostia Antica
**Theme:** Ancient Rome's harbour city — Pompeii-level ruins with a fraction of the crowds

Ostia Antica is 30 km from Rome, reachable by Metro Line B to Laurentina then bus, or direct regional train from Roma Ostiense (45 min, ~€3). An underrated gem — archaeologically comparable to Pompeii but far less visited and easier to access.

**What you'll see:**
- Entire ancient city preserved: streets, shops, apartments, temples, theatre, bathhouses, mosaics
- **Theatre of Ostia** — 2,000-year-old theatre still used for summer performances
- Ancient black-and-white floor mosaics in the Baths of Neptune
- The Forum, Capitol temple, ancient apartment blocks (insulae)
- Great for walking and photographing without massive crowds
- Entry: ~€12

**Physical note:** The site is large but flat. Good walking shoes and hats essential. Carry water — limited facilities inside.

**Why not Pompeii?** Pompeii is excellent but 75 minutes from Rome by high-speed train plus a bus from Naples. It's a longer, more exhausting day. Ostia Antica gives a comparable ancient city experience with far less logistics and travel. If the family has energy and interest, Pompeii can replace Ostia Antica (book a guided day tour from Rome via GetYourGuide or Viator).

---

### Day 8 — Sunday May 18 | Castel Sant'Angelo + Shopping + Leisure
**Theme:** Final big sight + leisure day

- **9:30am:** **Castel Sant'Angelo** (15-minute walk from Vatican; originally Hadrian's mausoleum, later a papal fortress, now a museum)
  - Rooftop terrace: the best free-ish view of the Tiber and Rome skyline
  - Walk the ancient ramparts, see the papal apartments, the angel statue on top
  - Entry: €14; can get very crowded — arrive early
- **11:30am:** Walk across **Ponte Sant'Angelo** (the bridge lined with Bernini's angels — free, beautiful)
- **Afternoon:** Choose your pleasure:
  - **Shopping:** Via del Corso (high street), Campo de' Fiori market (Mon–Sat mornings), Porta Portese flea market (Sunday mornings — massive open-air market, great for souvenirs and character)
  - **Church gems (free):** San Luigi dei Francesi (Caravaggio paintings — stunning; near Pantheon), Santa Maria della Vittoria (Bernini's Ecstasy of Saint Teresa)
  - **Gelato tour:** Try 3–4 different gelaterias and compare (Giolitti near Pantheon; Fatamorgana for creative flavours)
- **Evening:** Revisit Trevi Fountain at night (10pm+ — far fewer people, lit beautifully), then a farewell dinner at your favourite restaurant from the week.

### Day 9 — Monday May 19 | Departure
- Morning leisure — final espresso, last pastel cornetto at a Roman bar
- Pack, check out
- Transfer to airport (FCO: Leonardo Express from Roma Termini, departs every 30 min)

## 5. Getting Around Rome

### Metro (Metropolitana)
- **Line A:** Spanish Steps (Spagna), Vatican area (Ottaviano), Termini
- **Line B:** Colosseum (Colosseo), Termini, Laurentina
- Single ticket: €1.50 (valid 100 minutes, one metro ride)
- **Tap & Go:** Contactless cards, Apple Pay, Google Pay now work on all metro and buses — no ticket machine needed
- Buy a 48hr or 72hr pass if you plan heavy metro use

### Taxi / Apps
- Official Rome taxis are **white** with a taxi light on the roof
- Flat fare from FCO to Rome centre: **€50** (insist on this before getting in)
- Within Rome: metered; short trips €8–15
- **Free Now app** (formerly mytaxi): best app for hailing official licensed taxis in Rome
- **Uber** in Rome: operates as Uber Black (licensed professional drivers, not rideshare) via the itTaxi network. Works but more expensive than local taxis.
- **Never use unofficial drivers** who approach you at the airport or train stations

### Walking
- Most of central Rome's landmarks are within a 20–30 minute walk of each other
- The Historic Centre is pedestrianised in many areas — no cars
- Cobblestones (sampietrini) are Rome's charm but also ankle-twist risk — solid walking shoes are non-negotiable
- Flat areas: along the Tiber, Piazza Navona district, Prati. Hilly areas: Trastevere, Aventine, Pincian Hill (Borghese)

### Hop-On Hop-Off Bus
- Good orientation tool on Day 1–2 for the family; shows geography without walking
- ~€25/person for 24 hours
- Not the best way to see Rome but excellent for parents on tiring days

## 6. Food Guide

### Roman Cuisine Essentials — Order These
| Dish | What it is | Where |
|---|---|---|
| **Cacio e pepe** | Pasta with Pecorino Romano + black pepper (deceptively complex) | Any trattoria |
| **Carbonara** | Pasta, guanciale, egg yolk, Pecorino — no cream (ever) | Any trattoria |
| **Amatriciana** | Tomato, guanciale, Pecorino | Any trattoria |
| **Supplì** | Fried rice balls with mozzarella centre | Street food / pizza shops |
| **Pizza al taglio** | Pizza by the slice, sold by weight | Forno bakeries |
| **Artichokes (carciofi)** | Jewish-style deep fried or Roman-style braised | Jewish Quarter trattorias |
| **Gelato** | Never from a place with neon-bright towering mounds — real gelato is stored in covered metal containers |

### Recommended Restaurants
- **Armando al Pantheon** (near Pantheon) — Roman institution, reserve in advance
- **Er Faciolaro** (near Pantheon) — family trattoria, rustic, excellent value
- **Osteria dell'Angelo** (Prati) — neighbourhood classic, perfect for Vatican day dinner
- **Forno Campo de' Fiori** — best pizza bianca and mortadella sandwiches in Rome
- **Trapizzino** (Testaccio) — street food, triangular dough pockets stuffed with braised meats
- **Dal Cavalier Gino** (near Pantheon) — seasonal Roman dishes, gnocchi Thursday, baccalà Friday

### Practical Food Tips
- **Breakfast:** Stand at the bar — it's how Romans do it, half the price of sitting. Espresso + cornetto (croissant) = €2–3 standing vs. €6+ sitting.
- **Lunch:** Many good restaurants offer a fixed-price *pranzo* lunch menu (€10–15 for 2 courses + water/wine) — excellent value.
- **Dinner:** Romans eat late — restaurants fill up 8–9pm. Booking ahead is advisable at popular spots.
- **Avoid:** Restaurants with pictures on the menus directly on major tourist squares (Piazza Navona, Trevi). Walk one or two streets behind and quality doubles, price halves.
- **Water:** Rome's tap water and street fountains (nasoni) are safe to drink. Ask for *acqua del rubinetto* at restaurants (free tap water).
- **Coffee culture:** Espresso = coffee. *Americano* is available but marks you as a tourist. Cappuccino is acceptable only before noon.

## 7. Practical Essentials

### Money
- Euro (€) — Italy
- Cards accepted almost everywhere in 2026; contactless is universal
- Keep €50–100 cash for small trattorias, street food, markets, and tips
- ATMs (bancomat): use bank ATMs, avoid private ATMs (high fees); withdraw larger amounts to minimise fees

### Tipping
- Not mandatory or expected in Italy the way it is in the US
- Restaurants: leave €1–2/person if you were happy — locals rarely tip more
- Taxis: round up to nearest euro
- Tour guides: €5–10/person for a good guided tour is generous and appreciated

### Italian Phrases (Pocket Essentials)
| Italian | English |
|---|---|
| *Buongiorno* | Good morning / Hello (before noon) |
| *Buonasera* | Good evening (after noon) |
| *Per favore* | Please |
| *Grazie* | Thank you |
| *Mi scusi* | Excuse me |
| *Dov'è il bagno?* | Where is the bathroom? |
| *Il conto, per favore* | The bill, please |
| *Quanto costa?* | How much does it cost? |
| *Un caffè, per favore* | One coffee, please |
| *Non capisco* | I don't understand |

### Health & Comfort
- European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) or equivalent travel insurance — essential
- Any prescription medications: bring more than you need + prescriptions in writing
- Comfortable shoes break-in before the trip — do not arrive with new shoes
- Sunscreen reapply after lunch — Rome sun in May is strong
- Rome in May: no mosquito problem yet (July–August is the issue)

### Safety
- Rome is a safe city for tourists in all areas mentioned in this guide
- Standard city vigilance: watch bags in crowded areas (Colosseum, Trevi, metro), beware of pickpockets on Metro Line A
- Use money belts or keep cards in front pockets in busy areas
- Emergency number: **112** (European universal emergency)

## 8. Key Findings Summary

1. **Vatican Museums must be pre-booked** — May peak season means 3+ hour queues for unprepared visitors; skip-the-line guided tour saves the day
2. **Sistine Chapel Last Judgment was restored** — cleaning completed March 26, 2026 and the original vibrant colours are now visible for the first time in decades
3. **May is the best month** for this trip — mild temperatures, no summer heat, longer days, less crowded than July–August
4. **Borghese Gallery and Colosseum** require advance booking — non-negotiable
5. **Prati neighbourhood** is the optimal base — walking distance to Vatican, flat, excellent restaurants, less touristy
6. **Tivoli day trip** is the best choice for a family with older parents — two UNESCO World Heritage Sites, manageable walking, accessible train connection
7. **Ostia Antica** is Rome's most underrated day trip — Pompeii-scale ruins, 30 km away, tiny fraction of the visitors
8. **Cobblestones are the enemy** — comfortable, broken-in walking shoes are the single most important packing item; the difference between a great trip and a painful one

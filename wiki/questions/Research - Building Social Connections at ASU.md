# Research - Building Social Connections at ASU

**Question**: What are the best methods for building social connections at ASU, ranked by effectiveness?

---

## 8 Key Findings

### 1. Seat proximity is the single strongest predictor of college friendship
A 2022 Frontiers in Psychology study (n=235 undergraduates) found that classroom seat proximity increases friendship nominations by 3–5×. Students in nearby seats are far more likely to name each other as friends than students in distant seats, independent of any other variable. The mechanism is the **mere exposure effect** (Zajonc, 1968): repeated visual exposure alone increases liking before any interaction occurs.

### 2. Dorm proximity is even stronger than classroom proximity
Classic dormitory research (Festinger et al.; replicated multiple times) found that 41% of dorm students became friends with their immediate next-door neighbor, compared to fewer than 25% for a few doors down, and only ~10% for end-of-hall residents. Friendship formation is essentially a proximity function in the first semester. For freshmen in university housing, simply opening your door is a social strategy.

### 3. Friendship takes ~50–200 hours of contact — context dictates whether you accumulate it
Jeffrey Hall's 2019 landmark study on friendship formation:
- **Acquaintance → Casual friend**: ~50 hours
- **Casual friend → Friend**: ~100–150 hours
- **Friend → Close friend**: 200+ hours
- Casual friendships form in 3–9 weeks; close friendships require 3–4 months

Most one-off social encounters (parties, events) never accumulate enough hours. The question is: *which context reliably accumulates 50–200 hours with the same people?*

### 4. Small talk weakens relationships; shared stakes and real conversation build them
Hall's same research found that friend pairs who engaged in small talk became more distant over time, while friends who caught up about their lives, had serious conversations, or engaged playfully became closer. This directly argues for shared-mission contexts (project teams, study groups) over passive hangout contexts.

### 5. ASU has 1,000+ clubs — but the key is sub-team involvement, not attendance
ASU's Sun Devil Central lists 1,000+ registered student organizations. Simply attending general meetings has low friendship ROI. The upgrade is joining a project sub-team or committee within a club. A project team of 5–10 people meeting 4–6 hrs/week accumulates 50+ hours within a semester — enough for genuine friendship. Relevant for Joe: IEEE ASU has 600+ members but has sub-project teams in power electronics, embedded systems, PCB design; Solar Devils divides by car subsystem (electrical, battery, shell).

### 6. Technical project clubs double as career capital — uniquely high ROI for EE students
Most social methods have pure social value. Engineering project clubs (IEEE, Solar Devils, Formula SAE) simultaneously generate:
- Friendships (repeated contact + shared mission)
- Technical skills (real hardware on resume)
- Professional network (future internship referrals)
- Faculty connections (project advisors → research recommendations)
This unique 4x ROI makes them S-tier for EE students in a way they wouldn't be for other majors.

### 7. The first 2 weeks have a unique social window — it closes
Research on freshman social networks consistently shows the maximum receptivity window is weeks 1–2. During this period, everyone is in "meet people" mode with no established group. Existing research (Weeks et al.) shows that social groups stabilize by week 6–8, after which joining requires penetrating an established group rather than forming organically. The ASU Welcome Week and first week of classes are the highest-leverage social opportunity of the entire year.

### 8. Online students face real structural barriers — digital community is the partial fix
Joe is studying online/hybrid at ASU from Livermore, CA. The proximity mechanisms (dorms, seat adjacency) are structurally inaccessible. However:
- ASU Discord servers (unofficial, major-organized) replicate the "same people repeatedly" effect digitally
- On-campus visits (club events, makerspace trips) compress social exposure in high-density windows
- Study groups formed via Discord with eventual in-person meetups bridge the gap
- The strategy becomes: maximize on-campus presence for the high-value weekly contexts (IEEE project meeting, one class section), then maintain digitally

## The Formula

> **Best friendship context = High contact hours + Shared mission + Same people repeatedly**

Ranked methods:
- **S**: Technical project club sub-team, study group, first-year dorm floor
- **A**: Rec sports team, campus job, orientation week (convert to regular context)
- **B**: Club general meetings (upgrade to sub-team), Greek life (high cost), campus events
- **C**: Large lectures (proximity helps; add deliberate study group), ASU Discord
- **D**: Parties/bar scene (too transient), cold DMs

# Research: Electrolyte Supplements — Are They Needed, Best Brands, Best Practices

## Query
Are electrolyte supplements truly needed? Which are the best brands? What are the best practices for using them?

## 8-Key Findings

**1. Most people don't need them — the industry overstates the need.**
Unless you exercise >60–75 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity, sweat heavily, are ill, or eat low-carb, a balanced diet + plain water provides all the electrolytes you need. The global average sodium intake is already 4.3g/day — more than double WHO's 2g recommendation. Adding more is potentially harmful for sedentary people.

**2. The 60–75 minute threshold is the evidence-based line.**
Below that threshold at normal intensity, there is no scientific evidence that electrolyte supplements improve performance or hydration. Above it — especially in heat or humidity — sodium replacement becomes genuinely necessary to prevent hyponatremia and performance decline.

**3. Sodium is the dominant electrolyte to replace; potassium and magnesium matter secondarily.**
Sweat is primarily sodium and chloride. Potassium and magnesium losses are real but smaller; they matter more for cramp prevention and energy metabolism than acute hydration. A product that omits magnesium entirely (like Liquid IV) is missing a meaningful recovery component.

**4. Redmond Re-Lyte is the best overall brand for most athletes.**
Rationale: 810mg sodium, 400mg potassium (2:1 Na:K ratio — matches physiological sweat composition better than LMNT's 5:1), uses magnesium malate (superior absorption vs oxide), no sugar, no artificial sweeteners, third-party tested, affordable (~$1.00/serving). Best fit for general training use.

**5. LMNT is best for heavy sweaters, keto athletes, and sodium-prioritized use.**
1,000mg sodium is the highest on the market in a clean formula. Sugar-free, no artificial dyes, stevia only. The 5:1 Na:K ratio makes it sodium-dominant — right for keto dieters losing sodium via insulin suppression or endurance athletes in extreme heat. Overpowering for casual exercisers.

**6. Keto/low-carb athletes have legitimately elevated electrolyte needs.**
Reduced insulin from carb restriction causes the kidneys to excrete more sodium and potassium. Days 1–4 of keto are most acute. Athletes compound this with sweat losses. This is one clear use case where electrolyte supplementation transitions from "optional" to "strongly recommended."

**7. Liquid IV and Gatorade are the worst value propositions — sugar-heavy, marketing-heavy.**
Liquid IV contains 11g added sugar per serving with no magnesium. Gatorade contains 21–36g sugar. Both are formulated primarily for glucose delivery and commercial appeal, not electrolyte science. The sugar content defeats the purpose for anyone not doing 2+ hour endurance events requiring glycogen replenishment.

**8. Third-party testing (NSF Sport, Informed Sport) is the key quality signal.**
Most electrolyte powders are not independently verified for label accuracy or contamination. For competitive athletes subject to drug testing, NSF Sport certification (Thorne Hydration, Transparent Labs Hydrate) is non-negotiable. For everyone else, it's still the clearest quality proxy available.

## Priority Action for Joe
Given regular cycling + desk student lifestyle:
- **Normal training days (<60 min)**: no supplement needed; salt food to taste
- **Long rides or hot-weather rides (>60 min)**: Re-Lyte or LMNT during/after
- **Low-carb phase if pursued**: LMNT daily, prioritize potassium foods
- **Budget DIY**: 1/8 tsp sea salt + lemon + water = ~$0.02/serving

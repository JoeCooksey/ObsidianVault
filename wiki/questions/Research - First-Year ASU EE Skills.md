# Research: Top Skills to Build as a First-Year ASU EE Student

## Question
What are the highest-leverage skills a 19-year-old should build during their first year in ASU's Electrical Engineering BSE program?

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Know Your Actual Curriculum — Build Ahead of It
ASU's EE BSE Year 1 is almost entirely math and physics — no EE-specific courses until Year 2 (PHY 131 + EEE 120 in Term 3A). Year 1 courses: MAT 265, MAT 266, PHY 121/122, CHM 114/116, MAT 267, FSE 100. The leverage move is to **master the math deeply** (not just pass it) and **get ahead on EE tools** before the core courses hit.

### 2. Math Mastery Is the Hidden Gatekeeper
Calculus, differential equations, and linear algebra are not just prereqs — they are the language of EE. Students who "get through" MAT 267 and MAT 275 without true fluency hit a wall in EEE 202 (Circuits I) and EEE 203 (Signals & Systems). The specific math that matters: Fourier series, Laplace transforms, complex phasors (all from calc + diff eq). **Treat calc as a professional tool, not a grade hurdle.**

### 3. Learn LTspice in Year 1 (Before It's Required)
LTspice is the dominant free SPICE simulator used in EEE 202 and real industry work. A freshman who spends 10 hours learning LTspice before Term 4 (Circuits I) arrives with a massive advantage. LTspice is free, runs on Windows/Mac, and has a shallow learning curve. Build and simulate basic RC, RL, RLC circuits. Use it as a "preview" for what Circuits I will teach formally.

### 4. Python + MATLAB Combo Is the EE Productivity Stack
ASU's CSE 100/110 will teach C/C++ (Term 3B). But **Python is the practical scripting language** for data analysis, automation, and signal processing that employers actually use. MATLAB is required in upper-division courses (EEE 202 uses it) and is the signal processing/controls standard. Learn both: Python in Year 1 (self-study), MATLAB before Term 4. Minimum viable skill: write a script, read/plot data, run loops.

### 5. Start Breadboarding and Using a Multimeter Now
The three lab instruments every EE needs to be fluent with before junior year: **multimeter, breadboard, oscilloscope**. A freshman can start with a $30 breadboard kit and a $20 multimeter from Amazon. Build basic circuits from YouTube/Instructables. The oscilloscope comes later (usually PHY 132 lab), but breadboard fluency is immediately accessible. This directly separates candidates in early internship interviews.

### 6. Git/GitHub Is Non-Negotiable by Sophomore Year
Version control is expected at every EE internship by 2025–2026. Most EE freshmen don't learn it in class until junior year — which means those who self-teach in Year 1 arrive to interviews with a visible, public portfolio. Minimum: a GitHub account with a pinned project (even a simple Arduino/Python project), ability to push, pull, branch, and write a README.

### 7. Arduino Is the Best Freshman Hardware Playground
Arduino bridges the gap between pure circuit theory and real embedded systems. It teaches: GPIO, PWM, serial communication, sensor reading, and basic C code — all before formal coursework covers these. A freshman who builds 3–5 documented Arduino projects (posted on GitHub + documented with photos) has a portfolio item that beats most sophomores.

### 8. Soft Skills: Communication + Team Execution Are Ranked #1 by Employers
Per Siemens academic survey of hiring managers: communication and collaboration are the **two most important skills** for early-career EE hires — ahead of specific technical tools. FSE 100 (Intro to Engineering) at ASU is specifically designed to build team project skills. Take it seriously. Learn to write a clear technical email, present a lab result, and document a project in plain English.

## 18-Month Freshman Skill Plan

### Phase 1 — Year 1 (Now → Term 2B): Foundations
- Dominate MAT 265/266/267 — treat as professional tools
- Install and play with LTspice (10h self-study)
- Learn Python basics: variables, loops, functions, matplotlib
- Buy a breadboard kit; build 5 basic circuits (LED, resistor divider, RC filter)
- Open a GitHub account; push your first repo

### Phase 2 — Year 2 (Term 3A–3B): First EE Courses
- EEE 120: digital logic — build Boolean circuits on breadboard alongside coursework
- PHY 131/132: use the E&M lab as oscilloscope onboarding
- CSE 100/110: C/C++ — connect it to Arduino programming
- Build 2–3 Arduino projects; document on GitHub

### Phase 3 — Circuits I Onboarding (Term 4A)
- Pre-learn LTspice SPICE simulation (RC circuits, voltage dividers, op-amp basics)
- Learn MATLAB basics: plot, fft, bode — these appear in EEE 202
- This is the semester that separates students who thrived from those who just survived

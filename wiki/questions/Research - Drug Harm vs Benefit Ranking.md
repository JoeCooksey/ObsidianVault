# Research — Drug Harm vs Benefit Ranking

**Question:** Can drugs be ranked by how harmful vs. helpful they are, and what does that ranking look like?

**Short answer:** Yes. The most rigorous framework is the Nutt/ISCD 2010 multicriteria analysis (*The Lancet*), which scores 20 drugs across 16 harm dimensions. Overlaid with current therapeutic evidence, a clear harm-to-benefit tier list emerges — and it largely inverts common legal assumptions. Psilocybin (harm score 5) and MDMA (harm score 7) are among the most promising therapeutic substances; alcohol (harm score 72) is the most harmful drug to society by a wide margin while having no meaningful medical benefit.

---

## 8 Key Findings

**1. Alcohol is the most harmful drug overall — not heroin, crack, or meth.**
The ISCD scored 20 drugs on 16 weighted criteria (9 individual + 7 societal harms). Alcohol scored 72/100, nearly 17 points above heroin (55) and crack cocaine (54). Alcohol's outsized score comes from its harm to *others* — accidents, violence, family destruction — where it scored 46/100 vs. heroin's 21. Ecstasy is one-eighth as harmful as alcohol; psilocybin is fourteen times less harmful. Legal status systematically inverts actual harm.

**2. Tobacco kills more people than all illegal drugs combined but scores only 26.**
The Nutt harm scoring framework captures multiple dimensions, not just mortality. Tobacco scores lower than alcohol because it primarily destroys only the user (relatively low "harm to others" sub-score), despite causing ~8 million deaths/year globally — the single largest preventable cause of death. Tobacco has zero therapeutic benefit, making it an F-tier harm-to-benefit ratio by any measure.

**3. The lowest-harm substances — psilocybin, MDMA, LSD — have the strongest therapeutic pipelines.**
Nutt's three lowest-harm drugs (psilocybin: 5, MDMA: 7, LSD: 7) are all Schedule I in the US (no recognized medical use), yet: psilocybin shows >50% depression remission at 6 months with FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation; MDMA-assisted therapy eliminated PTSD criteria in >70% of participants (Phase 3); LSD shows dramatic results for cluster headaches. The scheduling of these substances reflects their 1970s-era political classification, not pharmacological evidence.

**4. The harm-to-self vs. harm-to-others axis fundamentally changes the ranking.**
When examining harm to *individuals only*: crack cocaine (37), heroin (34), and methamphetamine (32) are most harmful. When examining harm to *others only*: alcohol (46) dominates massively, followed distantly by heroin (21) and crack (17). A drug policy that prioritizes individual liberty gives a different answer than one prioritizing community safety — but under either framing, alcohol's regulation is inconsistent with its profile.

**5. Therapeutic benefit is highly context-dependent; the same molecule can be medicine or poison.**
Pharmaceutical diamorphine (heroin) is used in UK palliative care and heroin-assisted treatment (HAT) for intractable OUD — HAT shows genuine efficacy in patients who failed buprenorphine and methadone. Cocaine is still used as a surgical local anesthetic/vasoconstrictor in ENT. Methamphetamine (Desoxyn) is FDA-approved for ADHD and obesity, though almost never prescribed. This illustrates that harm-to-benefit ratio is inseparable from context: dose, purity, setting, and clinical oversight are the key variables.

**6. Buprenorphine has arguably the best harm-to-benefit ratio of any widely-used drug.**
With a harm score of only 6, buprenorphine is the gold standard for opioid use disorder. It reduces mortality by 50%+, dramatically lowers HIV/hepatitis C transmission, reduces criminal activity, and has a built-in ceiling effect that prevents respiratory-depression overdose. It is a case study in how a partially addictive opioid derivative, properly used, has an exceptional therapeutic index.

**7. Cannabis's medical evidence is much weaker than public perception — with notable exceptions.**
A 2025 systematic review of 2,500+ cannabis studies found strong evidence only for: epilepsy (CBD/Epidiolex), chemotherapy-induced nausea, and moderate evidence for neuropathic pain and MS spasticity. Evidence for the most common medical-use claims — chronic pain, anxiety, insomnia — is limited to weak or conflicting. Meanwhile, heavy daily use is associated with ~30% cannabis use disorder rate, 2× coronary heart disease risk, and doubled stroke risk vs. non-daily users.

**8. Addiction liability and acute toxicity are separate axes; both matter.**
Tobacco has relatively low acute toxicity but extreme addictive liability — and kills cumulatively. Cannabis has low acute toxicity but ~30% use disorder risk with heavy use. Heroin has extreme acute toxicity (overdose) and extreme addictive liability. Psilocybin has minimal addictive liability and minimal acute toxicity (no known lethal dose). MDMA has low acute toxicity in controlled settings but risk spikes significantly with mixing (alcohol, serotonin syndrome) and hyperthermia. Harm frameworks that only count overdose deaths miss the full picture.

## Open Questions

1. **Will psilocybin or MDMA receive FDA approval before 2030?** Psilocybin has two formulations with Breakthrough Therapy designation; the evidence gap is more political/regulatory than scientific.
2. **How should harm-to-benefit frameworks account for harm reduction drugs?** Methadone and buprenorphine score moderate harm but save lives at scale — should MAT drugs be scored differently from recreational drugs?
3. **Does any safe level of alcohol exist?** Post-2023 WHO guidance says no safe level for cancer risk; some cardiovascular benefit at very light drinking is contested. The Mendelian randomization studies suggest the CV benefit was confounded by sick-quitter effects.
4. **Will heroin-assisted treatment (HAT) scale beyond Switzerland and the UK?** Evidence base is strong; political feasibility is the limiting factor.
5. **How does THC potency shift the risk profile?** Modern high-THC cannabis (25–35% THC) is qualitatively different from 1970s cannabis (~4% THC). Psychosis risk data from the 1990s–2000s underestimates current risk.

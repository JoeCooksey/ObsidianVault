# Research — Testosterone Habits Tier List

**Query:** What are all the habits that increase testosterone, on a tier list?

---

## 8 Key Findings

1. **Sleep is the single biggest testosterone lever.** One night of complete sleep deprivation drops testosterone by 24% and raises cortisol by 21% (PMC study, multiple replications). Over 90% of daily testosterone production occurs during sleep, particularly during REM. Chronic <6 hr sleep is the most common unaddressed T suppressor in young men.

2. **Body fat is the most reversible structural T suppressor.** Adipose tissue contains aromatase — the enzyme that converts testosterone to estradiol. This creates the hypogonadal-obesity cycle: fat → aromatase → lower T → more fat deposition → more fat. Body fat % is independently negatively correlated with testosterone (PLOS ONE, 2024). Even 10–15 lb fat loss measurably raises T in overweight men.

3. **Resistance training is the best exercise modality for testosterone.** Compound multi-joint movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press) produce the largest acute T spike and best long-term hormonal environment. A meta-analysis confirmed hypertrophy training is a positive predictor of testosterone. Aerobic exercise helps primarily via fat loss and cortisol reduction rather than directly.

4. **Cortisol is the upstream testosterone suppressor.** Cortisol and testosterone compete for pregnenolone, their shared biochemical precursor. Chronic psychological stress → chronically elevated cortisol → chronically suppressed T. This means stress management is an A-tier T strategy, not a soft skill. Managing doomscrolling, overcommitment, and under-recovery is directly hormonal.

5. **Vitamin D and zinc are the only micronutrients with strong T-specific RCT evidence.** Meta-analysis of 17 RCTs: vitamin D significantly raised total T (WMD +0.38 ng/mL). Zinc restores T in deficient men in well-controlled RCTs (Leydig cell function). Both are primarily about correcting deficiency — excess supplementation above sufficiency does little. Magnesium increases free T by reducing SHBG binding, particularly relevant for sweating athletes.

6. **Intermittent fasting lowers testosterone in lean men.** 8-hour TRE + resistance training in lean young males showed reductions in free and total T after 8 weeks. 3-day fasting reduced T ~35%. The T boost seen in some IF studies is entirely mediated by fat loss in overweight subjects. For lean, active young men, IF is a C–D tier T strategy.

7. **NoFap / masturbation abstinence does NOT raise testosterone.** Multiple studies confirm masturbation has no long-term effect on serum T. The single widely-cited "7-day spike" study was a small, unreplicated Chinese study measuring transient androgen receptor sensitivity — not resting testosterone. Similarly, porn consumption causes a short-term T spike, not a long-term decrease. Neither avoiding porn nor abstaining from masturbation will raise baseline T.

8. **Cold exposure is overrated for testosterone.** No robust evidence supports cold showers or ice baths raising resting testosterone. A 1991 study found T dropped 10% during cold exposure. Cold water immersion post-resistance exercise blunts the training-induced T spike. The main scrotal-cooling benefit is sperm quality/motility, not circulating testosterone. Cold therapy has legitimate other benefits (mood, alertness, adaptation) but T is not one of them.

## Open Questions

1. Does sauna-then-cold contrast therapy affect T differently than cold alone? (Some data on Finnish sauna + T exists but is limited.)
2. What is the dose-response curve for resistance training frequency and resting T — does 5+ days/week become counterproductive?
3. Is the free T increase from magnesium clinically meaningful in non-athletes?
4. Does competing in combat sports (BJJ, boxing) produce larger T feedback loops than non-contact competition?
5. What is the threshold body fat % below which aromatase contribution to E2 becomes negligible?

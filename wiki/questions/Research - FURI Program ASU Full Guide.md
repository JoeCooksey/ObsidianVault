# Research — Full Guide to the ASU FURI Program

**Question:** What is the complete guide to the FURI program at ASU — eligibility, how to apply, what it involves, and how to use it strategically?

## What Is FURI?

FURI (Fulton Undergraduate Research Initiative) is the Ira A. Fulton Schools of Engineering's flagship undergraduate research program. Students spend a funded semester working one-on-one with a faculty mentor on an original research project, culminating in a public poster presentation at the Fulton Forge Student Research Expo.

It is the highest-leverage formal research experience available to ASU engineering undergrads — it pays a stipend, produces a faculty relationship, and is the single biggest differentiator for MS EE applications to programs like NC State MS-WBGS and VT CPES.

---

## Eligibility

- **Who can apply**: Undergraduate students enrolled in any Ira A. Fulton Schools of Engineering major
- **When**: Starting from the **second semester** of attendance at ASU through the **last semester before graduation**
- **Academic standing**: Must be in "good academic standing" (2.0+ GPA minimum; practically, competitive applicants tend to have 3.0–3.5+)
- **No GRE or test scores required**
- **No published experience required** — FURI is designed for students who have never done research before

## Funding and Benefits

| Benefit | Amount |
|---|---|
| Stipend | **$1,500** (disbursed at end of semester, pre-tax) |
| Research supplies budget | Up to **$400** per funded semester |
| Credit hours | Research credit toward degree (varies by mentor/department) |
| Conference travel | Eligible to apply for travel funding to present research |

- In Fall 2025, **117 students** participated across all themes
- You can apply and receive funding for **multiple semesters** if accepted each cycle

## The Seven Fulton Research Themes

Your FURI proposal must align with one of these seven themes:

1. **Data** — AI/ML, data systems, computational engineering
2. **Education** — engineering education research, learning technologies
3. **Energy** — power electronics, renewables, energy conversion ← *Joe's primary theme*
4. **Health** — biomedical engineering, medical devices
5. **Security** — cybersecurity, national defense
6. **Semiconductor Manufacturing** — IC fabrication, WBG devices, process engineering ← *Joe's secondary theme*
7. **Sustainability** — environmental engineering, circular economy

Joe's research would land in **Energy** (power electronics, EV/WBG) or **Semiconductor Manufacturing** (SiC/GaN device characterization).

## Application Components

FURI requires six documents submitted together. **No generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) are permitted** anywhere in the application — this is explicitly prohibited.

### 1. Resume (1 page)
- Updated and EE-specific
- Include: coursework, projects (LTspice circuits, GitHub repos, breadboard builds), clubs (IEEE, Solar Devils), skills (Python, LTspice, MATLAB)
- Visit the Fulton Schools Career Center for resume review
- Even Year 1 lab work and personal projects count — list them

### 2. Research Proposal (2 pages max)
The core of the application. Evaluated on:
- **Research question** — is it specific and answerable?
- **Plan** — what methods/approach will you use?
- **Timeline** — realistic milestone breakdown for one semester
- **Literature review** — evidence you've read 3–5 relevant papers
- **Alignment** with one of the 7 Fulton research themes

Use the official **Proposal Packet Template** (download from the FURI website) — do not invent your own format.

Structure your proposal with:
1. Introduction and background (why this question matters)
2. Research question / hypothesis
3. Methods and approach
4. Timeline with milestones
5. Expected outcomes
6. Bibliography (not counted in the 2 pages)

### 3. Bibliography
Minimum 3–5 sources. IEEE-format citations preferred for EE topics. Reading papers before approaching a faculty mentor also shows you're serious.

### 4. Timeline
A week-by-week or month-by-month Gantt-style breakdown of the semester. Shows you understand research has phases: literature review → experimental design → data collection → analysis → writing → poster.

### 5. Personal Statement (1 page)
Why you want to do research, why this specific project, and how it connects to your career goals. Write it yourself — this is evaluated holistically.

Key things to include:
- Why research (not just "for grad school")
- What specific aspect of the project excites you
- What skills or experiences make you a good fit
- Long-term career direction (MS EE, WBG power, etc.)

### 6. Transcript
Unofficial transcript is fine for application. Shows academic standing and relevant coursework completed.

### 7. Faculty Mentor Approval
The **most important prerequisite**. You must have a faculty mentor who agrees to supervise your FURI project before you submit. Applications without mentor approval are rejected.

## How to Find and Secure a Faculty Mentor

This is the hardest and most important step. Start at least **6–8 weeks before the deadline**.

### Step 1: Identify Target Faculty

Browse faculty profiles at:
- **forge.engineering.asu.edu/mentors/** — lists faculty who have mentored FURI students before
- **faculty.engineering.asu.edu/directory/ecee/power-grid-engineering/** — power electronics faculty directory

**Power electronics and WBG priority targets for Joe:**

| Professor        | Lab   | Focus                                                | Email                |
| ---------------- | ----- | ---------------------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Mike Ranjram** | MAPEL | WBG devices, miniaturized converters, gate drivers   | mike.ranjram@asu.edu |
| **Raja Ayyanar** | ECEE  | EV/PV/WBG/motor drives (senior faculty, broad scope) | rayyanar@asu.edu     |
| Yuji Zhao        | EECS  | GaN and Ga₂O₃ ultra-wide bandgap devices             | via faculty dir      |

Prof. Ranjram (MAPEL) is the **highest-priority cold approach** for Joe's WBG power track — he has supervised FURI students, his lab is accessible to undergrads, and his work is directly aligned.

### Step 2: Read Their Work First
- Read **1–2 recent papers** from the faculty member's group (Google Scholar or ASU library)
- Identify one result or method that genuinely interests you
- Look up the lab's GitHub, research center page, or recent student posters at Fulton Forge

### Step 3: Office Hours Approach (Preferred)
If they teach a course you're enrolled in or auditing:
1. Attend office hours
2. Ask a question about course material first (builds rapport)
3. After a few visits, mention your interest in their research
4. Say: "I'm interested in applying for FURI and I've read [your paper on X]. Would you be open to discussing whether there's a project that could fit your lab's current direction?"

This is more effective than cold email for most faculty.

### Step 4: Cold Email (If No Course Contact)
If you cannot attend their office hours, cold email works when done correctly:

**Cold Email Formula (7–12 sentences total):**
1. Who you are (name, year, major, GPA if strong)
2. What specifically you read in their work (paper title + one thing that interested you)
3. How it connects to your interests or goals
4. What you can currently offer (relevant coursework, tools you know: LTSpice, Python, etc.)
5. Clear ask: "I would like to apply for FURI under your mentorship — would you be available for a 15-minute meeting to discuss whether there's a fit?"

Send **one email at a time**. Wait **7 business days** before following up. Do not mass-email multiple professors simultaneously with the same generic message — it will be obvious.

Attach: resume + unofficial transcript.

### Step 5: The Meeting
If they agree to meet:
- Come with 1–2 specific project ideas based on their current research
- Ask what problems are unsolved in their group that an undergrad could contribute to
- Ask about the commitment (hours per week, what tools/skills needed)
- If it's a good fit, ask: "Would you be willing to mentor me through FURI?"

## What You Do During FURI

A funded FURI semester typically looks like:

| Phase | Weeks | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Literature + Setup | 1–4 | Deep dive into existing research; set up equipment/simulation environment |
| Experimental Design | 3–6 | Define methodology, build simulation model or experimental rig |
| Data Collection | 5–12 | Run experiments, simulations, iterations |
| Analysis + Writing | 10–14 | Analyze results, write research summary and abstract |
| Poster Creation | 13–15 | Design and print Fulton Forge poster |
| Expo Presentation | Week 16 | Present poster publicly at Fulton Forge Student Research Expo |

**FURI also requires:**
- Attending FURI-specific workshops (writing, presenting, research ethics)
- Submitting a mid-semester and final written progress summary
- Writing a short abstract of your findings

## End-of-Semester: Fulton Forge Student Research Expo

Formerly called the FURI Symposium. Held at the end of each fall and spring semester.

**Poster Specs:**
- Size: **23" × 35"** (horizontal or vertical — your choice)
- No bleed (no color going over the edge)
- Required sections: Title + Authors | Introduction/Background | Methods | Results/Data | Conclusions | References

**Design rules:**
- Less text, more visuals (charts, graphs, diagrams)
- Use tables for data — never present results as a paragraph of text
- Conclusions: 2–4 sentences max — what you found and what future work is needed
- Always include at least 2 background references
- Print through ASU's Fulton Forge Showcase program at print.asu.edu

## Why FURI Matters for Grad School

From the MS EE program research:
- **VT CPES and NC State MS-WBGS** both weight research experience heavily in admissions
- A FURI project = a faculty letter of recommendation from the specific research area you're applying in
- A FURI abstract or conference poster = a citable research output you can reference in your SOP
- For Stanford (~15% acceptance rate), FURI-level research is essentially a prerequisite
- NC State MS-WBGS (new Fall 2026 program) is the direct pipeline for Joe's WBG track — FURI in MAPEL lab would produce exactly the right background

**The chain**: FURI (Year 2–3) → named faculty recommendation → strong SOP → MS EE admission → LLNL/Sandia/WBG industry career

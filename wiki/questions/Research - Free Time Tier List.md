# Research: Top Things to Do With Your Free Time (Tier List)

## Question
What are the highest-ROI activities for a 19-year-old male EE student's free time — ranked by long-term compounding value?

## Context
Joe is a 19-year-old EE student at ASU (Year 1, Livermore CA), training 6 days/week (cycling), with an ambitious 5-year plan: LLNL/Sandia internship, NC State or VT CPES grad school, WBG power electronics career. This tier list is built from psychology research on leisure, productivity science, and direct mapping to Joe's goals.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Relationships predict happiness and longevity more than money or career success
The 80-year Harvard Study of Adult Development (world's longest happiness study) found that the quality of relationships — not wealth, fame, or achievement — is the strongest predictor of late-life happiness and physical health. High-quality social time in your 20s directly compounds into life satisfaction and career support (warm introductions, mentors) at 30–40.

**Implication for Joe**: In-person time with close friends, mentors, and lab peers is S-tier even when it "feels unproductive."

### 2. Deep reading builds cognitive advantages that passive media cannot
Books train sustained attention, vocabulary, abstract reasoning, and mental model depth in ways that short-form content (TikTok, YouTube shorts, Reddit) structurally cannot. A 2024 Wiley study found physical paper reading is correlated with deeper processing and meaningful experiences compared to digital scanning. Reading 15 pages/day = 15+ books/year = top 1% of adults.

**Implication**: Joe's 9-book Phase 1–3 reading list is not "optional enrichment" — it's a direct cognitive performance investment.

### 3. Skill-building during free time compounds exponentially faster in your early 20s
Human capital theory (Becker) and neuroscience both confirm that skill acquisition during early adulthood (when neuroplasticity peaks and time is relatively abundant) yields dramatically higher long-term returns than skill acquisition at 30–40. For Joe specifically, Year 1 at ASU has zero EE courses — the free-time window to build LTspice, Python, Git, and breadboard skills before Circuits I (Term 4A) is uniquely leveraged.

**Implication**: Self-directed EE skill-building in Year 1 free time outperforms almost any other use of that time on career ROI alone.

### 4. Flow-state activities maximize happiness per hour
Csikszentmihalyi's 30+ year research program: flow (full absorption in a challenging task at the edge of your skill level) is the highest-density happiness experience available. Programming projects, electronics builds, competitive cycling, and deep reading are all flow-capable. Passive consumption (scrolling, TV) is anti-flow.

**Implication**: Any activity that produces flow is likely better for long-term wellbeing than leisure that feels easier.

### 5. Exercise is already S-tier — but active leisure types matter
Joe already trains 6 days/week. Research confirms: 150+ min/week moderate exercise = −50% all-cause mortality risk vs. sedentary. But the *type* of active leisure matters post-workout: team sports and outdoor activity maintain happiness even at 5+ hours/day of leisure; solo screen time for 5+ hours reduces happiness (Rutgers 2023).

**Implication**: When not cycling, lean into social/physical free time (pickup basketball, hiking, etc.) rather than solo screen time.

### 6. Passive media and doom-scrolling are the dominant time sinks for young adults
US adults now average <16 min/day of leisure reading (down from 23 min in 2004) while social media/video consumption is ~3+ hours/day. Heavy short-form video use (TikTok) is linked to reduced P300 brainwave response — measurable degradation in sustained attention. This is the single biggest opportunity cost in a young person's free time.

**Implication**: Every hour shifted from scrolling to reading/building is worth 2–5× on ROI.

### 7. Creative and generative activities build identity capital
Identity capital (Arnett) — the collection of things you've built, tried, and committed to in young adulthood — predicts career resilience, social confidence, and self-concept in your 30s. Building projects (PCB designs, GitHub repos, cycling PRs, creative work) creates tangible proof of identity that scrolling never can.

**Implication**: Making things (GitHub repos, electronics projects, creative work) compounds in ways that consuming things cannot.

### 8. Optimal free time is 2–5 hours per day — and it requires intent
Psychology Today / Cassie Holmes research: 2–5 hours/day of free time maximizes happiness. Less than 2 hours = stress. More than 5 hours = boredom/meaninglessness (unless time is actively structured). The quality of leisure matters more than quantity. Unstructured time without intent defaults to the easiest (lowest-ROI) activity.

**Implication**: Having a default answer to "what do I do now?" prevents the drift into doom-scrolling.

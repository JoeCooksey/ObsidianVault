# Research: GLP Medications Complete Guide

## Overview

GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) are the most significant class of weight-loss medications ever developed, with clinical outcomes far exceeding prior pharmacological options. They work primarily by mimicking natural gut hormones to suppress appetite, slow gastric emptying, and improve insulin sensitivity. The three drugs covered here represent a generational progression: semaglutide (GLP-1 only), tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP), and retatrutide (GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon) — each generation adding receptor targets and achieving greater weight loss.

## Key Findings

1. **Each generation adds receptor targets and achieves meaningfully greater weight loss.** Semaglutide (GLP-1 only) achieves ~13.6–20% body weight loss. Tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP) beats semaglutide by ~47% in SURMOUNT-5 head-to-head data, achieving 20.2% vs. 13.7% at 72 weeks. Retatrutide (GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon) has achieved up to 28.7% weight loss at 68 weeks in Phase 3 trials — the highest figure ever recorded for a pharmacological weight-loss agent.

2. **All three share a "slow titration" protocol structure.** Starting doses are sub-therapeutic — designed to allow GI receptor downregulation to prevent nausea/vomiting. Increasing dose too fast is the #1 cause of dropout. Every 4 weeks is the minimum titration interval across all three drugs. Patience here is not optional; it's pharmacologically necessary.

3. **Tirzepatide's GIP component is the key differentiator from semaglutide.** GIP agonism improves insulin sensitivity and shifts fat distribution away from visceral organs. Some researchers initially questioned whether GIP agonism would even be beneficial; tirzepatide resolved this — the dual mechanism is clearly superior to GLP-1 alone at comparable doses.

4. **Retatrutide's glucagon component adds metabolic rate increase and liver fat mobilization.** The glucagon receptor drives lipolysis and raises resting energy expenditure — effects that semaglutide and tirzepatide lack. This is why retatrutide's weight loss numbers exceed the dual agonist; it's burning stored fat more aggressively. Side note: the dysesthesia (abnormal skin sensations) signal at 8.8–20.9% of high-dose patients is a genuine safety concern under Phase 3 review.

5. **Stopping GLP-1 drugs = weight regain by default.** STEP-10 and SURMOUNT-4 trials confirm >40–50% of lost weight returns within 28–52 weeks of stopping. The clinical consensus: these are chronic disease medications, not finite courses. The "exit strategy" question should reframe to "planned reduction" or "maintenance dosing," not full discontinuation for most patients.

6. **Gradual tapering + lifestyle architecture is the best exit approach.** A 9-week slow taper of semaglutide to zero with diet and exercise coaching in a 2024 study maintained weight stability (even -1.5% additional loss) at 26-week follow-up. Spacing shots to every 10–14 days before full cessation gives the brain time to restore natural appetite regulation. A "5-pound trigger rule" provides an objective re-intervention signal post-exit.

7. **Semaglutide now has an oral form.** The FDA approved oral semaglutide (25 mg daily tablet) for weight management in December 2025 — the first GLP-1 pill. Weight loss outcomes (~13.6% at 64 weeks in OASIS 4) are slightly below subcutaneous Wegovy, likely due to oral bioavailability, but the convenience factor is significant.

8. **Retatrutide is not yet FDA-approved.** Seven TRIUMPH Phase 3 trials are expected to complete in 2026, with an NDA filing likely Q4 2026 or early 2027 and approval ~mid-to-late 2027. It is currently not legally available as a prescription drug in the US.

## Open Questions

1. What maintenance dose strategy preserves the most weight loss while minimizing long-term side effects? (ongoing SURMOUNT-J and similar extension trials)
2. Will retatrutide show the dysesthesia signal resolves with dose reduction, or is it dose-limiting?
3. What happens to cardiovascular outcomes with retatrutide? (SELECT equivalents not yet published)
4. Can exercise-induced endogenous GLP-1 elevation meaningfully substitute for exogenous medication in the exit window?
5. Are there subpopulations (e.g., MC4R-deficient obesity) where one generation clearly outperforms another?

# Research — Top 90-Day Project Ideas for Joe

**Research question**: What are the highest-ROI projects Joe can execute over 90 days, given his profile as a first-year ASU EE student targeting WBG power electronics research (FURI), with interests in self-improvement, Catholic faith, and self-teaching?

**Synthesis page**: [[90 Day Project Tier List]]

---

## Finding 1: The Execution Gap Is Joe's Real Constraint

Joe's vault contains extensive research and detailed roadmaps covering: Python EE project ladders, FURI roadmaps, EE freshman action plans, self-teaching tier lists, hormone optimization, comfort zone habits, and Catholic spiritual growth. The research quality is high. The constraint is now *execution*, not information.

The 90-day project frame is valuable precisely because it forces a concrete deliverable rather than another research cycle. The best 90-day projects are those where more research would be procrastination and building would be progress.

**Implication**: Projects that require another phase of planning before starting are C/D-tier regardless of their inherent value. Projects where Joe can start on Day 1 with existing knowledge are S/A-tier.

---

## Finding 2: FURI Outreach Must Begin in Summer to Hit October

ASU FURI applications close the third Wednesday of October (Fall → Spring semester). Students who succeed in FURI typically:
1. Identify a faculty mentor months before the deadline
2. Build a relationship via paper-specific cold emails, office hours, or lab meetings
3. Draft the proposal iteratively with faculty feedback

Starting FURI outreach in May gives Joe 5 months before the October deadline — the ideal runway. Students who start in September scramble; students who start in May build genuine relationships.

**Source**: ASU FURI Student Hub — faculty mentors assess "strong endorsement," "authenticity," and "research alignment." These require time to establish.

**Implication**: FURI outreach is not a one-time action — it's a 90-day campaign. It's also the highest career-leverage project Joe can run this summer.

## Finding 3: A Physical Hardware Project Is the FURI Portfolio Differentiator

EE students applying to power electronics labs face a common problem: two students with similar GPAs and coursework look identical on paper. A working hardware project (documented, tested, published to GitHub) breaks this tie decisively.

A synchronous buck converter built with STM32 PWM control is the ideal scope for 90 days because:
- Directly maps to Ranjram's MAPEL lab work (converter architecture, MHz switching)
- Demonstrates: embedded C, analog design, Laplace/transfer functions (content Joe already knows), PCB layout
- Scope is right: challenging but achievable in 12 weeks with focused effort
- Cost is low (~$50–70 total including board + components + PCB fabrication)
- Deliverable is unambiguous: either it works and regulates voltage or it doesn't

**Source**: EIT Best Projects for Engineering Students; STM32 embedded systems curricula; PowerAmerica WBG education programs.

## Finding 4: MIT 18.06 Is the Highest-ROI Academic Project Before Fall 2026

Joe's Fall 2026 courses include MAT 343 (Applied Linear Algebra). Completing MIT OCW 18.06 (Gilbert Strang) before September gives:
- 2-month head start on the class
- Deep conceptual foundation that coursework typically glosses over
- Covers exactly what EE needs: eigenvalues (modal analysis, control), SVD (signal processing, data), orthogonality (Fourier, least squares)

At 34 lectures × ~1 hr each = 34 hrs of video. At 40 min/day (25 min video + 15 min problems) = done in 85 days. Fits perfectly in a 90-day window.

**3Blue1Brown Essence of Linear Algebra** (14 short videos, ~3 hrs total) should be watched first for visual intuition, then MIT 18.06 for rigor.

**Source**: Joe's own [[MAT 343 Applied Linear Algebra — Topics and Prep]] + MIT OCW.

## Finding 5: The Roth IRA Action Has the Highest ROI Per Hour of Any Project

This is not a 90-day project — it's a 2-hour action that must happen during the 90-day window. Joe's own research found: "Roth IRA at 19 = ~$500k+ vs 35 at retirement" from [[Self-Teaching Topics Tier List]].

Mechanism: $100/month from age 19, invested in a low-cost total-market index fund averaging 8%/year nominal return = ~$525,000 at age 65. Every month of delay reduces this by ~$5,000–$10,000 due to the exponential compounding tail.

**Action**: Open Fidelity or Vanguard Roth IRA, fund with $100, set up $50–100/month auto-invest, buy a target-date index fund (FXIFX or VTTSX). Set it and forget it.

**ROI calculation**: 2 hours of action time → potentially $250,000–$500,000 in wealth difference vs. waiting until age 35. No other project in this list comes close in terms of ROI per hour.

## Finding 6: Progressive Overload Training Is the Meta-Habit That Unlocks Everything Else

From [[Human Hormones Complete Guide]]: resistance training 3–4×/week is the single habit that simultaneously improves testosterone, HGH (quality of overnight pulse), insulin sensitivity, cortisol management, dopamine baseline, and sleep quality.

From [[Comfort Zone Habits Tier List]]: physical training is the closest thing to a "keystone habit" — the neural discomfort tolerance built in the gym transfers to academic and professional domains.

A beginner strength program (Starting Strength or StrongLifts 5×5) takes exactly 12 weeks (one training cycle). At day 90, typical beginner gains are 30–50% improvement in squat/deadlift max. This is a concrete, measurable deliverable.

**The hidden benefit**: male students who exercise regularly 3×/week report 30–50% higher academic productivity and significantly better sleep quality (Harvard Health Publishing, 2024). For a student carrying a heavy self-study load, this ROI is multiplicative.

## Finding 8: 90-Day Project Failure Modes — What to Avoid

From research on 90-day challenges and student project failure patterns:

1. **Too many parallel projects**: Cognitive load is the primary killer. More than 2 "active build" projects simultaneously results in none completing. Joe's recommended stack: MIT 18.06 + Buck Converter as the two primary builds.
2. **More research instead of starting**: The "I'll start after I finish reading X" pattern. Joe's vault is comprehensive — more research before starting is comfort-seeking, not preparation.
3. **No external accountability**: Self-reported 90-day projects fail at higher rates without external check-ins. Options: FURI mentor relationship, GitHub public commits, IEEE club progress updates.
4. **Vague deliverables**: "Learn STM32" is not a deliverable. "Build a working buck converter that regulates 12V → 5V at ±2% accuracy" is a deliverable.
5. **Scope creep**: The buck converter doesn't need to be 97% efficient or ultra-miniaturized. Ship a working prototype first.

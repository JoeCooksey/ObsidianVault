# Tail Events

> "A few things account for most of the outcomes." — Morgan Housel

## Core Idea

A **tail event** is an outcome in the far end of the probability distribution — rare, but when it occurs, it dominates all the normal-range outcomes combined. The term comes from the shape of a probability distribution: most events cluster in the middle ("body"), while rare events occupy the long "tails."

In finance, investing, and career outcomes, the extreme positive tail events are disproportionately responsible for total value created. This is sometimes called the **power law distribution** or the **Pareto principle** taken to its logical extreme.

## The Amazon Example

Housel uses Amazon as his central illustration:
- Amazon has launched hundreds of products and initiatives over its history
- Most of them have failed, been discontinued, or underperformed
- A tiny handful — AWS (cloud), Prime membership, third-party marketplace — have been world-historical successes
- The gains from those few tail events dwarf all the losses from every failure

Jeff Bezos has effectively said: "If we have good odds on something, we'll try it. We can afford to fail many times because the wins are so asymmetric."

This is not recklessness — it is structural understanding of how value is created in power-law environments.

## The 1% → 90% Rule

Housel's version of the insight: roughly **1% of investments or events drive 90% of the outcomes**. This applies across domains:
- Venture capital: most investments go to zero; one unicorn returns the fund
- Stock market: ~4% of all stocks account for all net wealth creation since 1926 (Bessembinder, 2018)
- Publishing: a handful of bestsellers fund hundreds of midlist books
- Career: one breakthrough project or relationship can redirect an entire trajectory

## Implications for Investing

1. **You don't need to win every trade.** The investor who holds a diversified portfolio and catches a few tail events will outperform the investor who "plays it safe" by avoiding the volatility that also generates the tails.
2. **Don't panic-sell.** If you exit a position during a downturn, you may be exiting just before the tail event recovery.
3. **Time in market > timing the market.** The more time you spend invested, the more likely you are to capture tail events. Timing-based strategies miss the rare days that account for most annual returns.

## Implications for Career

The same logic applies to skill-building and career bets:
- Most side projects, skills explored, and career experiments will not pay off directly
- But the few that do — the right skill at the right time, the right project that becomes a standout — can define a career
- For Joe's EE/WBG path: the "tail event" bet is deep specialization in SiC/GaN at exactly the moment industry demand is peaking. Most efforts may be preparatory; one skill demonstration at the right moment could be the defining event.

## What This Requires Psychologically

Housel argues the key failure mode is **intolerance for the losses that make the wins possible**. Investors who cut their winners early to lock in gains and hold their losers to avoid realizing losses are structurally preventing tail event capture.

The psychological requirement:
- Accept that most bets won't pay off
- Stay long enough to catch the ones that do
- Don't confuse the many small failures with the system being broken

## Connection to Margin of Safety

[[Margin of Safety (Finance)]] is the mechanism that *allows* tail event investing: if you have a financial buffer, you can survive the many failed bets without being forced to quit before the tail arrives. Without margin of safety, tail event investing is reckless; with it, it is systematic.

# GLP-1 Medications Overview

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) receptor agonists are a class of injectable (and now oral) medications originally developed for type 2 diabetes that have become the dominant pharmacological treatment for obesity. They work by mimicking incretins — hormones released by the gut after eating that suppress appetite and regulate blood sugar.

The three most important current and near-future agents represent a progression of receptor targeting: each generation adds more receptor agonism, increasing metabolic impact.

---

## Mechanism of Action (Shared Core)

All three drugs activate the **GLP-1 receptor**, which produces:
- **Appetite suppression** — GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus reduce hunger signals and food cravings
- **Slowed gastric emptying** — food stays in the stomach longer, prolonging satiety
- **Insulin secretion** — stimulates insulin release in a glucose-dependent manner (reduces hypoglycemia risk)
- **BBB crossing** — reaches the brain's appetite centers directly

## What Each Added Receptor Does

**GIP receptor (added in tirzepatide):**
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Shifts fat storage away from visceral depots (organs) toward subcutaneous (under skin)
- Amplifies GLP-1 effects; synergistic, not merely additive

**Glucagon receptor (added in retatrutide):**
- Increases resting metabolic rate (burns more calories at rest)
- Promotes lipolysis — direct breakdown of stored fat into usable energy
- Mobilizes liver fat specifically — key for MASH/NAFLD treatment
- Responsible for retatrutide's superior weight loss vs. dual agonists

## Safety Profile (Shared)

**Most common (all three):** nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, and fatigue — predominantly GI side effects, worst during titration phase, improve with time.

**Black box warning (all three):** Thyroid C-cell tumor risk (based on rodent data); contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or MEN2 syndrome.

**Pancreatitis:** Listed as a risk on all labels. Multiple meta-analyses show no statistically significant increase in actual acute pancreatitis vs. placebo, but contraindicated in history of pancreatitis.

**Retatrutide-specific:** Dysesthesia (abnormal skin sensations) — 8.8% at 9 mg dose, 20.9% at 12 mg dose vs. 0.7% placebo. Under review in Phase 3 program.

**Contraindications (all):** Medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 history, severe kidney disease, pregnancy, active pancreatitis.

## Decision Framework

**Use semaglutide if:**
- You need an FDA-approved option right now
- You prefer the oral form (Rybelsus / oral Wegovy) for convenience
- You have insurance coverage for Wegovy/Ozempic but not tirzepatide

**Use tirzepatide if:**
- You want the most effective currently FDA-approved agent
- Your goal is maximum weight loss with proven safety profile
- You have metabolic syndrome or significant insulin resistance (GIP benefit is greatest here)

**Use retatrutide when approved (~2027) if:**
- Maximum possible pharmacological weight loss is the goal
- Prior agents have not achieved target weight loss
- You can tolerate the emerging safety profile (dysesthesia risk at high doses)

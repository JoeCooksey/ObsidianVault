# ASU Social Connection Methods Ranked

Ranked by **friendship formation probability and depth**, calibrated to ASU Tempe campus.
The science is clear: **repeated contact + shared mission** is the engine. Everything else is a supplement.

Calibrated for Joe (19yo EE/engineering student, ASU Year 1, online student but on-campus for key activities).

> Research and sources at [[Research - Building Social Connections at ASU]].

---

## The Core Mechanism

Two variables predict friendship formation more than anything else:

1. **Proximity + repeated exposure** — seat proximity alone makes you 3–5× more likely to name someone as a friend (2022, Frontiers in Psychology, n=235). Next-door dorm neighbors have 41% friendship rate vs 10% for end-of-hall.
2. **Hours of contact** — casual friendship requires ~50 hrs; good friendship ~200 hrs (Jeffrey Hall, 2019). You need a context that generates this automatically.

The question for every method: *does it reliably produce 50–200 hours of contact with the same people over a semester?*

## S-Tier — Maximum Connection Density

### Technical Project Clubs (IEEE ASU, Solar Devils)
**The single best social context for an EE student at ASU.**

- **IEEE ASU**: 600+ active members; oldest engineering org at ASU; project teams, workshops, networking events; award-winning branch
- **Solar Devils**: cross-disciplinary solar car build; EE, ME, CS students; project-based = 4–8 hrs/week same people
- **Why S-tier**: shared mission + technical skill-building + professional network + same people weekly for a full semester = the friendship formula
- **Bonus**: the people you meet here are future internship referrals, study partners for EEE 202, and LinkedIn connections. Social ROI doubles as career ROI.
- **How to maximize**: join a sub-team (electrical systems, battery, controls) not just the general meetings — sub-team = tight group of 5–10 people, not 200

### Study Groups (Lab Sections, Recitations, Project Teams)
**Proximity is the strongest predictor of college friendship, and class is where proximity is maximized.**

- Seat proximity increases friendship nominations 3–5× vs. students in distant seats
- Lab sections and recitations (small group, 10–30 people) are far better than 200-person lectures
- Group projects force repeated contact and shared stakes — accelerates friendship timeline from months to weeks
- **How to maximize**: sit in the same area every lecture (builds familiarity), introduce yourself in the first week, propose a study group for the first exam within 2 weeks of class starting
- **Best for Joe**: MAT 265 (Calc 1), PHY 121 (Physics 1) — these are shared by hundreds of EE freshmen; the person next to you is probably in your same major

### First-Year Residence Hall Community
**Proximity-based friendship is the most underrated asset of freshman year.**

- Dorm proximity: 41% friendship rate with immediate neighbor; drops below 10% beyond 5 doors
- First-year housing at ASU groups students by academic college — an EE floor puts you with future classmates
- Daily contact without effort: dining hall, laundry room, lounge, hallway = 50+ hrs by week 4 without trying
- **How to maximize**: leave door open first 2 weeks (signals openness), say yes to floor events (even lame ones), invite 2–3 floor neighbors to dining hall in week 1
- **Note**: Joe is in Livermore (online/hybrid) — if not living in dorms, this tier is inaccessible. If ever on-campus housing is possible, it's S-tier.

## A-Tier — High Value, Requires Initiation

### Club Fitness / Recreational Sports (ASU Running Club, Cycling, Intramurals)
Shared physical effort is one of the strongest bonding contexts in human psychology. Endorphins + vulnerability + shared suffering = fast-tracking connection.

- ASU Intramural Sports: co-ed teams, weekly games, same group over 6–8 week season
- ASU Running Club (Downtown Phoenix campus; Tempe has general rec sports)
- Sun Devil Fitness Complex group fitness classes: consistent attendees form natural sub-groups
- **Joe's advantage**: already trains 6 days/week cycling — joining ASU cycling or triathlon club aligns with existing habit; social ROI is additive to existing training

### Campus Jobs (On-Campus Employment)
Dramatically underrated for friendship formation. 10–20 hrs/week with the same 5–10 coworkers produces 50+ hrs/month automatically.

- Campus dining, library, IT help desk, tutoring center, research labs
- Higher-value for Joe: **undergraduate research assistant** (connect with faculty + grad students) or **Makerspace staff** (connect with engineers and makers)
- ASU Handshake and campus job boards list all positions

### Welcome Week / Orientation Events (First 2 Weeks)
The social scene is maximally open in week 1–2 — everyone is trying to meet people, no one has formed groups yet. This window closes.

- Welcome Week (Move-In Week, Sun Devil Welcome): mixers, campus tours, floor events
- First Year Experience (FYE) programming: workshops, social events
- **Rule**: say yes to everything in weeks 1–2, even if awkward. Filter later, not now.
- **Limitation**: one-off contacts are weak ties without follow-up; need conversion to a regular context (club, study group) within 2–3 weeks

## B-Tier — Good Supplement, Lower Contact Density

### Large Club General Meetings (Without Sub-Team Commitment)
Going to a club's first meeting is A-tier. Attending only general meetings without joining a project team or committee is B-tier.

- General body meetings: 50–200 people, surface-level contact, low name-remembering
- The upgrade: immediately sign up for a sub-team, project group, or leadership role — this moves you from B to S-tier within the same club
- **Rule for Joe**: don't just attend IEEE general meetings; get on a project team in semester 1

### Greek Life (Fraternities)
5,500 ASU students in Greek life. High social density and weekly contact — but time-intensive and expensive.

- Weekly chapter meetings, philanthropy events, social events
- Best social ROI for people without strong career-adjacent clubs
- **Why not S-tier for Joe**: high time cost (10–15 hrs/week), financial cost ($1,500–3,000/year), and culture may not align with serious EE technical identity; the clubs (IEEE, Solar Devils) give you better career AND social ROI for less cost
- Worth considering if the specific EE clubs don't feel like a fit

### Campus Events (Games, Concerts, Career Fairs)
Fun, good for exposure to the campus culture, occasional new contacts — but low repeat-contact density.

- ASU Devil Athletics (Sun Devil Stadium, desert financial arena) — football/basketball; good for shared experience
- Career fairs: meet recruiters AND see who from your major shows up; follow up with classmates you met
- **Limitation**: one-off events produce weak ties; you need a follow-up conversion to a regular context

## C-Tier — Supplementary, Situation-Dependent

### Large Lecture Classes (Without Study Group Formation)
The proximity effect is real, but 200-person lectures diffuse it. You see people but rarely convert to friendship without deliberate follow-up.

- Lab sections and recitations are C-tier on their own (see S-tier study groups — the upgrade is deliberate group formation)
- Sit in the same seat every class; eventually people recognize you → say hi → propose study session

### Apps and Digital-First Social (Bumble BFF, Discord ASU Servers)
Can work — but at 19 in college, organic methods dominate. Apps are supplements for introverts or remote students.

- ASU Discord servers (unofficial, organized by major/year) — good for online students like Joe
- Bumble BFF: low stigma, better than cold DMs
- **Joe's case**: being partially online makes digital community more relevant; ASU EE Discord can fill the gap where in-person contact is limited

---

## D-Tier — Low ROI for Deep Connection

### Parties and Bar Scene
You meet people but you don't connect. Noise + alcohol + transient crowd = weak ties at best, D-tier for deep friendship.

- Good for expanding surface-level social exposure; zero good for close friendship
- If you meet someone interesting at a party: the friendship only forms if you follow up to a real context within 48 hours
- Regular heavy party attendance: time cost, sleep disruption, money = compounding harm (see [[Free Time Tier List]])

### Social Media-First Outreach (DMing Classmates)
Awkward initiator in college unless you already have a real-world connection. Feels low-effort and gets low-effort responses.

- Exception: following up after meeting in person (DM within 24 hrs = high conversion)
- Exception: Discord/GroupMe for a specific class study group — functional and normal

## The Time Math

Jeffrey Hall (2019) research gives us the numbers:
- **Acquaintance → Casual friend**: ~50 hours
- **Casual friend → Friend**: ~100–150 hours  
- **Friend → Close friend**: 200+ hours

A study group that meets 3 hrs/week for 12 weeks = **36 hrs** — enough for casual friendship.
A project team (Solar Devils/IEEE) that meets 6 hrs/week for 16 weeks = **96 hrs** — solid friendship.
Dorm proximity: 30 min/day of casual contact = **90 hrs by end of semester** — deep friendship possible.

Parties: 4 hrs every 2 weeks, different people = **fewer than 20 total hours with any one person** — rarely crosses friendship threshold.

## Joe's Recommended Action Stack

**Semester 1 priority list:**
1. Join IEEE ASU → immediately sign up for a project team (not just general meetings)
2. Form a study group for MAT 265 or PHY 121 in week 1 — be the one who initiates
3. Say yes to all Welcome Week events in first 2 weeks
4. If doing any on-campus time: use the Makerspace and talk to people there
5. Join ASU EE Discord (digital community for online student gaps)

**The rule**: two high-density contexts is better than ten surface-level ones. Pick 2 clubs max, go deep in both.

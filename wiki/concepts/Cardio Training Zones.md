# Cardio Training Zones

Cardio training zones are intensity bands defined as a percentage of maximum heart rate (max HR). Each zone produces distinct physiological adaptations and serves different training goals. The standard 5-zone model is the most widely used by coaches, sports scientists, and consumer platforms (Garmin, Polar, Whoop).

## Max Heart Rate for a 19-Year-Old

The most common formula: **220 − age = max HR**

For age 19: **220 − 19 = 201 bpm**

> Note: This formula has ±10–12 bpm standard deviation. A more accurate formula (Tanaka et al., 2001): **208 − (0.7 × age)** → 208 − 13.3 = **194.7 bpm** (~195 bpm). For healthy athletic 19-year-olds, measured max HR via field testing is the gold standard.

## The 5-Zone Model — Complete Reference

| Zone | Name | % Max HR | BPM (Age 19, Max HR 201) | Primary Benefit |
|------|------|----------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Zone 1 | Recovery / Easy | 50–60% | 101–121 bpm | Active recovery, fat metabolism foundation |
| Zone 2 | Aerobic Base | 60–70% | 121–141 bpm | Mitochondrial biogenesis, fat oxidation, aerobic base |
| Zone 3 | Aerobic Threshold / Tempo | 70–80% | 141–161 bpm | Lactate buffering, cardiovascular efficiency |
| Zone 4 | Lactate Threshold | 80–90% | 161–181 bpm | Lactate threshold elevation, race pace conditioning |
| Zone 5 | VO2 Max / Anaerobic | 90–100% | 181–201 bpm | VO2 max ceiling, neuromuscular power, peak output |

## Zone-by-Zone Breakdown

### Zone 1 — Recovery (50–60% | 101–121 bpm at 19)

**What it feels like:** Very easy. Fully conversational. Could hold a long speech.

**Primary benefits:**
- Active recovery — flushes metabolic waste, reduces muscle soreness
- Trains the aerobic system without fatigue accumulation
- Light fat oxidation
- Builds capillary density over time with sufficient volume
- Lowers resting heart rate with consistent practice

**Best uses:** Recovery days between hard sessions, long easy base miles, post-race recovery week, warm-up/cool-down.

**Caution:** Purely sedentary people get meaningful benefit here. Fit athletes may need Zone 2 to get stimulus. Don't count this as "training" if you're already fit — it's recovery tool.

---

### Zone 2 — Aerobic Base (60–70% | 121–141 bpm at 19)

**What it feels like:** Comfortable but deliberate. Can speak in sentences, not paragraphs. Nose breathing possible.

**Primary benefits:**
- **Mitochondrial biogenesis** — increases the number and density of mitochondria in slow-twitch muscle fibers; this is the most important long-term adaptation for endurance athletes
- **Fat oxidation** — trains the body to use fat as fuel at intensity; spares glycogen for harder efforts
- **Cardiac output increase** — stroke volume increases over months of Zone 2 training (larger heart chambers)
- **Capillary density** — more delivery vessels to working muscle
- **Aerobic base** — the foundation all other zones are built on

**Best uses:** Base training phase, long slow distance (LSD) runs or rides, 80% of total training volume in polarized training, general aerobic fitness building.

**Research:** Dr. Iñigo San Millán (University of Colorado) emphasizes Zone 2 as the primary driver of metabolic health improvements in both athletes and patients with metabolic syndrome. 45–60 min minimum per session to get full mitochondrial stimulus.

---

### Zone 3 — Aerobic Threshold / Tempo (70–80% | 141–161 bpm at 19)

**What it feels like:** Comfortably hard. Can speak in short phrases. Rhythmic breathing required. Sustainable for ~1 hour max.

**Primary benefits:**
- **Lactate buffering** — trains the body to process lactate faster; raises the aerobic threshold
- **Cardiovascular efficiency** — heart becomes more efficient at higher sustained outputs
- **Tempo conditioning** — trains sustained race-pace efforts
- **Mental toughness** at sustained discomfort

**Best uses:** Tempo runs, steady-state cycling, marathon/half-marathon pace training, "sweet spot" training in cycling (a refined Zone 3–4 blend).

**Caution (Seiler's polarized model):** This zone is the "grey zone" — hard enough to cause fatigue, not hard enough to drive VO2 max adaptations. Elite endurance athletes minimize Zone 3. It can be valuable for competitive athletes racing at threshold, but it crowds out Zone 1 recovery volume and Zone 5 intensity if overdone. See [[Polarized Training]].

---

### Zone 4 — Lactate Threshold (80–90% | 161–181 bpm at 19)

**What it feels like:** Hard. Short sentences only. Breathing is labored. Sustainable for 20–60 min max.

**Primary benefits:**
- **Lactate threshold elevation** — the single most powerful adaptation for improving race performance; raising LT means you can go faster before lactate accumulates
- **Improved lactate clearance** — muscles and liver get better at removing lactate from blood
- **Race-pace conditioning** — 5K–10K run pace, cyclocross pace, rowing sprint pace
- **VO2 max floor rises** — indirectly elevates baseline aerobic ceiling

**Best uses:** Race-pace intervals (1–10 min efforts), threshold training blocks, 10K–half marathon training, cycling FTP work, rowing 2K training.

**Protocols:** 4 × 5 min at Zone 4 with 2 min recovery; 2 × 20 min at FTP; 6 × 1 mile at 10K race pace.

---

### Zone 5 — VO2 Max / Anaerobic (90–100% | 181–201 bpm at 19)

**What it feels like:** Maximal effort. Cannot speak. Breathing maxed out. Sustainable only 1–5 minutes per effort.

**Primary benefits:**
- **VO2 max ceiling expansion** — the largest aerobic adaptation possible; directly raises the "engine size"
- **Anaerobic capacity** — glycolytic system trained, increases ability to sustain maximal efforts
- **Neuromuscular power** — fast-twitch fiber recruitment and coordination
- **Post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC)** — elevated calorie burn for hours after session

**Best uses:** VO2 max intervals (2–5 min efforts), 800m–mile run training, HIIT workouts, all-out sprint training, competitive sport conditioning.

**Protocols:** 5 × 3 min at 110–120% FTP with 3 min recovery; 30 sec on / 15 sec off (Tabata variant); 6 × 800m at mile race pace. See [[VO2 Max Interval Training]] for full protocol guide.

**Caution:** High recovery cost (36–48 hrs between sessions). No more than 2 sessions/week. Must be built on a Zone 2 aerobic base to be effective.

## Summary: Which Zone for Which Goal?

| Goal | Primary Zone | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| General health | Zone 2 | 150 min/week (WHO minimum) |
| Fat loss | Zone 2 | Highest fat oxidation rate per minute |
| Aerobic base | Zone 2 | 80% of total volume |
| Race performance (endurance) | Zone 4 | Raise lactate threshold |
| Maximum fitness / VO2 max | Zone 5 | 2×/week max, in blocks |
| Recovery | Zone 1 | Day after hard session |
| Weight loss + time efficiency | Zone 5 (HIIT) | EPOC effect; 20 min = equivalent calorie burn |

## Zone System Variations

Different platforms and coaches use different zone counts. All are compatible — just different segmentation of the same HR range:

| System | Zones | Primary Use |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5-Zone (standard) | 1–5 | Running, general fitness, most coaches |
| 3-Zone (Seiler) | Easy / Medium / Hard | Elite endurance research model |
| 7-Zone (Coggan) | 1–7 | Cycling power (FTP-based, not HR-based) |
| Garmin (5-zone) | 1–5 | Consumer default, uses same formula |
| Polar (5-zone) | 1–5 | Adds "light aerobic" split in lower zones |

For a 19-year-old getting started: **use the 5-zone HR model** above. It's the most accessible, well-researched, and practical.

## Karvonen Formula (Heart Rate Reserve)

A more precise zone calculation uses **heart rate reserve (HRR)** — the range between resting and max HR.

**Formula:** Target HR = [(Max HR − Resting HR) × % intensity] + Resting HR

**Example for a 19-year-old with resting HR of 60 bpm:**
- Max HR = 201 bpm
- HRR = 201 − 60 = 141 bpm
- Zone 2 at 65%: (141 × 0.65) + 60 = 91.7 + 60 = **152 bpm**

Karvonen zones are ~10–15 bpm higher than simple % max HR zones, because resting HR is accounted for. This is a closer match to metabolic intensity — a fitter person with lower resting HR will have shifted zones compared to a sedentary person of the same age. See [[Research - Cardio Training Zones]] for full Karvonen zone table.

## Practical Application for a 19-Year-Old

1. **Start with Zone 2** — build aerobic base over 8–12 weeks before adding hard work
2. **Get a chest strap** — wrist HR monitors are ±10–15 bpm off during exercise; a chest strap (Garmin HRM-Pro, Polar H10) is accurate enough to zone-train reliably
3. **Follow polarized principle** — 80% Zone 1–2, 20% Zone 4–5; avoid chronic Zone 3 grey zone
4. **Progress Zone 5 in blocks** — 2–3 weeks of VO2 max work, then recover; don't sustain all year
5. **Track resting HR** — declining resting HR over weeks = aerobic adaptation working

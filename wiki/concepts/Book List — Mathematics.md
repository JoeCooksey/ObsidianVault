# Book List — Mathematics

> Mathematics books divide into two kinds: problem-solving books that build skill, and conceptual books that build intuition. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other.

## S-Tier

- **How to Prove It** — Daniel Velleman — proof-writing from scratch; the bridge from computation to mathematics; essential before any upper-division math course; ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid** — Douglas Hofstadter — self-reference, consciousness, and formal systems; math + music + art + logic; one of the most mind-expanding books ever written; 800+ pages, serious investment, worth it

## A-Tier

- **The Art of Problem Solving** series — Richard Rusczyk — competition math; the best problem-solving training available; *Introduction to Algebra* → *Introduction to Counting & Probability* → *Precalculus*; better than any school curriculum
- **Calculus** — Michael Spivak — rigorous single-variable calculus with proofs; builds real mathematical maturity beyond computation; the standard text for math-track calculus
- **Linear Algebra Done Right** — Sheldon Axler — the conceptually cleanest linear algebra text; determinant-free approach; essential for ML, signals, and control systems; ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ for EE
- **An Introduction to Mathematical Reasoning** — Peter Eccles — gentler proof-writing intro than Velleman; good for complete beginners to formal math

## B-Tier

- **The Princeton Companion to Mathematics** — Timothy Gowers — encyclopedia of mathematics; dip in and out; for the curious generalist who wants breadth
- **What Is Mathematics?** — Courant & Robbins — classic survey of mathematics from number theory through topology; dated but still illuminating
- **Mathematics: Its Content, Methods and Meaning** — Aleksandrov, Kolmogorov, Lavrentiev — Russian mathematics textbook tradition; 3 volumes; dense but comprehensive

## For EE Students (Priority Order)

1. *Linear Algebra Done Right* — most directly applicable: eigenvectors, SVD, state-space
2. *How to Prove It* — read before taking EEE 350 (Probability) or any grad-level course
3. *Calculus* (Spivak) — builds the rigor that distinguishes strong from weak EE graduate applicants
4. AoPS series — if you want to strengthen mathematical intuition before these

# Book List — Psychology and Persuasion

> Understanding how humans actually think — not how we think we think — is leverage for every domain: salary negotiation, relationships, leadership, sales, communication.

## S-Tier — Non-Negotiable

### Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion — Robert Cialdini (1984, updated 2021)
- The foundational text; every book on persuasion references this one
- **6 Principles of Influence**:
  1. **Reciprocity** — we feel obligated to return favors; give first, generously
  2. **Commitment & Consistency** — once committed, we follow through; get small yeses
  3. **Social Proof** — "people like me do this"; uncertainty defaults to consensus
  4. **Authority** — expertise signals trigger compliance; credentials + confidence
  5. **Liking** — we comply more for people we like; similarity, compliments, familiarity
  6. **Scarcity** — what's rare is valuable; deadlines and limited availability drive action
- Updated 2021 edition adds 7th principle: **Unity** (shared identity — "we are family")
- Read the 2021 edition; the new chapter on Unity alone is worth it

### Never Split the Difference — Chris Voss (2016)
- Former FBI lead hostage negotiator; most practical negotiation book in existence
- **Tactical Empathy**: identify and verbalize the emotions of the other side; "it seems like you feel..."
- **Mirroring**: repeat the last 1–3 words they said; creates safety + draws out more information
- **Labeling**: "It seems like..." statements that name emotions without judgment
- **The Accusation Audit**: list the worst things the other side could say about you, say them first
- **Calibrated Questions**: "How am I supposed to do that?" — not yes/no; opens problem-solving
- **No-Oriented questions**: "Would it be ridiculous to...?" — easier to say no than yes; gets honest responses
- Joe context: every salary negotiation, internship pitch, pricing conversation — this book pays for itself 100x

### How to Win Friends and Influence People — Dale Carnegie (1936)
- Already in wiki; timeless human relations principles
- See [[Dale Carnegie]] entity page

## A-Tier

### Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman (2011)
- Nobel Prize framework: System 1 (fast, intuitive, emotional) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate, rational)
- **Key cognitive biases to know**: Availability heuristic, Anchoring, Loss aversion (losses hurt 2× more than gains feel good), Halo effect, Substitution bias
- **Prospect Theory**: we make decisions based on gains and losses relative to a reference point, not absolute outcomes — the foundation of behavioral economics
- Dense (500 pages) — read Part I (heuristics and biases) and Part III (overconfidence) as minimum viable read
- Changes how you think about your own thinking permanently

### Pre-Suasion — Robert Cialdini (2016)
- The moment BEFORE the ask matters as much as the ask itself
- **Channel factors**: small environmental details (a candle, a warm cup of coffee, a good font) prime the frame
- **Privileged moments**: direct attention before communicating the message; what you say first determines what lens they use
- Read after Influence — it's the meta-level upgrade

### Getting to Yes — Roger Fisher & William Ury (1981, revised 2011)
- Harvard Negotiation Project; foundational principled negotiation framework
- **BATNA** (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement): know your walk-away and theirs; the source of power
- **Separate people from the problem**: the other side is not the enemy; the problem is the problem
- **Focus on interests, not positions**: positions are what people say they want; interests are why they want it
- Pairs naturally with Never Split the Difference — complementary frameworks (win-win structure vs. tactical empathy)

## Core Mental Models

- **Reciprocity First**: give without expectation; creates obligation without manipulation; Cialdini's most powerful principle
- **BATNA = Power**: you can't negotiate well without knowing your alternative; the stronger your BATNA, the more power you have
- **Tactical Empathy** (Voss): it's not about being nice — it's about demonstrating that you understand the other person's emotional reality
- **Loss Aversion** (Kahneman): frame what they stand to lose by not acting, not what they gain by acting — 2× more motivating
- **Anchoring**: the first number in any negotiation anchors the entire conversation; whoever sets the first anchor wins the frame

## Sequence
Read in this order for maximum compounding:
1. *How to Win Friends* — relational foundation
2. *Influence* — principles of compliance
3. *Never Split the Difference* — tactical implementation
4. *Getting to Yes* — structural framework for agreements
5. *Thinking, Fast and Slow* — the cognitive science underneath all of it

# Caffeine Timing Protocol

When you take caffeine matters as much as how much. Two critical timing decisions: (1) how long to wait after waking, and (2) how early to cut off before sleep.

---

## The 90-Minute Morning Delay (Huberman Protocol)

**Protocol**: Wait 90–120 minutes after waking before first caffeine dose.

**Mechanism rationale**:
- Morning cortisol peaks naturally in the first 30–60 minutes after waking (Cortisol Awakening Response) — this naturally clears adenosine and provides alertness
- Consuming caffeine during this window "wastes" caffeine's blocking effect when adenosine is already being cleared
- Delaying caffeine until adenosine starts re-accumulating (~90 min) means the caffeine works at a more productive moment
- Result: more sustained afternoon energy, reduced afternoon crash, less late-day caffeine need

**Scientific status**: The theory is mechanistically plausible, but direct RCT evidence for superiority of delayed timing vs. immediate consumption is limited. One major review of timing research found no studies showing measurably superior alertness effects from a delayed start vs. immediate.

> [!gap] The 90-minute rule is widely cited (Huberman Lab) and mechanistically coherent, but lacks dedicated RCT confirmation. The cortisol-timing argument assumes naive adenosine accumulation that may not apply identically to all wake patterns.

**Practical benefit confirmed**: Delaying the first dose does reduce the need for a second afternoon dose, which in turn protects sleep. This is the strongest downstream argument for the protocol.

---

## Sleep Cutoff — The Most Important Timing Rule

Caffeine's half-life is 5–6 hours (range: 1.5–9.5 hrs based on genetics). Even if you fall asleep normally, caffeine in your system degrades sleep architecture — particularly REM sleep and deep sleep — without you subjectively noticing.

| Dose | Last Dose Recommendation | Rationale |
|------|--------------------------|-----------|
| 100 mg (1 cup coffee) | ≥6 hours before bed | Standard guideline (AASM) |
| 100 mg | ≥8.8 hours before bed | Quantitative meta-analysis recommendation |
| 400 mg (full daily limit) | ≥12 hours before bed | Research shows significant sleep disruption this close to bed |
| Any dose | No later than 2 PM if sleeping at 10–11 PM | Conservative rule of thumb covering most people |

**Key finding**: Caffeine consumed 6 hours before bed still significantly reduces total sleep time by ~1 hour (measured objectively). The sleeper does not subjectively feel the disruption — they still fall asleep — but sleep quality is measurably impaired.

> [!warning] "I can drink coffee at 8 PM and sleep fine" is usually a sign of tolerance to caffeine's subjective alerting effects, not immunity to its sleep architecture disruption. The damage to sleep quality happens below the threshold of subjective awareness.

## Cortisol Interaction

Acute caffeine ingestion raises cortisol levels. However:
- **Regular users develop complete tolerance to the cortisol response within 5 days** of consistent caffeine intake (PMC2257922)
- This means the cortisol argument for delaying morning caffeine applies primarily to new or infrequent users
- Daily users are not meaningfully affected by caffeine-driven cortisol spikes

---

## Timing for Physical Performance

**Pre-workout dose**: 30–60 minutes before exercise is optimal for performance benefits.
- Caffeine peaks in blood plasma at ~45–60 minutes post-ingestion
- 3–6 mg/kg body weight is the ergogenic range (see [[Caffeine Complete Guide]])
- For evening workouts: use a lower dose (100–150 mg) and complete workout ≥6 hours before bed

---

## Morning Coffee Health Benefit Data

A 2025 NHLBI study found that **drinking coffee before noon** was associated with the greatest reductions in all-cause mortality and cardiovascular disease mortality. Morning timing appears to confer the strongest disease-prevention benefits — this is correlational data but aligns with existing coffee health research.

## Practical Timing Template

| Time | Action | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Wake (e.g., 6:30 AM) | No caffeine | Let cortisol and natural adenosine dynamics work |
| 8:00–9:00 AM | First dose (100–200 mg) | 90-min delay; optimal disease prevention window |
| 12:00–1:00 PM | Second dose if needed (100 mg max) | Only if needed; watch the sleep cutoff |
| 2:00 PM | Hard caffeine cutoff (for 10 PM bedtime) | Conservative; adjust for your sleep time and genetics |
| Pre-workout | 100–200 mg (if morning/midday workout) | Time to 45 min before training start |

# Book List — Stoicism and Philosophy

> The Stoic tradition is the most practical philosophy ever developed — designed for active people living in uncertain, high-stakes environments. Not academic. Immediately applicable.

## The Three Core Stoics (Primary Texts)

### Meditations — Marcus Aurelius (~170 AD)
- Private journal of a Roman emperor; never meant to be published
- Most re-readable philosophical text in existence — different insights emerge at different life stages
- **Core practice**: Negative Visualization (premeditatio malorum) — imagine losing what you have → deepens gratitude + preparedness
- **Dichotomy of Control**: "Some things are in our control, some are not. Our opinions, impulses, desires, aversions are in our control. Everything else is not."
- **Amor Fati**: love of fate; embrace what happens as if you had chosen it
- Gregory Hays translation (Modern Library, 2002) is the best modern English version
- Read 1 entry per day (Book 2, 4, 5, and 7 are highest density)

### Letters from a Stoic (Epistulae Morales ad Lucilium) — Seneca (65 AD)
- 124 letters from Seneca to his friend Lucilius; highly readable; feels like personal correspondence
- Best entry points: Letters 1 (on time), 7 (on crowds), 12 (on aging), 77 (on death)
- Letter 1: "Claim time for yourself, Lucilius; gather and save it." — the most important letter on focus ever written
- Seneca is the most witty and accessible of the three core Stoics

### Discourses — Epictetus (~108 AD)
- Slave turned philosopher; most rigorous treatment of the dichotomy of control
- "Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things." — foundational CBT precursor
- Harder to read than Meditations — use Robin Hard or Robert Dobbin translations
- The Enchiridion (handbook) is a 30-page distillation — read this first

## S-Tier Modern Stoicism

### Ego Is the Enemy — Ryan Holiday (2016)
- Targeted specifically at ambitious young people; most important Ryan Holiday book
- Three stages where ego destroys: Aspire (blocks learning), Succeed (breeds entitlement), Fail (prevents recovery)
- "The ego we see most commonly goes by a more subtle name: Confidence." — distinguishes healthy confidence from corrosive ego
- **Key figures throughout**: Katharine Graham, Bill Walsh, Ulysses S. Grant, Angela Merkel — case studies of ego-controlled success
- Joe context: 19yo with high ambitions in EE + tech — this book is essential calibration NOW, before success

### The Obstacle Is the Way — Ryan Holiday (2014)
- Three disciplines: Perception (how you see the obstacle), Action (what you do about it), Will (enduring what you cannot change)
- **Amor Fati in practice**: "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."
- Historical case studies: Rockefeller, Demosthenes, Marcus Aurelius, Amelia Earhart
- Pairs directly with Can't Hurt Me (Goggins) — one is philosophical, one is visceral

## A-Tier

### The Daily Stoic — Ryan Holiday & Steve Hanselman (2016)
- Best on-ramp for Stoicism; 366 daily entries (original Greek/Latin quote + meditation + commentary)
- Each entry is 1 page; 5 minutes/day; builds the mental models gradually
- Read before or alongside Meditations — provides the interpretive framework

### How to Be a Stoic — Massimo Pigliucci (2017)
- Modern philosophical exploration; Pigliucci is a former evolutionary biologist turned Stoic
- Uses Epictetus as a guide through practical modern scenarios (health, relationships, work, death)
- Best intellectual treatment of Stoicism for someone with a scientific mindset

## Stoic Practice Stack

The books above converge on the same practice stack:

1. **Morning journaling**: what am I grateful for? what could go wrong today (premeditatio)? what virtues do I want to exercise?
2. **Evening review**: where did I fall short? where did I hold my principles? what would a Stoic have done differently?
3. **Negative visualization** (weekly): deliberately imagine losing what matters — health, relationships, progress — to renew gratitude and reduce complacency
4. **Dichotomy check** (real-time): before reacting to anything, ask: is this in my control? If not, redirect energy to your response.

## Core Mental Models

- **Dichotomy of Control**: opinions, intentions, actions = yours; outcomes, reputation, others' behavior = not yours
- **Premeditatio Malorum**: premeditate the bad so it doesn't break you; Seneca: "let us prepare our minds as if we had come to the very end of life"
- **Amor Fati**: don't just tolerate what happens — love it; it's the only moment you have
- **Memento Mori**: remember you will die; this makes the present moment serious and precious, not depressing

# Neuroplasticity and Habit Formation

**Neuroplasticity** is the process by which the nervous system changes in response to experience. Habit formation *is* neuroplasticity — the formation of new neural circuits that make certain behaviors more likely to occur and others less likely.

## Habits as Neural Circuits

A habit is not a behavior stored in memory — it is a **neural pathway** that has been sufficiently reinforced to execute with reduced conscious control. With each repetition, small changes occur in the cognitive and neural mechanisms associated with **procedural memory** (Wood & Runger). These changes accumulate until the behavior becomes automatic.

## Procedural Memory's Role

**Procedural memory** holds the specific sequence of steps required for a habit to execute. It is distinct from declarative (factual) memory. The hippocampus is where these sequences are initially *formed* (not stored). As a habit consolidates:

1. The procedural sequence is encoded in the hippocampus
2. Over time, with repetition, it **migrates** to other brain regions (particularly basal ganglia circuits)
3. Once migrated, the behavior is context-independent and near-reflexive

This migration is what transforms a conscious, effortful behavior into a reflex-like habit.

## Where Rewiring Happens

Most neural circuit consolidation for habits occurs during **Phase 3 of the day** — deep sleep. The waking phases (Phase 1 and Phase 2) *trigger* the plasticity; sleep *consolidates* it. Disrupting sleep interrupts this consolidation process. See [[Three-Phase Day Framework]].

## Timeline

Habit formation varies enormously across individuals. Lally et al. (2010) found the same habit takes **18 to 254 days** to form in different people. Key variables:
- The person's baseline ability to manage [[Limbic Friction]]
- The nature of the habit (complexity, reward structure)
- Consistency of context and execution conditions

## Automaticity as the End State

The goal of habit formation is **automaticity** — when the neural circuits execute the behavior without requiring conscious initiation. Automaticity is confirmed when:
- The habit occurs across varied contexts (context independence)
- Limbic friction required is near zero
- The behavior happens even under adverse conditions (poor sleep, stress, distraction)

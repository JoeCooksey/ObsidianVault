# Classical Mechanics in Electrical Engineering

Physics 1 (mechanics) seems far from circuits — until you realize that motors, generators, MEMS sensors, acoustics, and vibration analysis all require mechanical reasoning. More subtly, the mathematical structure of mechanics is identical to that of circuits.

---

## The Mechanical-Electrical Analogy

The single most powerful insight from mechanics for EE:

| Mechanical Quantity | Electrical Equivalent | Unit analogy |
|---|---|---|
| Force $F$ | Voltage $V$ | "effort" variable |
| Velocity $v$ | Current $I$ | "flow" variable |
| Mass $m$ (inertia) | Inductance $L$ | $F = m\,da/dt$ ↔ $V = L\,dI/dt$ |
| Spring constant $k$ (compliance $1/k$) | Capacitance $C$ | $F = kx$ ↔ $Q = CV$ |
| Damper $b$ (viscous friction) | Resistance $R$ | $F = bv$ ↔ $V = RI$ |
| Mechanical power $P = Fv$ | Electrical power $P = VI$ | identical form |

This means every mechanical system has an electrically equivalent circuit, and vice versa. A mass-spring-damper is mathematically identical to an RLC circuit. You solve them with the same ODE:
$$m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = F(t) \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad L\ddot{q} + R\dot{q} + \frac{q}{C} = V(t)$$

---

## Newton's Laws → Kirchhoff's Laws

Newton's second law $\sum F = ma$ is structurally analogous to KVL:
$$\sum V = 0 \quad \text{(KVL)} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \sum F = 0 \text{ (D'Alembert)}$$

KCL (sum of currents = 0) is analogous to the continuity of flow at a junction.

## Motors and Generators — Mechanics Meets EE

### DC Motor
A DC motor converts electrical energy to mechanical energy. The governing equations couple both domains:

**Electrical side** (from Faraday's law + KVL):
$$V_a = L_a\frac{di_a}{dt} + R_a i_a + K_e\omega$$

**Mechanical side** (from Newton's second law for rotation):
$$J\frac{d\omega}{dt} = K_\tau i_a - b\omega - T_\text{load}$$

Here:
- $J$ = moment of inertia of rotor (from rotational mechanics)
- $K_e$ = back-EMF constant (V·s/rad)
- $K_\tau$ = torque constant (N·m/A)
- $\omega$ = angular velocity (rad/s)

Torque $\tau = J\alpha = J\,d\omega/dt$ is Newton's 2nd law for rotation — directly from PHY 121.

### Work-Energy in a Motor
Work done by a motor: $W = \int \tau \, d\theta = \int F \cdot v \, dt$. The power balance is:
$$P_\text{elec} = V_a i_a = i_a^2 R_a + \underbrace{K_e \omega i_a}_{P_\text{mech}}$$

Back-EMF ($K_e\omega$) is the voltage generated by the spinning rotor (Faraday's law). Mechanical output power = electrical input minus resistive losses.

---

## Conservation of Energy → Power Balance in Circuits

The work-energy theorem from mechanics ($W = \Delta KE$) generalizes to circuit power balance:

$$P_\text{source} = P_\text{resistors} + \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{2}LI^2\right) + \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{2}CV^2\right)$$

Energy stored in $L$ parallels kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$); energy in $C$ parallels potential energy ($\frac{1}{2}kx^2$). This isn't an analogy — it's the same physics.

## Vibration Analysis → RLC Resonance

A mechanical resonance problem (find the natural frequency of a spring-mass system):
$$\omega_n = \sqrt{k/m}$$

Identical form to RLC resonance:
$$\omega_n = \sqrt{1/LC} = 1/\sqrt{LC}$$

Application: **MEMS resonators** (micro-electromechanical systems) used in phone accelerometers and gyroscopes are literally mass-spring systems. The resonant frequency shifts when acceleration is applied — that's how the sensor works. The readout circuit is modeled as an equivalent LC circuit.

---

## Stress and Strain → Piezoelectrics and Transducers

Hooke's law: $\sigma = E\varepsilon$ (stress = Young's modulus × strain) is the mechanical foundation for:
- **Piezoelectric sensors**: mechanical strain generates voltage (e.g., ultrasonic transducers, quartz crystal oscillators in MCUs)
- **MEMS pressure sensors**: membrane deflection converts to capacitance change → voltage output
- **Accelerometers (MEMS)**: proof mass displacement → capacitance change → 3-axis acceleration readout (used in every smartphone, ABS system, airbag)

---

## Acoustic Systems — Vibration at Audible Frequencies

Loudspeaker design is a direct mechanical-electrical conversion problem:
- Electrical: voice coil driven by amplifier
- Mechanical: cone mass, spider/surround stiffness, air damping → mass-spring-damper
- Acoustic: radiation impedance of air

Loudspeaker modeling uses the **Thiele-Small parameters** — all derived from the mechanical-electrical analogy. The crossover filter network (LC filter) is designed to match the acoustic mechanical resonance of the drivers.

---

## Rotational Dynamics → AC Machine Analysis

For three-phase AC motors (induction, PMSM):
- Rotor inertia $J$ and load torque $T_L$ determine the speed ramp-up time: $t_\text{ramp} = J\omega_\text{rated}/T_\text{avg}$
- Slip in an induction motor comes from the difference between synchronous speed and rotor speed — a rotational mechanics problem
- EV traction inverter design must account for the inertia of the drivetrain, wheels, and vehicle mass to size the inverter current rating

---

## Momentum and Charge

Linear momentum $p = mv$ is conserved in collisions → analogous to charge conservation (KCL). Both are continuity equations. In plasma physics and vacuum tubes, particle momentum directly determines current.

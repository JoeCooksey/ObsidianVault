# Active Reading Techniques

Specific techniques for engaging with text while reading — turning passive consumption into active processing. Each technique forces cognitive work, which is what creates memory.

---

## The SQ3R Method

Developed by Francis P. Robinson, 1948. The most research-validated structured reading framework. Designed for textbooks and dense nonfiction; adapts to most books.

### The Five Steps

**1. Survey (Before reading)**
- Read chapter title and headings
- Read introduction and conclusion/summary
- Scan any bolded terms, figures, and captions
- Time: 5–10 minutes per chapter
- Goal: build a mental skeleton before filling in the flesh

**2. Question (Before reading)**
- Convert every heading into a question
- Example: "The SQ3R Method" → "What is SQ3R and why does it work?"
- Write these questions down
- Goal: reading to answer questions is 40–60% more effective than reading to absorb information (Educational Psychology Review, 2017)

**3. Read (During)**
- Read to answer your questions — not to finish the chapter
- Move at the pace of understanding, not the pace of the words
- When you find an answer to a question, mark it
- Do not re-read sentences just because they were hard — move forward; context often resolves confusion

**4. Recite (After each section)**
- Close the book
- Answer your questions aloud or in writing, in your own words
- Do NOT look at the text
- Where you stumble = where you did not truly understand
- Research: the 3R group (Read–Recite–Review) outperformed note-takers and re-readers in free recall AND worked faster

**5. Review (After the chapter)**
- Reread your questions and answers
- Look at headings again
- Fill gaps
- Review again 24 hours later

## Annotation System

Highlighting alone is nearly useless — studies show it produces no significant retention benefit without processing. The annotation is the technique; the highlight just marks where you annotated.

### Rules

**Rule 1 — Highlight ≤ 1 sentence per paragraph**
If you highlight more than one sentence per paragraph consistently, you are not selecting — you are copying. Forced selectivity = forced processing.

**Rule 2 — Always write a marginal note explaining WHY you highlighted**
The annotation is where the learning happens. Good annotations:
- Summarize the point in your own words
- Flag a question ("But what about X?")
- Note a connection ("Like the SQ3R principle")
- Flag a disagreement ("I think this is wrong because...")
- Mark application potential ("Apply to study sessions")

**Rule 3 — Use a symbol system**
Develop a consistent shorthand:
| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| ⭐ | Core insight — important to remember |
| ? | Confused, need to investigate |
| ! | Surprising or counterintuitive |
| → | Action or application |
| ≈ | Connects to something I already know |
| ✗ | I disagree |

**Rule 4 — Annotate relationships, not just content**
The most powerful annotations are connections: "This contradicts what Adler says about syntopical reading" or "This is the same as Feynman's explanation in QED."

## The Feynman Technique (Applied to Reading)

Developed from Richard Feynman's personal learning practice. The most powerful real-time comprehension check.

### Steps
1. **Read a section** (a chapter, a concept, a key argument)
2. **Close the book** — do not peek
3. **Explain the concept simply**, as if teaching it to a smart 12-year-old
4. **Identify where you stumbled** — these are genuine gaps in understanding
5. **Return to the source** to fill exactly those gaps
6. **Re-explain** until you can do it cleanly

### Why It Works
- Forces retrieval (not re-reading)
- Distinguishes familiarity from understanding (they feel identical; Feynman breaks the illusion)
- Simplifying forces you to discard jargon and reach for the actual meaning
- Reveals exactly where the gaps are (vs. re-reading which reassures you without testing you)

### Variants
- **Teach-back**: explain it out loud to another person (or an imaginary one)
- **Blank-page summary**: after a chapter, write everything you remember on a blank page — no notes, no book
- **Podcast method**: imagine you're being interviewed about this book on a podcast — what do you say?

## The Connection Rule

After every key idea:
1. Ask: "What does this connect to in what I already know?"
2. Ask: "Where does this contradict or refine something I believe?"
3. Ask: "Where can I apply this in the next 7 days?"

This is Charlie Munger's "latticework of mental models" built bottom-up. Every new idea without a hook to existing knowledge is a loose thread — it will fall out. Every new idea connected to existing knowledge strengthens both.

## When NOT to Read Slowly

**Superficial first pass** (Adler's inspectional reading rule): read through the whole book once, continuously, without stopping. Don't re-read, don't look up words, don't pause on confusion. Just move.

**Why**: Most confusion at Chapter 3 resolves itself at Chapter 5. Reading slowly through confusion makes you pedantic, not thorough. Finish the book once, then do a proper analytical reading pass.

**Apply this to**: new domain books, narrative nonfiction, any book where you're not sure it deserves deep treatment.

## The 50-Page Rule (for quitting books)

Per Ryan Holiday and Naval Ravikant:
- Subtract your age from 100 — that's how many pages to give a book before quitting
- At age 19: give any book ~81 pages
- If it hasn't earned continued attention by then, put it down

**Quitting books strategically is not failure — it is curation.** Time spent finishing bad books is time stolen from great ones.

## Research Backing

- **Retrieval practice > re-reading**: Testing yourself on material produces 50–100% better retention than re-reading (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006)
- **Annotation + marginalia beats highlighting alone**: Processing required by annotation activates deeper encoding
- **Spaced review compounds retention**: Reviewing at increasing intervals (1, 3, 7, 14, 30 days) dramatically outperforms single-pass review

# Food Health Tier List — Dairy

Ranking criteria: **pure healthiness** — protein content, probiotic richness, calcium bioavailability (~30% absorption from dairy, highest of non-enriched foods), vitamin content, processing level, and net health outcomes. Sources: Harvard T.H. Chan nutrition, NHS, USDA Dairy, Cleveland Clinic, Dairy MAX.

Note: Dairy debate in nutrition is ongoing. Full-fat vs. low-fat evidence is mixed. Fermented dairy (yogurt, kefir, cheese) consistently shows the strongest positive outcomes. Non-fermented/processed dairy shows less consistent benefits.

---

## S Tier — Best Dairy

| Food | Per Serving | Why |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Greek Yogurt (plain, full-fat or 2%)** | 17–20g protein per 3/4 cup | Most protein of any common dairy product; live active cultures (Lactobacillus, Streptococcus) → gut microbiome support, immune function; calcium (20% DV); B12; thicker texture = more protein concentration; plain is critical — flavored versions load sugar |

---

## A Tier — Excellent Dairy

| Food | Per Serving | Why |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Kefir (plain)** | 10g protein per 8oz | Most diverse probiotic profile in dairy (up to 61 strains of bacteria and yeasts vs. 2–7 in yogurt); calcium, B12, vitamin K2 (directs calcium to bones, not arteries); often tolerated by lactose-intolerant individuals due to bacteria that pre-digest lactose |
| **Plain Yogurt (whole milk)** | 9g protein per 8oz | Similar probiotic benefit to Greek yogurt; calcium (30% DV); potassium (most concentrated of all dairy per calorie); gut and immune health evidence solid; choose plain, add your own berries |
| **Cottage Cheese (low-fat or full-fat)** | 25g protein per cup | Surprisingly high protein; casein protein → slow-digesting (ideal before bed); calcium, B12, selenium; whey protein in cottage cheese shown to reduce blood pressure and H. pylori bacteria |

---

## B Tier — Good Dairy

| Food | Per Serving | Why |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Whole Milk** | 8g protein, 300mg calcium per 8oz | 13 essential vitamins and minerals; vitamin D (fortified in most countries); vitamin A; most bioavailable calcium source; full-fat version also contains vitamin K2 and more fat-soluble vitamins than skim; debate on whether saturated fat in milk causes harm is not settled |
| **Parmesan Cheese** | 11g protein, 336mg calcium per 1oz | Highest calcium of any cheese — 24% DV per ounce; aged hard cheeses have lowest lactose; some evidence of beneficial gut bacteria surviving in aged cheese |
| **Ricotta Cheese (whole milk)** | 7g protein, 257mg calcium per 1/2 cup | Whey protein base; shown to reduce H. pylori and hypertension risk; calcium; good source of B vitamins; lower in saturated fat per protein gram than many cheeses |
| **Swiss Cheese** | 8g protein, 224mg calcium per 1oz | Lower sodium than most cheeses (54mg/oz vs. cheddar at 176mg); high calcium; vitamin B12; holes = lower calorie density |

---

## C Tier — Decent Dairy With Notable Caveats

| Food | Per Serving | Why |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Skim / 2% Milk** | 8g protein, 300mg calcium | Same protein/calcium as whole milk; lower saturated fat; but removes fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K that come with the fat naturally (must be fortified back); some evidence full-fat dairy is equivalent or better for metabolic outcomes |
| **Cheddar Cheese** | 7g protein, 200mg calcium per 1oz | Calcium, protein; but 176mg sodium and 6g saturated fat per ounce; moderate in portions |
| **Mozzarella (fresh)** | 6g protein, 143mg calcium per 1oz | Lower sodium and fat than cheddar; part-skim has good protein-to-fat ratio; modest calcium |
| **Ice Cream (plain, low additives)** | 3g protein, 85mg calcium per 1/2 cup | Contains some dairy nutrition but significant added sugar (14–20g/serving); the dairy base is real but the sugar load diminishes health value considerably |
| **Sour Cream** | 1g protein, 3% DV calcium per 2 tbsp | Mostly fat; low protein; some benefit from culture if it says "active cultures" |

---

## D Tier — Minimal Health Value

| Food | Per Serving | Why |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Processed Cheese Slices (American cheese)** | 3–4g protein, 10% DV calcium | Legally must be called "cheese product" not cheese; contains emulsifiers, food coloring, added whey; calcium absorption is lower than natural cheese; high sodium (200–300mg/slice); minimal probiotic value |
| **Flavored Yogurt (sweetened)** | 5–8g protein, 15% DV calcium | The yogurt base is healthy; but 20–30g added sugar per serving in many brands completely undermines the benefit; some brands have more sugar than a candy bar; read labels carefully |
| **Cream Cheese** | 2g protein, 2% DV calcium per 2 tbsp | Mostly fat (10g); negligible protein or calcium per serving; no probiotic value; essentially edible fat with minimal nutrition |
| **Whipped Cream** | 0g protein, 2% DV calcium per 2 tbsp | Almost pure fat; no nutritional value beyond trace calcium; treat only |

## F Tier — Dairy Products to Avoid

| Food | Why |
|------|-----|
| **Coffee Creamer (non-dairy)** | Misnomer — often contains hydrogenated oils, corn syrup, artificial flavors; no dairy nutrition whatsoever; associated with worse metabolic outcomes than real dairy |
| **Dairy-Based Desserts (ultra-processed)** | Processed ice cream bars, flavored dairy drinks, milkshakes with additives — ultra-processed dairy with maximum sugar/fat and minimal nutrient density |

## Key Principles for Dairy

1. **Fermented > fresh > processed**: Kefir > yogurt > cheese > milk > processed cheese — fermentation increases bioavailability, adds probiotics, reduces lactose
2. **Plain is always better**: Every dairy product's health value drops when manufacturers add sugar; always buy plain and add your own flavoring
3. **Full-fat vs. skim is not settled**: Recent meta-analyses find no clear benefit of skim over full-fat; some evidence full-fat dairy is associated with LOWER diabetes risk; the fear of dairy fat is likely overstated
4. **Calcium absorption is 30% from dairy** — better than most plant sources (spinach calcium is ~5% absorbed due to oxalate binding); dairy is genuinely the most efficient calcium source
5. **Lactose intolerance doesn't mean all dairy**: Fermented dairy (yogurt, kefir, aged cheese) is often tolerated because bacteria pre-digest lactose; kefir specifically is often tolerated by lactose-intolerant individuals
6. **Probiotic dairy requires "live and active cultures"** on label — pasteurization after fermentation kills cultures; look for this label

# Habit Loop

The four-stage neurological cycle that underlies every automatic behavior. Proposed by [[James Clear]] in *Atomic Habits* (2018) as an extension of Charles Duhigg's three-stage model (Cue → Routine → Reward).

## The Four Stages

```
CUE ──► CRAVING ──► RESPONSE ──► REWARD
 ▲                                  │
 └──────────── feedback ────────────┘
```

### 1. Cue
A trigger that predicts a reward. The brain is constantly scanning the environment for signals of where rewards are located.
- Can be anything: time of day, location, emotional state, preceding action, other people
- The cue itself is not the habit — it is the information that predicts a coming reward
- **Design implication**: to build a habit, make the cue obvious and consistent

### 2. Craving
The motivational force behind every habit. The craving is for the *change in state* that the reward will bring, not the reward itself.
- You do not crave a cigarette — you crave the feeling of relief it produces
- You do not crave checking your phone — you crave the stimulation and novelty
- Dopamine spikes during the craving phase (anticipation), not just at reward delivery
- **Design implication**: to build a habit, make it attractive (so craving fires); to break one, reduce the craving signal

### 3. Response
The actual behavior — the habit itself. This can be a thought or an action.
- Only occurs if the craving is sufficiently strong and the friction is low enough
- Friction = [[Limbic Friction]]: physical effort, mental effort, emotional resistance
- **Design implication**: reduce friction (make it easy) to ensure the response executes

### 4. Reward
The end goal of every habit. Rewards satisfy the craving and signal to the brain to encode the loop.
- Two functions: (1) satisfies the craving, (2) teaches the brain that the cue → response sequence is worth remembering
- Immediate rewards encode faster than delayed rewards (temporal discounting problem)
- **Design implication**: make it satisfying; add immediate rewards to habits with delayed natural payoffs

## How the Loop Builds

Repeated cue → craving → response → reward cycles cause:
1. The cue becomes reliably linked to the craving (conditioning)
2. The response becomes automated — shifted from hippocampus (deliberate) to basal ganglia (automatic)
3. The habit runs with minimal conscious effort

See [[Neuroplasticity and Habit Formation]] for the neural substrate of this migration.

## Comparison: Duhigg's 3-Step vs. Clear's 4-Step

| Duhigg (2012) | Clear (2018) |
|---|---|
| Cue | Cue |
| — | **Craving** (new) |
| Routine | Response |
| Reward | Reward |

Adding the Craving stage matters because: (1) it explains why identical cues can produce different behaviors in different people (different craving associations), and (2) it explains the dopamine-anticipation research of Wolfram Schultz (reward prediction error).

## The Four Laws Correspondence

Each stage of the loop maps to one of the [[Four Laws of Behavior Change]]:

| Loop Stage | Law to Build Habit | Law to Break Habit |
|---|---|---|
| Cue | Make it **Obvious** | Make it **Invisible** |
| Craving | Make it **Attractive** | Make it **Unattractive** |
| Response | Make it **Easy** | Make it **Difficult** |
| Reward | Make it **Satisfying** | Make it **Unsatisfying** |

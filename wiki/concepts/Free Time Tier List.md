# Free Time Tier List

Ranked by **long-term compounding ROI** across career, health, relationships, and identity.
Calibrated for Joe (19yo EE student, ASU Year 1, Livermore CA, trains 6 days/week).

> See research and sources at [[Research - Free Time Tier List]].

---

## S-Tier — Maximum Compounding, Do More

### Deep Skill-Building (EE-Specific)
**LTspice, Python, Git, breadboarding — self-study in Year 1 free time**

The single highest-leverage use of free time for Joe right now. Year 1 at ASU has zero EE courses — that gap is the opportunity. Every hour building these skills before EEE 202 Circuits I creates a compounding advantage over peers. Career ROI is direct: internship differentiation, GitHub portfolio, faster course mastery.

- **Where**: Bedroom desk, Makerspace, online (Neso Academy, Paul McWhorter)
- **Time investment**: 5–10 hrs/week is enough to be dramatically ahead
- **Rule**: Build something, don't just watch tutorials → output matters

### Deep Reading (Books from the Reading List)
**15–30 pages/day = 15+ books/year = top 1% of adults**

Builds sustained attention, mental models, vocabulary, and abstract reasoning. No other free-time activity produces the same breadth of cognitive gain per hour. Joe's Phase 1–3 reading list (Psychology of Money → Ego Is the Enemy → Deep Work → Can't Hurt Me…) is precisely calibrated.

- **Rule**: Physical book or Libby audiobook during commutes; no skimming
- **Current priority**: [[Book List — Finance and Investing]] + [[Book List — Stoicism and Philosophy]]
- **Evidence**: Harvard 80-year study participants who read regularly had higher cognitive reserve at 70+

### High-Quality In-Person Social Time
**Close friends, mentors, study groups, club meetings**

The Harvard Study of Adult Development (80 years, n=700+): relationship quality — not wealth, career success, or fame — is the single strongest predictor of late-life happiness, physical health, and cognitive longevity. Joe is at the most critical period for forming deep friendships (college years). These relationships will warm-introduce him to LLNL connections, grad school recommenders, and career mentors.

- **Rule**: Prioritize repeat-contact contexts (clubs, study groups, cycling teams) over one-off parties
- **Target**: 2–3 consistent "people" per semester — goes deeper than spreading thin
- **Evidence**: One good relationship predicts health outcomes as strongly as quitting smoking

## A-Tier — High Value, Compound Slowly but Consistently

### Building Projects (GitHub, PCB Designs, Side Projects)
Any project you **make and ship** builds identity capital — tangible proof of who you are and what you can do. GitHub repos visible to internship recruiters. PCB designs to show at LLNL interviews. Even tiny projects compound over 4 years into a portfolio that separates you.

- **Examples**: Arduino LED matrix, Python circuit simulation script, LTspice RLC design writeup
- **Rule**: Ship something small every 3–4 weeks, even if imperfect

### Joining and Showing Up to Clubs (IEEE, Solar Car, Cycling Team)
Consistent presence in the right contexts is how social capital compounds. It's not what you do at a single event — it's the cumulative effect of repeated contact with the same people over a semester. IEEE ASU connects you to engineering networks; Solar Car is technical experience + team leadership. These are also how you find study partners, project collaborators, and eventually references.

### Deliberate Rest (Non-Screen, Non-Passive)
Walking, napping, slow cooking, journaling — cognitively restorative activities that allow subconscious processing. The "incubation" phase of creativity relies on genuine downtime. This is different from scrolling, which provides stimulation without restoration.

- **Evidence**: Dijksterhuis (2004) "unconscious thought advantage" — complex decisions improve after true rest vs. deliberate thinking
- **Rule**: When tired, rest deliberately (walk, nap) rather than scroll

### Journaling / Weekly Review
10–15 min/week of structured self-reflection dramatically improves decision-making, goal tracking, and self-awareness over time. Spencer Greenberg research: people who write about their goals are 42% more likely to achieve them. Also the most evidence-backed stress-reduction tool after exercise.

## B-Tier — Good, Supporting Role

### High-Quality Educational Media
Huberman Lab, 3Blue1Brown, Lex Fridman technical episodes, Kurzgesagt. Better than passive entertainment — builds mental models. But it's consuming, not building. Don't let it crowd out reading or project work.

- **Rule**: Use for commutes, workouts (Zone 2 cardio = perfect pairing), not as a primary free-time block

### Cooking / Food Prep
Daily life skill that compounds every single day for 60+ years. Learning to cook efficiently (5–10 high-nutrition recipes well) saves money, improves diet quality, and provides genuine flow. Underrated by male college students.

### Outdoor Time / Nature
Even 20–30 min in nature measurably reduces cortisol and improves attention (ART: Attention Restoration Theory, Kaplan 1989). Especially valuable before deep work sessions. Livermore has excellent trails.

### Watching High-Quality Films / Documentaries
Not "B-tier" because it's mediocre — some films (2001: A Space Odyssey, The Thin Red Line, Andrei Rublev) are genuine works of art. But schedule it intentionally, not as a default.

## C-Tier — Okay in Moderation, Low Compounding

### Gaming
Flow-state possible in complex strategy/action games. Social value in multiplayer. But external ROI is near-zero, and it has a history of crowding out higher-value activities. Cap at 1–2 hrs/week intentional sessions.

### Casual TV / Netflix
Entertainment is legitimate. But it's passive and low-density. The 2024 average American watches 4+ hours of TV daily — this is the baseline to beat, not to match. One series at a time, watched deliberately, is fine.

### Passive Socializing (Hanging Out Without Activity)
Fine for recovery and bonding. But significantly lower compounding than activity-based social time (cycling together, cooking together, project collaboration). Mix both.

---

## D-Tier — Time Sinks, Minimal Return

### Social Media Browsing (Instagram, TikTok, Reddit Rabbit Holes)
The research is unambiguous: heavy short-form video use degrades sustained attention (reduced P300 brainwave response in heavy users). Leisure reading time has declined from 23 min/day (2004) to <16 min/day (2018) as social media rose — these activities directly cannibalize each other. Occasional check-ins fine; mindless scrolling is the default failure mode.

- **Rule**: No phone in the first 60 min after waking; set a 20-min daily cap if possible

### Excessive Partying / Regular Heavy Drinking
Social upside (meeting people) is entirely achievable without the alcohol. Disrupts sleep architecture even if you "sleep through" (alcohol suppresses REM), degrades next-day cognition, and drains money. Occasional celebration ≠ D-tier; regular heavy drinking = genuinely harmful for a 19-year-old trying to compound health and cognition.

---

## F-Tier — Actively Harmful

### Doom-Scrolling at Night
Phone in bed → blue light → delayed melatonin onset → less REM → worse cognition next day → feel worse → need dopamine hits → more phone. The loop is measurable and well-documented. Physical alarm clock, phone charger outside bedroom.

### Comparison Scrolling (Keeping Up With Social Media "Success Stories")
Leads to upward social comparison, which is one of the most reliable happiness-reducers identified in modern social psychology. Instagram highlight reels at 19 compared against your own realistic life = toxic.

## The Core Principle
> Every hour has an opportunity cost. The question is not "is this enjoyable?" but "is this the best thing I could be doing with this hour, given who I'm trying to become?"

The biggest lever is not doing more S-tier activities — it's **eliminating D and F-tier defaults** that fill the gaps by inertia.

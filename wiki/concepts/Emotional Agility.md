# Emotional Agility

A framework developed by [[Susan David]] for navigating thoughts, emotions, and stories in healthy ways. The core claim: the goal is not to eliminate difficult emotions, but to relate to them differently.

## Definition

**Short**: The ability to experience your inner life — thoughts, emotions, and stories — in ways that are healthy.

**Full**: The ability to be with difficult thoughts, emotions, and stories in ways that are:
- **Compassionate** — without judgment about whether you "should" be feeling this
- **Curious** — asking what this emotion is signposting about your needs and values
- **Courageous** — willing to take values-connected action even when it's uncomfortable

## The Three Internal Targets

Emotional agility operates on three types of inner experience:

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Thoughts** | "I'm not good enough," "there's no point in trying" |
| **Emotions** | Stress, grief, anxiety, sadness, anger |
| **Stories** | Narratives written in childhood about whether you're worthy, creative, capable |

All three are normal. None of them are facts. Emotional agility means holding them lightly rather than being driven by them.

## The Three Practical Tools

1. **[[Gentle Acceptance]]** — stop struggling with whether you're allowed to feel what you feel; drop the rope
2. **[[Emotion Granularity]]** — replace broad labels ("stressed") with precise ones ("disappointed," "unsupported") to activate the brain's action pathways
3. **[[Defusion Language]]** — replace "I am X" with "I notice I'm feeling X" to create space between stimulus and response

## What Emotional Agility Is Not

- Not suppression — pushing emotions aside doesn't process them; it stores them
- Not toxic positivity — forcing false positivity is less resilient than engaging with difficulty
- Not passive resignation — gentle acceptance of an emotion is the starting point for moving through it, not staying stuck

## Contrast with [[Emotional Rigidity]]

Emotional rigidity is when thoughts, emotions, and stories become fused with identity and drive behavior away from values. Emotional agility is the antidote: holding those same experiences lightly, staying connected to values, and choosing action deliberately.

## Values as the Compass

The endpoint of emotional agility is not just "feeling better" — it is values-connected action. The question to ask after accepting and labeling an emotion is: *what does this tell me about what I care about, and what do I want to do?*

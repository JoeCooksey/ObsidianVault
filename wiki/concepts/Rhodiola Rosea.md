# Rhodiola Rosea

Rhodiola rosea is an adaptogenic herb primarily studied for reducing fatigue and improving performance under stress. It is the best-evidenced adaptogen for acute mental fatigue — relevant for exam periods and late-night study sessions.

## Mechanism

Rosavins and salidroside (active compounds) modulate the HPA axis (stress response), reduce cortisol, and may influence serotonin and dopamine pathways. Unlike stimulants, it reduces the perception of fatigue rather than masking it with stimulation.

## Evidence Quality: Medium (for fatigue / stress)

**Best evidence context:** Physicians on night shifts, students under exam stress, professionals with high cognitive load.

Key findings:
- SHR-5 extract (standardized): improved attention, cognitive function, and mental performance in physicians under fatigue conditions
- EMA (European Medicines Agency) approved Rhodiola for traditional use as an adaptogen for temporary relief of stress-related fatigue
- 16 RCTs (2000–2023, total ~363 participants): mostly positive for fatigue reduction, but study quality is mixed
- Small 2024 study in athletes: cognitive (Stroop) and physical (anaerobic) improvements at short-term supplementation

**Honest caveat:** "Results are contradictory," sample sizes small. The EMA approval is for "traditional use" — a lower bar than full clinical efficacy. Larger, high-quality RCTs are needed.

## Practical Details

**Dosage:**
- 300–680 mg/day of standardized extract (3% rosavins / 1% salidroside)
- SHR-5 is the most studied standardized form
- Take on an empty stomach, 30 minutes before work or exercise

**Timing:** Morning or pre-study. Has mild stimulant properties — avoid evening dosing.

**Cycling:** Some practitioners recommend 5 weeks on, 1–2 weeks off. Not mandatory but may prevent tolerance.

**Safety:** Well-tolerated. Mild side effects: irritability, insomnia (if taken late), vivid dreams. Avoid if on SSRIs or antidepressants without medical consultation (potential serotonergic interaction).

## Best Use Case

Acute high-stress periods — finals week, project deadlines, sleep-restricted study sessions. Not a daily baseline supplement like foundational nutrients. Think of it as a situational tool.

> [!gap] Most studies are small and heterogeneous. The 363 total participants across 16 trials is insufficient for high-confidence conclusions. Evidence for cognitive performance specifically (vs. fatigue reduction) is weaker.

# Limbic Friction

A term coined by [[Andrew Huberman]] (not found in formal neuroscience literature) to describe the **activation energy required to override internal state** and execute a desired behavior.

## Two Sources of Limbic Friction

Limbic friction arises from one of two states governed by the **autonomic nervous system**:

1. **Over-arousal** — too anxious or stressed to settle into a task; the nervous system is stuck in a high-alert state
2. **Under-arousal** — too tired, unmotivated, or low-energy to initiate; the system is in a low-activation state

These two states are on opposite ends of the autonomic nervous system seesaw (alert ↔ calm). Both impair the ability to engage in new or effortful behaviors.

> [!note] Not a formal term
> "Limbic friction" synthesizes autonomic nervous system research and habit psychology into a single practical metric. It is not a clinical or peer-reviewed term.

## Role in Habit Formation

Limbic friction is one of the two axes defining [[Habit Strength]]:
- **High limbic friction** = habit is weakly embedded, requires conscious effort each time
- **Low / zero limbic friction** = habit has reached [[automaticity]]; executes near-reflexively

The goal of habit formation is to progressively reduce limbic friction for a target behavior until it reaches automaticity.

## Practical Measurement

Huberman proposes using limbic friction as a self-assessment tool before attempting to execute a habit:
- High limbic friction right now → predict difficulty, potentially schedule the habit in Phase 1 of the day (see [[Three-Phase Day Framework]])
- Low limbic friction → habit may already be well-embedded, or current neurochemical state is favorable

## Relationship to Phase 1 of the Day

Phase 1 (0–8h post-waking) is the best window to tackle high-limbic-friction habits because elevated norepinephrine, epinephrine, and dopamine naturally lower the cost of overriding internal resistance. See [[Three-Phase Day Framework]].

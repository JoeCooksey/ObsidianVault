# Tirzepatide

Tirzepatide is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist developed by Eli Lilly — the first drug in its class and currently the most effective FDA-approved treatment for obesity. It is a single synthetic peptide molecule that activates two hormone receptors simultaneously: the glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) receptor and the glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor. This dual action produces weight loss results meaningfully superior to any prior GLP-1 monotherapy.

## Brand Names and Indications

| Brand | Indication | Max Dose |
|---|---|---|
| **Mounjaro** | Type 2 Diabetes | 15 mg/week |
| **Zepbound** | Chronic weight management / Obstructive sleep apnea | 15 mg/week |

---

## Mechanism — Why Dual Agonism Wins

**GLP-1 receptor activation (shared with semaglutide):**
- Central appetite suppression (hypothalamus/brainstem)
- Slows gastric emptying → prolonged satiety
- Glucose-dependent insulin secretion

**GIP receptor activation (unique to tirzepatide):**
- Improves insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues (muscle, fat, liver)
- Shifts fat deposition away from visceral (organ-surrounding) toward subcutaneous locations
- Amplifies post-meal insulin response synergistically with GLP-1
- Reduces appetite independently of GLP-1 via GIP brain receptors
- Thought to reduce GI side effects vs. pure GLP-1 agonists (some GIP signaling in the gut appears anti-nausea)

The GIP mechanism was initially controversial — earlier GIP-only drugs were neutral on weight. The key insight: GIP and GLP-1 are *synergistic*, not additive. The combination exceeds what either achieves alone.

## Dosing Table (Zepbound / Mounjaro)

| Week | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–4 | **2.5 mg/week** | Initiation — sub-therapeutic, GI adaptation |
| 5–8 | **5 mg/week** | Begin to see appetite suppression |
| 9–12 | **7.5 mg/week** | Meaningful weight loss for most patients |
| 13–16 | **10 mg/week** | Significant effect; some patients maintain here |
| 17–20 | **12.5 mg/week** | Continue escalation if tolerated |
| 21+ | **15 mg/week** | Maximum dose; maximum efficacy |

**Important:** 2.5 mg is not a therapeutic dose — it is purely an initiation step. Minimum 4 weeks at each dose before escalation. Many patients find their ideal maintenance dose is 5–10 mg (not everyone needs 15 mg to achieve their goals).

## Weight Loss Outcomes (SURMOUNT Trials)

| Trial | Population | Duration | Weight Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| SURMOUNT-1 | Obesity, no T2D | 72 weeks | **20.9%** (15 mg arm) |
| SURMOUNT-2 | Obesity + T2D | 72 weeks | **15.7%** (15 mg arm) |
| SURMOUNT-5 | Head-to-head vs. semaglutide 2.4 mg | 72 weeks | **20.2% vs. 13.7%** — tirzepatide wins by 47% |
| SURMOUNT-4 | Maintenance | 88 weeks | Weight regain study — see [[GLP Medication Exit Strategy]] |

---

## SURMOUNT-5: Head-to-Head vs. Semaglutide

The most important comparison trial. Direct head-to-head (NEJM, 2025):
- **Tirzepatide 15 mg:** −20.2% body weight, −18.4 cm waist circumference
- **Semaglutide 2.4 mg:** −13.7% body weight, −13.0 cm waist circumference
- Tirzepatide won on every measured endpoint at 72 weeks
- The gap was not marginal — 47% greater relative weight loss

This is a definitive result. For weight management, tirzepatide is currently the superior agent.

## Additional Indications

**Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA):** Zepbound received FDA approval specifically for OSA in adults with obesity in 2024 — the first weight-loss medication with this indication. SURMOUNT-OSA trial showed dramatic reduction in apnea-hypopnea index (AHI).

**Cardiovascular outcomes:** The SURPASS-CVOT trial is ongoing. The SELECT-equivalent for tirzepatide is not yet complete; CV outcomes approval expected in 2026–2027.

---

## Side Effects

**Very common (>10%):** Nausea (peaks during titration), diarrhea, constipation, vomiting, decreased appetite

**Common (1–10%):** Fatigue, abdominal pain, injection site reactions, GERD, headache, dizziness

**Reported lower GI side effects vs. semaglutide** in head-to-head comparisons. Tirzepatide is generally better tolerated, possibly due to the anti-nausea properties of GIP co-agonism.

**Serious (rare):** Acute pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, kidney injury (secondary to GI dehydration)

**Black box warning:** Same as semaglutide — thyroid C-cell tumor risk in rodents; contraindicated in MTC/MEN2 history.

---

## Contraindications

Identical to semaglutide:
- Personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or MEN2
- Hypersensitivity to tirzepatide
- Pregnancy
- Active pancreatitis

## Tirzepatide vs. Semaglutide Summary

| | Semaglutide | Tirzepatide |
|---|---|---|
| Weight loss | ~14–15% (SubQ) | ~20–22% |
| GI tolerance | Standard | Slightly better tolerated |
| CV outcomes data | Yes (SELECT trial, 2024) | Pending (SURPASS-CVOT) |
| OSA indication | No | Yes |
| Best evidence base | Larger (older) | Growing rapidly |
| Cost/insurance | More widely covered | Catching up fast |

**Bottom line:** If you want maximum weight loss from a currently FDA-approved drug, tirzepatide is the clear choice as of 2026.

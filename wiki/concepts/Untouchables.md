# Untouchables

> "The most important financial skill is controlling the goalpost." — Morgan Housel

## Definition

The **Untouchables** are the five things Morgan Housel identifies as never worth sacrificing for money or status:

1. **Reputation** — your character and integrity; once damaged, rarely fully repaired
2. **Freedom** — your autonomy over time and decisions; the thing money is ultimately supposed to buy you
3. **Family** — relationships with people who matter most; time lost cannot be recovered
4. **Friends** — genuine connection and community; money cannot buy authentic belonging
5. **Happiness** — subjective wellbeing; no amount of wealth compensates for a life that feels meaningless

## The Core Problem: Goalpost Movement

The psychological trap Housel describes is this: humans are remarkably good at **comparing upward**. No matter what level of wealth you reach, someone above you on the ladder makes your current position feel inadequate.

This produces an endless cycle where:
- You sacrifice one Untouchable (family time, health, integrity) to gain wealth
- The gain provides only brief satisfaction before the goalpost moves
- You've permanently traded something irreplaceable for something that didn't buy what you wanted

Housel uses the example of executives who committed financial fraud. They were already rich. They had "enough." But the comparison to even-wealthier peers made them feel behind, and they destroyed their freedom and reputation chasing a number they had no rational need to reach.

## "Enough" as a Discipline

Defining "enough" is not pessimism or settling — it is a rational, defensive act. The person who can clearly state "I have enough" is protected from the worst financial behaviors: excessive risk-taking, fraud, unethical shortcuts, and the sacrifice of Untouchables for incremental wealth.

The failure to define "enough" is itself a decision — and it defaults to "never enough."

## Connection to Financial Freedom

See [[Financial Freedom vs Wealth]]. The Untouchables framework explains *why* financial freedom (control of time) is more valuable than raw wealth accumulation: because freedom is itself an Untouchable. Once you have enough to be free, chasing more wealth beyond that threshold trades freedom for numbers.

## Connection to Emotional Agility

[[Emotional Agility]] (Susan David) describes the same trap in emotional terms: people who are "fused" with social comparison or extrinsic metrics lose contact with their actual values. The Untouchables are an explicit list of intrinsic values — the things that show up when you strip away performance and status.

## Application

- Before making any major financial/career trade-off, ask: "Am I trading an Untouchable for this?"
- Define your personal version of "enough" — write it down; revisit annually
- Notice goalpost-movement thoughts: "If I just had X, then I'd feel Y" — this is the trap in motion
- Status symbols that require sacrificing Untouchables are never worth it (see [[Wealth Paradox]])

# 21-Day Habit Formation System

A structured protocol for wiring new habits into the nervous system, developed by [[Andrew Huberman]] and grounded in the neuroscience of [[Neuroplasticity and Habit Formation|neuroplasticity]].

## Core Premise

Rather than trying to form one specific habit perfectly, the system builds the **meta-habit of doing habits** — making habit execution itself the practiced behavior. This dramatically increases the probability that specific habits stick.

## Protocol

### Phase A: Days 1–21 (Stimulus Period)

1. **List 6 target habits** you want to perform daily
2. **Expect to complete only 4–5 per day** — this is by design, not failure
3. **Do not attempt to do all 6 every day** — some habits (e.g., resistance training) shouldn't be performed daily anyway; having 6 allows natural shuffling
4. **No slip compensation** — if you miss a day and complete fewer than 4, do not add extra the next day. Trying to compensate ("habit slip compensation") is counterproductive
5. **Use the [[Three-Phase Day Framework]]** to place habits in their appropriate neurochemical windows

### Phase B: Days 22–42 (Assessment Period)

1. **Stop the deliberate 21-day schedule** — go on autopilot
2. **Assess which habits became reflexive**: How many of the 6 are you now doing automatically without planning them?
3. **Do not add new habits yet** — only after the previous 6 are fully automatic should new habits be introduced

### Phase C: Iteration

- Repeat the 21-day cycle to add new habits or reinforce ones that didn't consolidate
- The cycle is a **stimulus period** (wiring in) + **assessment period** (testing automaticity), not a continuous daily practice forever

## Why 21 Days

21 days is approximately the median of the 18–254 day range found by Lally et al. (2010) for habit formation. It is a meaningful stimulus period that initiates neuroplastic change, not a guarantee that habits are fully formed by day 22 — that is what Phase B tests.

## Built-In Permission to Fail

The 4–5 out of 6 expectation is intentional. It:
- Removes guilt from imperfect execution
- Accounts for the biological reality that some habits (recovery-dependent exercise) should not be daily
- Reduces the psychological pressure that causes people to abandon entire systems after one missed day

## Key Constraint

This system works best when combined with the [[Three-Phase Day Framework]] — placing hardest habits in Phase 1 ensures the neurochemical environment supports execution.

# Financial Order of Operations

> The 9-step sequence for allocating every dollar — ensures you never leave guaranteed returns on the table while building a comprehensive financial foundation.

*Framework popularized by Brian Preston and Bo Hanson (Money Guy Show). The sequence is not arbitrary — each step is ordered by guaranteed return, risk, and accessibility.*

---

## The 9 Steps (In Order)

### Step 1 — Cover Your Highest Insurance Deductible
Save enough cash to cover your highest deductible (health, auto, homeowner). Without this, one emergency wipes out any financial progress.

- Starter target: $1,000–$2,000 in savings
- Keep it liquid in a high-yield savings account (HYSA)
- This is your first layer of financial defense, not your full emergency fund

---

### Step 2 — Capture 100% of Employer 401(k) Match
If your employer offers any matching contribution, contribute enough to capture it all before doing anything else with your money.

**Why:** A 50% match is a guaranteed 50% instant return. No interest rate you carry, no investment you could make, will beat that. If your employer matches 50 cents per dollar up to 6%, contribute 6%.

---

### Step 3 — Pay Off High-Interest Debt
Define high-interest as **above 7%**. Typical culprits: credit cards (18–27%), personal loans (10–25%), payday loans (300%+).

**Strategy options:**
- **Avalanche** (mathematically optimal): attack highest interest rate first
- **Snowball** (psychologically effective): attack smallest balance first for wins

Either is fine. Pick the one you'll actually stick to.

---

### Step 4 — Build a 3–6 Month Emergency Fund
Grow savings to cover 3–6 months of essential expenses. Keep in a high-yield savings account.

| Situation | Recommended Size |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Stable salaried employment | 3 months |
| Variable income / freelance | 6 months |
| Self-employed / business owner | 9–12 months |
| Single income household | 6 months |

This fund protects your investments from forced liquidation during a crisis.

---

### Step 5 — Max Roth IRA + HSA
After high-interest debt is gone and emergency fund is complete, shift focus here before returning to the 401(k).

**Why Roth IRA before maxing 401(k):**
- Better tax treatment for young, lower-income earners (pay taxes now at low rate, withdraw tax-free at high rate)
- More flexible: contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn at any time without penalty
- More investment options than most 401(k) plans
- 2026 limits: **Roth IRA = $7,500** | **HSA = $4,300 single**

**HSA triple-tax advantage:** Deductible contributions → tax-free growth → tax-free withdrawals for healthcare. Best account in the tax code if you have a qualifying high-deductible health plan.

---

### Step 6 — Max Remaining 401(k) / 403(b)
After Roth IRA and HSA are maxed, fill the 401(k) up to the annual limit.

**2026 limit: $24,500** (employee contribution only)

Note: If your 401(k) offers a Roth 401(k) option, that can be preferable to traditional 401(k) for young workers in lower tax brackets.

---

### Step 7 — Hyper-Accumulation (25% of Gross Income)
At this stage, ensure you are saving at least 25% of your gross income across all vehicles.

If Steps 1–6 don't add up to 25% of gross income, put the difference into a taxable brokerage account (buy VTI/VXUS — same index funds as Roth IRA).

---

### Step 8 — Save for Abundance Goals
Medium-term financial goals that matter to you:
- Home down payment (20% to avoid PMI)
- Kids' education (529 plan)
- Starting a business
- Major purchase or life transition

These come after investment foundation is solid — not before.

---

### Step 9 — Pay Off Low-Interest Debt
Low-interest debt (student loans at 4–6%, mortgage at 3–7%) is last in the sequence. The math: dollars used to pay these down would have earned more in a diversified portfolio over a long time horizon.

**Exception:** If the psychological burden of debt is impairing your life and decisions, paying it off faster has a non-financial return that can justify the mathematical suboptimality.

---

## The Priority Matrix

```
              HIGH RETURN      |     LOW RETURN
              ────────────────────────────────
HIGH CERTAIN │ Employer match  │ High-interest debt
             │ (50-100%)       │ payoff (18-25%)
             ─────────────────────────────────
LOW CERTAIN  │ Roth IRA + index│ Low-interest debt
             │ funds (7-10%)   │ payoff (3-6%)
```

The sequence flows: guaranteed → high certain → investment → debt by rate → abundance.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Mistake**: Investing in taxable account before capturing 401k match → leaving guaranteed money on table
- **Mistake**: Maxing 401k before Roth IRA (for young low-income workers) → paying more tax than necessary
- **Mistake**: Building large investment account without emergency fund → forced to sell investments in a crisis at market lows
- **Mistake**: Paying off student loans aggressively before investing → missing compound growth during highest-leverage years

---

## Student / Part-Time Worker Simplified Version

If you have no employer, no 401(k), and limited income, the simplified FOO:

1. Cover your deductible ($500–$1,000 savings)
2. Pay off any credit card debt
3. Build $1,000–$2,000 emergency fund
4. Open Roth IRA; contribute any earned income (even $50/month)
5. Grow emergency fund to 3 months
6. Increase Roth IRA contributions as income rises

# Electrolyte Supplements

## What Electrolytes Are

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electrical charge when dissolved in water. The body uses them to regulate fluid balance, nerve signaling, muscle contraction, and pH. The six primary ones:

| Electrolyte | Primary Role | Lost in Sweat? |
|---|---|---|
| **Sodium** | Fluid balance, nerve impulse | Yes — highest volume |
| **Potassium** | Muscle contraction, heart rhythm | Yes — moderate |
| **Magnesium** | Energy metabolism, cramp prevention | Yes — low-moderate |
| **Chloride** | Fluid balance, stomach acid | Yes — paired with sodium |
| **Calcium** | Muscle contraction, bone density | Minimal |
| **Phosphate** | Energy (ATP), bone structure | Minimal |

---

## Do You Actually Need Them?

**For most people: No.** A balanced diet with fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean protein provides adequate electrolytes. Drinking plain water is sufficient for sedentary individuals and light exercisers.

**The evidence-based threshold for supplementation:**
- Exercise lasting **>60–75 minutes** at moderate-to-high intensity
- Hot/humid environment (sweat rate increases dramatically above 25°C)
- Heavy sweaters (individuals vary 3–4× in sweat rate)
- Low-carb/keto diet (see below)
- Illness causing vomiting or diarrhea
- High altitude or extended heat exposure

**Important caveat on overuse:** Global mean sodium intake is already 4.3g/day — more than **double** the WHO recommendation of 2g/day. Most people are not sodium-deficient. Excess electrolytes can cause heart rhythm problems, hypertension, nausea, fatigue, and muscle cramps.

---

## Signs of Electrolyte Deficiency

- Muscle cramps or twitching (especially at night)
- Headaches and brain fog
- Fatigue and dizziness
- Irregular heartbeat or palpitations
- Nausea and weakness
- Poor exercise performance

---

## Keto/Low-Carb Athletes: Higher Need

Low-carb diets lower insulin levels, which causes the kidneys to excrete more sodium and potassium. This is most acute in days 1–4 of keto. Combined with exercise sweat losses, athletes on keto face substantially elevated electrolyte needs.

**Keto-specific protocol:**
- Add 1,000–2,000 mg sodium/day above baseline
- Include potassium-rich foods (avocado, spinach)
- Supplement magnesium (magnesium malate preferred — less GI upset)

## Best Practices

### Timing
- **During exercise >60 min**: start sipping electrolyte drink at 20–30 min mark
- **Post-exercise**: within 30–60 min for recovery; pair with carbohydrates if >90 min training
- **Morning**: if training fasted or on keto, a serving before training prevents early cramps
- **Illness**: use oral rehydration solution (Pedialyte or equivalent) — clinically formulated ratio

### Dosing
- Don't exceed 100% DV for sodium from supplements on non-training days
- 1,000–2,000 mg sodium is the typical useful range for intense exercise
- **Water first**: electrolytes without adequate water don't help — shoot for 16–20 oz water/hour of exercise
- Start low; adjust based on sweat rate and symptoms

### What to Look For in a Product
1. **Third-party tested** — NSF Sport or Informed Sport certified
2. **No artificial sweeteners in excess** — stevia is acceptable; avoid sucralose if GI-sensitive
3. **Magnesium malate or glycinate** — better absorbed than magnesium oxide
4. **No fillers or proprietary blends** — dosages should be transparent on label
5. **Reasonable sodium range** — 300–1,000 mg per serving for athletic use

### DIY Option
- Add a pinch (1/8 tsp) of sea salt to water + squeeze of lemon + trace of honey = cheap homemade version
- Cost: ~$0.02/serving vs $1.00–$2.50 for commercial products

## DIY vs Supplement: When Each Makes Sense

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Sedentary / light exercise (<60 min) | Food + plain water; no supplement needed |
| Moderate exercise, cool conditions | DIY salt water or low-sodium option (Nuun) |
| Intense exercise >60 min, hot environment | LMNT or Re-Lyte |
| Keto/low-carb athlete | LMNT (high sodium) or Re-Lyte + extra potassium foods |
| Illness (vomiting/diarrhea) | Pedialyte — clinically validated ratio |
| NSF-certified competitive athlete | Thorne Hydration or Transparent Labs Hydrate |

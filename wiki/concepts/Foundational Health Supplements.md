# Foundational Health Supplements

Before any nootropic or performance supplement, address the basics. Deficiencies in foundational micronutrients impair cognition and health regardless of what else you take. These are not optional — they are the baseline.

> [!key-insight] Fix deficiencies first
> No nootropic will offset the cognitive drag of low vitamin D, poor sleep from low magnesium, or impaired membrane function from omega-3 deficiency. Foundation first.

---

## Vitamin D3

**Why it matters:** 36% of Americans aged 18–29 are vitamin D deficient, the highest rate of any age group. Vitamin D receptors are present throughout the brain. Deficiency is associated with impaired cognitive performance and mood.

**Evidence quality for cognition:** Medium. Associations are strong; RCTs show inconsistent results (most done in elderly). Cognitive benefit from supplementing in a young deficient male is plausible but not definitively proven.

**Evidence for general health:** High. Immune function, bone health, testosterone production.

**Target blood level:** ~79.9 nmol/L (32 ng/mL) — optimal per NHANES analysis; above this level no additional cognitive association detected.

**Dosage:**
- If deficient: 2,000–4,000 IU/day until replete
- Maintenance: 1,000–2,000 IU/day with vitamin K2 (MK-7, 100mcg) to direct calcium to bones
- Upper safe limit: 4,000 IU/day for normal-weight adults (Polish guidelines 2023)

**Form:** D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2.

**Testing:** Get a 25-OH vitamin D blood test before supplementing. Blind supplementation is less efficient than targeted correction.

## Magnesium

**Why it matters:** ~50% of Americans do not meet the RDA for magnesium from diet. Magnesium is a cofactor in 300+ enzymatic reactions, including ATP synthesis — relevant for anyone studying intensively.

**Evidence quality for sleep:** Medium. RCTs show improvements in sleep onset, deep sleep, and cortisol reduction, especially in those with low baseline magnesium. Strongest evidence in deficient populations.

**Evidence quality for cognition:** Medium. Magnesium L-threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier more effectively; RCT in healthy adults (18–45) showed improvement in working and episodic memory and a 7.5-year reduction in estimated cognitive brain age.

**Dosage:**
- Magnesium glycinate: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium at night (most bioavailable, gentle on digestion)
- Magnesium L-threonate (Magtein®): 1,500–2,000 mg/day if prioritizing brain specifically (more expensive)
- Avoid magnesium oxide — poorly absorbed

**Timing:** Evening / before sleep — supports relaxation and sleep quality.

## Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA + DHA)

**Why it matters:** DHA is a structural component of neuronal cell membranes (~97% of omega-3 in the brain is DHA). EPA has anti-inflammatory effects relevant to stress and mood.

**Evidence quality:** Medium. RCTs in young adults show DHA (1.16 g/day × 6 months) improved episodic memory in women and working memory reaction time in men. Fish oil at 2,800 mg/day (1,680 mg EPA + 1,120 mg DHA × 35 days) stabilized mood markers under stress.

**Dosage:**
- Target: 1,000–2,000 mg combined EPA+DHA per day from fish oil or algal oil
- Algal oil is the vegan source — same DHA, better for sustainability
- Check the label: grams of fish oil ≠ grams of EPA+DHA

**Timing:** With food (fat-soluble, better absorbed with a meal).

## Zinc

**Why it matters:** Zinc is critical for testosterone synthesis, immune function, and neurotransmitter regulation. Young males with high sweat output (exercise) can become mildly deficient.

**Evidence quality:** Medium for general health. Limited direct cognitive RCTs in healthy young males.

**Dosage:** 15–30 mg/day. **Do not exceed 40 mg/day** — high-dose zinc depletes copper. Take with food (reduces nausea). If supplementing long-term at 25+ mg: add 1–2 mg copper to prevent deficiency.

**Note:** Get levels tested if possible. Supplementing when already replete provides minimal benefit.

---

## Priority Order

1. **Vitamin D3 + K2** — highest deficiency prevalence in this demographic
2. **Magnesium glycinate** — second most common deficit; immediate sleep and stress benefits
3. **Omega-3** — foundational for brain membrane health
4. **Zinc** — only if dietary intake is low (common in low meat/seafood diets)

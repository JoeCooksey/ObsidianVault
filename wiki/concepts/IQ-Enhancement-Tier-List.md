# IQ Enhancement Tier List

> Evidence-based ranking of all major interventions for improving cognitive intelligence. Organized by effect size, research quality, and practical ROI. See [[EQ-IQ-Enhancement-Guide]] for the master framework and [[Combined-EQ-IQ-Tier-List]] for dual-benefit activities.

---

## How to Read This Tier List

**Criteria for placement:**
- **Effect size**: How large is the cognitive benefit? (Small ≈ 0.2 SD; Medium ≈ 0.5 SD; Large ≈ 0.8 SD)
- **Evidence quality**: RCT meta-analyses > individual RCTs > observational studies > mechanistic theory
- **Transfer**: Does the gain apply to general cognition or only to the trained task?
- **Practicality**: Cost, time requirement, accessibility

**IQ is two-headed.** Fluid intelligence (Gf) — novel problem-solving — is hard to move. Crystallized intelligence (Gc) — accumulated knowledge — grows reliably and accumulates indefinitely. Most high-impact interventions affect Gc; a few move Gf modestly.

## S-Tier — Highest Impact, Best Evidence

### 1. Formal Education and Deliberate Challenging Study

**What it is:** Sustained engagement with structured, progressively difficult material — coursework, textbooks, technical problem sets.

**Evidence:** The Flynn Effect (3 IQ points per decade across the 20th century) is attributed substantially to expanded formal education and exposure to abstract problem-solving. Studies of agrarian communities with variable schooling show that each additional year of schooling produces ~1–5 IQ points of measurable gain. Formal schooling disproportionately boosts fluid reasoning on nonverbal tests (Raven's Progressive Matrices gains of 5–6 pts/decade vs. 0–2 pts/decade on crystallized verbal measures).

**Mechanism:** Schooling trains the meta-skills tested by IQ: abstract categorization, hypothesis testing, working backward from goals. Neuroimaging shows formal numeracy education shapes prefrontal cortex maturation — the same substrate as fluid IQ.

**Expected gains:** Large for Gc (indefinite accumulation); small-moderate for Gf (especially with novel problem-solving emphasis).

**Prescription:** Engage at the edge of current ability. Reading textbooks at subject level +1 beyond current competence, working problem sets that require genuine struggle, and teaching material you've learned all produce stronger gains than passive review.

### 2. Sleep Optimization

**What it is:** Achieving consistent 7–9 hours of high-quality sleep with good sleep architecture (adequate SWS + REM).

**Evidence:** 24 hours of sleep deprivation temporarily reduces functional IQ by 10–15 points, primarily through impaired working memory and processing speed (Frontiers in Sleep, 2025). SWS (slow-wave sleep) consolidates declarative memories via sleep spindles — short neural oscillations that replay and stabilize learning from the prior day. Sleep spindle density correlates significantly with IQ scores. Chronic mild sleep restriction (6h/night) accumulates cognitive deficits equivalent to total sleep deprivation without subjective awareness.

**Mechanism:** Sleep serves three IQ-relevant functions: (1) memory consolidation — hippocampus-to-cortex transfer during SWS; (2) synaptic homeostasis — pruning weak connections to sharpen neural signal-to-noise; (3) metabolic clearance — glymphatic system removes cognitive waste products (amyloid, tau) during sleep.

**Expected gains:** Correcting chronic sleep deficit can recover 5–15 IQ points of functional capacity. Optimal sleep is a prerequisite, not a performance hack.

**Prescription:**
- Fixed wake time daily (anchors circadian rhythm)
- 7.5–9h in bed (allows full 4–5 sleep cycles)
- Dark, cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C) room
- No caffeine after 2 PM (100 mg caffeine half-life = 5–7h)
- Avoid blue light 1h before bed
- See [[Caffeine Timing Protocol]] and [[Sleep Optimization Supplements]]

### 3. Aerobic Exercise (BDNF-Mediated)

**What it is:** Regular moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise, particularly sustained Zone 2 or interval work.

**Evidence:** A meta-analytic review (PMC4314337) found aerobic exercise reliably elevates BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) by 200–300% above baseline during and immediately after exercise. BDNF promotes neurogenesis in the hippocampus, synaptogenesis, and long-term potentiation — the cellular basis of learning and memory. A 2024 systematic review (PMC10932589) confirmed beneficial effects on executive functions, working memory, and processing speed. Children in aerobic exercise interventions show measurable IQ gains; older adults show protection against cognitive decline.

**Mechanism:** Aerobic exercise → elevated BDNF → hippocampal neurogenesis → improved episodic memory capacity → better learning rate. Secondary: improved cerebrovascular health (increased gray matter volume, better oxygen delivery), reduced neuroinflammation, improved stress resilience via HPA axis regulation.

**Expected gains:** Small-moderate (equivalent to 3–5 IQ points on working memory and Gf measures in adults). Larger effects in children and cognitively impaired populations. Acute benefit (post-exercise cognitive boost) is separate from the long-term structural benefit.

**Prescription:**
- 150+ min/week Zone 2 cardio (60–70% max HR)
- 2–3 HIIT sessions (sprint intervals, VO2 max intervals) for peak BDNF spike
- Exercise in the morning to maximize BDNF during peak learning hours
- Joe already does this at S-tier volume (6×/week cycling) — continue

### 4. Dual N-Back Training

**What it is:** A demanding working-memory task that requires tracking two simultaneous streams of stimuli (position + sound) N steps back in time.

**Evidence:** The original Jaeggi et al. (2008) PNAS study showed fluid intelligence gains from n-back training, triggering a decade of research. The consensus from meta-analyses is nuanced: real but modest effects (~2–4 IQ points on Gf measures). Gwern's comprehensive meta-analysis of 23+ studies found an aggregate effect equivalent to 3–4 IQ points. Studies with active control groups (doing another cognitively demanding task) show smaller effects than those with passive controls, suggesting some motivational component to gains. However, the dual n-back shows stronger transfer to fluid intelligence than other working-memory tasks.

**Mechanism:** Dual n-back trains the fronto-parietal network (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex + parietal cortex) — the same network that underlies fluid reasoning on IQ tests. Neuroimaging shows structural changes in these regions after training.

**Limitations:** Transfer to real-world performance is debated. Gains may reflect improved ability to take IQ tests rather than generalized cognitive improvement. Placebo effects may account for some reported gains (PNAS placebo study, 2016).

**Expected gains:** Small (~2–4 IQ points Gf). Primarily benefits working memory and attention. Best ROI for someone who wants to specifically target fluid intelligence.

**Prescription:**
- 20–25 sessions over 4–8 weeks (diminishing returns after this)
- Use dual-nback.com or Brain Workshop (free)
- Start at N=2, advance only when accuracy > 85%
- Combine with sleep and exercise for maximum consolidation

## A-Tier — Meaningful Gains, Strong Evidence

### 5. Learning a New Cognitively Demanding Skill

**What it is:** Acquiring a genuinely new domain of knowledge or skill — a new language, musical instrument, programming language, or complex game.

**Evidence:** Learning new skills builds crystallized intelligence (indefinite compounding), and the process of initial acquisition exercises fluid intelligence. Language learning specifically increases gray matter density in the hippocampus and temporal cortex. Musical training in children produces measurable gains in IQ of approximately 7 points on average (Schellenberg meta-analysis). New skill acquisition forces the brain out of automated pattern recognition into genuine problem-solving mode — the state that exercises Gf.

**Expected gains:** Large for Gc in the learned domain; small-moderate for Gf during active acquisition phase.

**Prescription:** Choose domains with high transfer value: a second programming language, a foreign language, chess, music theory, or mathematics. Deliberate practice (focused effort at the edge of ability) is required — passive exposure does not produce gains.

---

### 6. Reading Challenging Material

**What it is:** Active reading of difficult nonfiction, technical texts, literary fiction, and philosophy — material that requires genuine comprehension effort.

**Evidence:** Vocabulary size is the single strongest predictor of crystallized intelligence and correlates substantially with overall IQ (r ≈ 0.70–0.80 with verbal IQ). Each new word learned represents a stored conceptual node; a larger vocabulary allows faster encoding of new information. Technical reading (textbooks, scientific papers) builds domain-specific Gc and forces abstract reasoning. Literary fiction additionally builds theory-of-mind and empathy circuits (see [[EQ-Enhancement-Tier-List]]).

**Expected gains:** Large for Gc (vocabulary, world knowledge, conceptual fluency); small for Gf.

**Prescription:**
- Read above your current comfort level — use dictionaries actively
- Engage with primary sources, not just summaries
- Active reading (annotations, questions in margins, re-reading difficult passages) > passive skimming
- Joe's Phase 1–3 reading lists are well-calibrated here

### 7. Chess and Strategy Games

**What it is:** Competitive chess play combined with deliberate study (tactics puzzles, endgame training, game analysis).

**Evidence:** A meta-analysis found a significant positive correlation (r = 0.35) between full-scale IQ and chess rating. Chess instruction studies in children report IQ gains averaging ~10 points after 4.5 months of instruction, though most lack active control groups. The cognitive skills exercised include working memory (holding the board state), pattern recognition (stored tactical motifs), planning ahead, and hypothesis testing — all Gf-adjacent. A PMC review found chess instruction effects on cognitive skills and academic performance.

**Caveat:** The IQ-chess correlation weakens substantially at high skill levels (r ≈ 0.11–0.14 among grandmasters), suggesting elite chess relies more on domain-specific pattern libraries than raw intelligence. For developing players, chess genuinely exercises Gf.

**Expected gains:** Small-moderate (especially in youth and novice-intermediate players). The cognitive benefit is in the *playing and studying*, not the ranking.

**Prescription:**
- 15–30 min daily tactical puzzles (chess.com or Lichess) more cognitively valuable than blitz games
- Study your own games for mistakes (metacognitive element)
- Use Lichess (free) or Chess.com
- See [[Video Games Cognitive Benefits]] for comparative ranking

### 8. Meditation and Mindfulness

**What it is:** Sustained attention training through formal mindfulness meditation practice.

**Evidence:** A meta-analysis of 111 RCTs (PMC10902202) found small-to-moderate significant effects of mindfulness-based interventions on global cognition, working memory accuracy, sustained attention, and executive function. A 2025 meta-analysis on working memory specifically found a medium effect size (d ≈ 0.5) across 29 studies. The military mindfulness study found that 8 weeks of mindfulness training maintained working memory capacity during high-stress predeployment periods while non-meditators declined. Structural brain changes require ~8 weeks of consistent practice; functional benefits in attention appear earlier.

**Mechanism:** Mindfulness trains the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and prefrontal cortex — both central to working memory and attentional control. Reduces default mode network (mind-wandering) activation, which competes with focused cognitive work.

**Expected gains:** Small-moderate for working memory and attention (Gf-adjacent). The attention benefits directly translate to improved learning efficiency.

**Prescription:**
- 10–20 min/day of focused attention meditation (breath as object)
- 8-week commitment minimum before assessing gains
- Apps: Waking Up (Sam Harris) or Headspace for structure; then drop app when practice is established
- Insight Timer has free guided meditations for all levels

## B-Tier — Supporting Gains, Good Evidence

### 9. Nutrition — Omega-3, Iron, Iodine, Glucose Stability

**What it is:** Ensuring adequate levels of key cognitive nutrients through diet and supplementation.

**Evidence:**
- **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA):** A meta-analysis (Scientific Reports, 2025) found each 2,000 mg/day omega-3 supplementation produced significant improvement in attention and perceptual speed. Dietary omega-3 from fish associated with higher IQ in adolescents (Swedish cohort, IQ measured at 18). DHA is the dominant structural fatty acid in the brain — adequate levels support synaptic membrane fluidity and neurotransmission.
- **Iron:** Iron deficiency (even without overt anemia) measurably suppresses working memory and attention in children and adults. Common in young women and athletes.
- **Iodine:** Severe iodine deficiency reduces IQ by 10–15 points on population level. Subclinical deficiency produces smaller but real deficits.
- **Glucose stability:** Volatile blood sugar (spikes and crashes) impairs sustained cognitive performance. Stable glucose (from low-GI foods, regular meals, exercise) supports consistent cognitive function.
- **Creatine:** A 2024 EFSA review found insufficient evidence for a health claim on cognitive improvement. Earlier positive studies (backward digit span, Raven's matrices) not reliably replicated in recent large RCTs. However, creatine supplementation during sleep deprivation shows acute cognitive benefit. See [[Creatine for Cognitive Performance]].

**Expected gains:** Correcting deficiencies produces small-moderate gains. Supplementing above adequate baseline produces minimal additional benefit.

**Prescription:**
- 2–3g EPA+DHA/day from fish oil or fatty fish
- Test ferritin (iron stores), not just hemoglobin
- Use iodized salt or seaweed as iodine source
- Creatine 3–5g/day has sufficient other evidence (muscle) to justify continuing regardless

### 10. Spaced Repetition and Active Recall

**What it is:** Using spaced repetition systems (SRS) like Anki combined with active retrieval practice to maximize retention of learned material.

**Evidence:** The testing effect (retrieval practice effect) is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology — actively recalling information strengthens memory 2–3× more than re-reading the same amount. Spaced repetition leverages the forgetting curve to schedule reviews at optimal intervals. Wozniak's SuperMemo algorithm (which Anki implements) produces retention rates of 90%+ with a fraction of the review time of massed practice.

**Mechanism:** Retrieval practice strengthens memory traces through reconsolidation. Each retrieval attempt forces active reconstruction of the memory, adding new associative cues and deepening encoding. This is how deliberate study translates into crystallized intelligence.

**Expected gains:** Large for retention and Gc. Effective for anyone acquiring large bodies of domain knowledge (medical students see the clearest gains). Secondary benefit: reduces cognitive load in expert practice by automating foundational knowledge, freeing working memory for complex reasoning.

**Prescription:**
- Anki (free, cross-platform) is the gold standard
- Add cards while studying, not after
- Keep cards atomic (one fact per card)
- Do daily reviews — skipping creates exponential debt

## C-Tier — Limited Transfer, Moderate Evidence

### 11. Brain Training Apps (Lumosity, Elevate, etc.)

**What it is:** Gamified cognitive exercises targeting specific abilities (working memory, processing speed, attention).

**Evidence:** Lumosity paid $2M to the FTC in 2016 for false advertising claims. A 2021 systematic review found that commercially available brain training programs show small but significant near-transfer effects — improvement on tasks similar to the training tasks — but limited far transfer to general IQ or real-world cognitive performance. BrainHQ has substantially better evidence (300+ peer-reviewed studies; ACTIVE trial 20-year follow-up showed speed-of-processing training reduced dementia risk). Most programs' benefits are primarily "you get better at those specific games."

**Exception:** BrainHQ's Double Decision exercise has the strongest far-transfer evidence of any brain training program.

**Expected gains:** Small (mostly near-transfer). Better than nothing, worse than real cognitive exercise.

**Prescription:** If using, choose BrainHQ over Lumosity. Treat as supplementary, never primary. 15–20 min/day maximum.

---

### 12. Crossword Puzzles and General Trivia

**What it is:** Engaging with word puzzles, trivia, and knowledge games.

**Evidence:** Crosswords build vocabulary and crystallized knowledge but involve high pattern repetition once you know common puzzle conventions. Very limited evidence of far transfer. Primarily a maintenance activity for existing Gc rather than a builder of new cognitive capacity.

**Expected gains:** Minimal (maintenance, not growth).

## D-Tier — Weak Evidence, Small Effects

### 13. Nootropic Supplements (Beyond Core Stack)

**What it is:** Supplements specifically marketed for cognitive enhancement beyond the foundational stack.

**Evidence:**
- **Caffeine + L-theanine:** Well-evidenced for acute cognitive performance (see [[Caffeine and L-Theanine Stack]]). Tolerance develops rapidly. No long-term IQ gain — a performance tool, not a builder.
- **Bacopa monnieri:** 4–12 weeks of supplementation shows modest memory improvements, particularly in older adults. Effect size small (d ≈ 0.25). See [[Bacopa Monnieri]].
- **Lion's Mane:** NGF stimulation hypothesis. Thin human evidence. Preliminary signal in older adults. See [[Lion's Mane Mushroom]].
- **Racetams (piracetam, aniracetam):** Weak human evidence; some benefits in cognitively impaired populations; minimal evidence in healthy young adults.
- **Modafinil:** Promotes wakefulness and reduces fatigue effects on cognition; not a true IQ enhancer; prescription only.

**Prescription:** Fix foundational nutrition first. The core stack (D3+K2, omega-3, magnesium, creatine, zinc) addresses deficiencies and supports the floor. Additional nootropics add little on top of a solid foundation, adequate sleep, and regular exercise.

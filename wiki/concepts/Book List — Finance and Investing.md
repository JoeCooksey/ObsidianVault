# Book List — Finance and Investing

> Tiered reading list for building real financial intelligence. Sequence matters: mindset before mechanics, behavior before strategy.

## S-Tier — Read These First

### The Intelligent Investor — Benjamin Graham (1949, revised 2003)
- Warren Buffett's single most recommended book; called it "by far the best book on investing ever written"
- Core thesis: margin of safety + Mr. Market analogy; price ≠ value
- Read chapters 1, 8 (Mr. Market), and 20 (Margin of Safety) at minimum
- Jason Zweig commentary added in 2003 edition is essential — connects Graham to modern context

### The Psychology of Money — Morgan Housel (2020)
- **The single best starting point** for anyone in their 20s
- 19 short essays on how people think about money — why behavior matters more than intelligence
- Key idea: wealth is what you don't spend; reasonable > rational; long tails of luck and risk dominate outcomes
- No formulas or complex theory — pure mental model installation

### The Essays of Warren Buffett — Lawrence Cunningham (compiler)
- Berkshire Hathaway annual letters organized by theme
- Business quality, management character, capital allocation thinking at its most distilled

## A-Tier — Read Within 12 Months

### Just Keep Buying — Nick Maggiulli (2022)
- Data-driven personal finance; debunks common myths (lump sum vs. DCA, paying off debt vs. investing)
- Key finding: if you can invest, invest now; time in market > timing the market
- Especially relevant for people starting with little capital (saves first, invests as income grows)

### I Will Teach You to Be Rich — Ramit Sethi (2019)
- Designed specifically for 20-somethings; covers credit, bank accounts, investing accounts, automation
- 6-week setup plan; automate everything so you never have to think about it again
- Most practical implementation guide for someone just starting out

### Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order — Ray Dalio (2021)
- 500-year macro lens on how empires rise and fall; maps to current US/China cycle
- Useful for understanding why portfolio diversification matters at the macro level
- Dense — use the animated YouTube summary first, then read the book

### The Little Book of Common Sense Investing — John Bogle (2007)
- Mathematical case for low-cost index funds over active management
- Core argument: in aggregate, investors earn market returns minus costs; minimize costs, maximize holding period
- Short, rigorous, and the philosophical foundation of the entire passive investing movement

### One Up On Wall Street — Peter Lynch (1989)
- How retail investors can identify great companies before Wall Street does (shop at the mall, observe your life)
- Investment frameworks: PEG ratio, 6 categories of stocks, when to sell
- Still the best argument for the individual investor having an edge in small-cap consumer companies

## B-Tier — Good, Read When Ready

| Book | Author | Key Idea |
|------|--------|----------|
| *Rich Dad Poor Dad* | Robert Kiyosaki | Assets vs. liabilities mindset; financial literacy gap between rich and middle class |
| *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* | Burton Malkiel | Efficient markets; historical data on stocks vs. alternatives; index fund endorsement |
| *The Millionaire Next Door* | Stanley & Danko | Empirical: actual millionaires are boring, frugal, un-flashy; wealth ≠ income |
| *The Little Book That Still Beats the Market* | Joel Greenblatt | "Magic formula" — earnings yield + return on capital screen |
| *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* | Philip Fisher | Scuttlebutt method; qualitative research on business quality; Buffett's other hero |

## Core Mental Models from This Category

- **Margin of Safety**: only buy assets trading significantly below intrinsic value — protects against being wrong
- **Mr. Market**: the market is a manic-depressive partner offering you prices daily; you don't have to trade
- **Compounding**: the 8th wonder of the world; $1 at 10%/year for 30 years = $17.45; time is the multiplier
- **Behavior > Intelligence**: the investor's greatest enemy is themselves; emotions cause buy high / sell low
- **Wealth = Unseen**: wealth is the money you didn't spend; the Rolex and Ferrari owners often have less net worth than the quiet index-fund millionaires

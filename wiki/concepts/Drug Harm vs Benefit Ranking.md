# Drug Harm vs Benefit Ranking

Composite ranking of psychoactive substances by **net harm-to-benefit ratio**, drawing on the Nutt/ISCD 2010 multicriteria harm analysis (*The Lancet*) and current therapeutic evidence. Two axes matter: **harm** (self + society) and **benefit** (medical/therapeutic value). Tier placement reflects the ratio, not just one dimension.

## Harm Scoring Framework (Nutt 2010 — ISCD)

Scored out of 100 across **16 criteria** (9 individual harms + 7 societal harms) by independent drug experts. Higher = more harmful.

## Harm-to-Benefit Tier List

### S-Tier — High Benefit, Low Harm (Best Net Ratio)

**Psilocybin (mushrooms)** — harm score 5 (lowest of all 20 drugs)
- Strongest therapeutic potential per unit of harm
- >50% depression remission at 6 months in RCTs; FDA breakthrough designation for MDD
- Effective for treatment-resistant addiction (alcohol, smoking), cluster headaches, end-of-life anxiety
- Not physically addictive; physiologically very safe; no known lethal dose in humans
- Currently Schedule I in the US; growing number of state-level decriminalizations
- *Caveat: rare risk of triggering psychotic episodes in predisposed individuals*

**Buprenorphine** — harm score 6
- Gold standard for opioid use disorder (OUD); dramatically reduces overdose deaths, HIV transmission, crime
- Shown to reduce overall opioid-related mortality by 50%+ in treated populations
- Partial opioid agonist — acts as a ceiling, preventing respiratory depression overdose
- Low recreational appeal relative to full agonists; ceiling effect limits misuse
- FDA-approved; widely used in medication-assisted treatment (MAT)

---

### A-Tier — Strong Benefit, Moderate Harm

**MDMA/Ecstasy** — harm score 7
- 71% of PTSD patients no longer met diagnostic criteria after MDMA-assisted psychotherapy (Phase 3)
- FDA declined approval in 2024 pending more research, but breakthrough therapy status maintained
- Primary risks: hyperthermia, hyponatremia (almost entirely from uncontrolled recreational contexts); rare cardiac risk
- In therapeutic dosing, physical harm is low; psychological risk is real but manageable
- Strongest single therapeutic signal of any unapproved compound for PTSD

**LSD** — harm score 7
- Emerging evidence for cluster headaches (dramatic response even at sub-hallucinogenic doses), treatment-resistant depression, anxiety
- Very low physical toxicity; not physiologically addictive
- Psychological risk (ego dissolution, anxiety in bad set/setting) is real but manageable
- Research is earlier-stage than psilocybin or MDMA

**Ketamine / Esketamine** — harm score 15
- Only rapid-acting antidepressant — works within hours, not weeks; FDA-approved (Spravato/esketamine) for TRD
- 86% abstinence rate for alcohol use disorder in Phase 3 trials
- Proven for treatment-resistant depression where SSRIs fail
- Harm: dissociation at high doses; bladder damage with chronic heavy recreational use; abuse potential
- Moderate harm score but high benefit ceiling, especially for otherwise untreatable cases

**Amphetamines / Methylphenidate** — (not in Nutt's 20; amphetamine harm ~23)
- Transformative for ADHD — large effect sizes; quality of life improvement well-documented
- Narcolepsy treatment (scheduled stimulants are first-line)
- Moderate recreational harm; misuse and cardiovascular risk at high doses
- Benefit-harm ratio is excellent when medically supervised

**Methadone** — harm score 14
- Highly effective opioid substitution therapy; used when buprenorphine is insufficient
- Reduces mortality, disease transmission, criminal activity in OUD
- Harm: narrow therapeutic window; dangerous in overdose; long QT cardiac risk; significant abuse potential
- Net positive in therapeutic context; net very negative recreationally

### B-Tier — Meaningful Benefit, Significant Harm (Context-Dependent)

**Cannabis (CBD-dominant / medical)** — harm score 20
- Epidiolex (CBD) FDA-approved for Dravet syndrome and Lennox-Gastaut syndrome (pediatric epilepsy) — dramatic seizure reduction
- Strong evidence for chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting
- Limited but real evidence for chronic pain (especially neuropathic), spasticity in MS
- THC-dominant cannabis: ~30% risk of cannabis use disorder with heavy use; daily use doubles coronary heart disease risk; evidence for pain/sleep/anxiety is surprisingly weak (2025 systematic review)
- Harm from high-THC recreational use is moderate; harm from CBD is low

**Benzodiazepines** — harm score 15
- Genuinely valuable for acute anxiety, panic disorder, seizure management, alcohol withdrawal
- Very high benefit in correct short-term context (alcohol withdrawal without benzos can be fatal)
- Significant addiction liability; dangerous in overdose with alcohol or opioids; cognitive impairment with chronic use
- Net positive when prescribed appropriately; net negative with long-term casual use

**GHB / Sodium oxybate** — harm score 19
- Sodium oxybate (Xyrem) FDA-approved for narcolepsy with cataplexy — rare but devastating condition
- Dramatically improves quality of life for narcolepsy patients
- Very narrow therapeutic window; dangerous with alcohol; significant abuse and "date rape drug" history
- Harm is high in recreational context; justified medically only for narcolepsy

### C-Tier — Moderate Benefit, Very High Harm (Net Negative)

**Cocaine** — harm score 27
- Medical use: topical local anesthetic/vasoconstrictor in ENT and ophthalmic surgery; still used in some procedures
- Recreational use: extremely high addiction liability; cardiac events (MI, arrhythmia); stroke; neurotoxicity
- The legitimate medical benefit is narrow and replaced largely by safer alternatives (lidocaine + epinephrine)

**Heroin / Diamorphine** — harm score 55
- Pharmaceutical diamorphine is used in UK palliative pain care and, controversially, in heroin-assisted treatment (HAT) for intractable OUD — HAT shows real efficacy in patients who failed other treatments
- Otherwise: catastrophic addiction, overdose, disease transmission, social destruction
- The rare medical/HAT use doesn't offset recreational harm at the societal level

**Methamphetamine** — harm score 33
- Desoxyn (methamphetamine HCl) is FDA-approved for obesity and ADHD — almost never prescribed; safe alternatives exist
- Recreational meth causes severe neurotoxicity, dental destruction ("meth mouth"), psychosis, cardiovascular damage
- Negligible real-world therapeutic benefit vs. massive societal harm

---

### F-Tier — Very High Harm, Negligible Benefit

**Alcohol** — harm score 72 (highest of all 20 drugs)
- Highest societal harm of any drug; third leading preventable cause of death globally
- Some historical studies suggest light drinking protects against CVD — WHO now states there is **no safe level of alcohol** for health
- Responsible for enormous crime, domestic violence, accidents, liver disease, cancer (Group 1 carcinogen: oral, pharyngeal, esophageal, colorectal, breast cancer)
- Addictive withdrawal is medically dangerous (unlike most illegal drugs)
- Culturally normalized despite being arguably the most harmful substance in the ranking

**Tobacco / Nicotine** — harm score 26
- Zero therapeutic benefit; nicotine patches and gum are cessation tools, not sources of benefit
- Tobacco kills ~8 million people per year globally; single largest preventable cause of death
- Causes lung, oral, throat, esophageal, bladder, pancreatic, cervical cancers; COPD; heart disease
- Nicotine itself is highly addictive with moderate independent cardiovascular risk
- No acceptable harm-to-benefit ratio under any framing

**Crack cocaine** — harm score 54
- No medical use; more rapidly addictive than powder cocaine due to faster onset
- Catastrophic community-level harm; associated with crime, violence, family destruction
- No therapeutic dimension

## Key Insights

1. **Legal status ≠ harm level.** Alcohol (harm 72) and tobacco (harm 26) are legal; psilocybin (harm 5) and LSD (harm 7) are Schedule I. Legal status reflects politics, not pharmacology.

2. **The most therapeutically promising drugs are mostly Schedule I.** Psilocybin, MDMA, and LSD — the bottom three in harm score — have among the strongest emerging therapeutic profiles.

3. **Harm to self ≠ harm to others.** Heroin, crack, and meth are most harmful *to the individual*. Alcohol is uniquely most harmful *to others* (violence, accidents, family destruction) — which is why it ranks #1 overall.

4. **Context determines almost everything.** Heroin in a palliative ward is morphine. MDMA in a clinical PTSD protocol is therapy. The same molecule has radically different risk profiles depending on context, dose, purity, and setting.

5. **Addiction liability is a separate axis from acute toxicity.** Tobacco scores low on acute harm but is extremely addictive and deadly cumulatively. Cannabis is relatively benign acutely but ~30% of heavy users develop use disorder.

6. **Therapeutic benefit does not neutralize recreational harm.** Cocaine is still a surgical anesthetic. Heroin is still used in UK palliative care. The societal harm of recreational use vastly exceeds the benefits from rare medical use for C-tier drugs.

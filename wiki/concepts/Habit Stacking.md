# Habit Stacking

A technique for anchoring a new habit to an existing one by using the completion of a current behavior as the cue for the new behavior. A specialized form of [[Implementation Intention]].

## Formula

> **"After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."**

Examples:
- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will open my textbook."
- "After I sit down at my desk, I will write the first item on my to-do list."
- "After I brush my teeth at night, I will do 5 minutes of stretching."
- "After I take off my shoes from cycling, I will write in my training log."

## Why It Works

The brain already has a well-worn neural pathway for the existing habit. The cue for that habit fires reliably. By appending a new behavior to the end of that sequence, you *borrow* the trigger — no new cue needs to be installed.

This is sometimes called a **behavioral scaffold**: the new habit is supported by the existing structure of the old one.

From the [[Habit Loop]] perspective:
- The reward of Habit A becomes the **cue** for Habit B
- After enough repetitions, the AB sequence fuses into a single behavioral unit

## Stacking Chains

Multiple habits can be stacked into a sequence:

```
Wake up
  → (after waking) brew coffee
  → (after brewing) 5 minutes journaling
  → (after journaling) open study material
  → (after study session) 10-minute walk
```

Each link in the chain reinforces the next. Morning routines are the classic application.

## Design Rules

1. **Match the frequency**: the anchor habit must occur at least as frequently as the new habit is intended
2. **Match the context**: the anchor habit and new habit should occur in the same location or flow naturally
3. **Start small**: use the [[Two-Minute Rule]] for the new habit; the stack should be easy to complete even on low-energy days
4. **Be specific**: "After breakfast" is better than "In the morning." "After I sit down at my desk" is better than "After breakfast."

## Habit Stacking vs. Clock-Based Implementation Intention

| | Implementation Intention | Habit Stacking |
|---|---|---|
| Anchor | Clock time + location | Existing habit completion |
| Strength | Requires remembering time | Leverages existing neural trigger |
| Flexibility | Fixed time | Shifts with life schedule |
| Best for | Time-sensitive tasks | Habits without fixed timing |

Both are [[Four Laws of Behavior Change]] — Law 1 (Make It Obvious) tools.

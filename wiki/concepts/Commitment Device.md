# Commitment Device

A choice made **in the present** that locks in better behavior in the future by removing the optionality to choose badly in the moment.

Also called a **Ulysses Contract** (named after Odysseus binding himself to the mast to resist the Sirens — he made a present decision that constrained his future self).

## Core Mechanism

Humans are inconsistent across time: the self that makes a calm, rational decision at 9 AM is not the same self that faces temptation at 11 PM. Commitment devices exploit this by having the rational self pre-decide for the impulsive self.

> "The best way to improve your future behavior is to automate your present self's decisions."

From the [[Habit Loop]] perspective, commitment devices operate on the **Response** and **Reward** stages — they either make the bad response impossible, or make it immediately costly.

## Three Categories

### 1. Friction-Adding Devices
Make the bad habit harder or impossible to perform.
- **Examples**: website blockers (Freedom, Cold Turkey) with scheduled locks; putting the TV in a closet; leaving the phone in another room; keeping junk food out of the house
- **Principle**: you are competing against your tired, tempted, distracted future self — give that self fewer options

### 2. Pre-Commitment Contracts
Formally lock in a behavioral obligation, often with social or financial stakes.
- **Examples**: paying a trainer in advance (sunk cost); publicly announcing a goal; joining a class that starts at a fixed time
- The [[Habit Contract]] (Ch17) is an explicit written version: specify the behavior, the stakes, and an accountability partner who enforces consequences

### 3. One-Time Actions
Single decisions that pay dividends indefinitely without requiring further willpower.
- **Examples**: buying a standing desk; setting up automatic savings transfers; subscribing to a meal-prep service; enrolling in auto-pay
- These are the highest-leverage commitment devices — one act of discipline prevents hundreds of future temptations

## Automation as Commitment Device

Modern technology enables commitment devices at scale:
- **Automatic bill pay** → never miss a payment, never tempted to spend the money elsewhere
- **Auto-savings** → wealth builds before the money can be spent
- **App blockers** → social media is inaccessible during designated work hours
- **Pre-set workouts in calendar** → the decision is already made

Clear's argument: investing time to set up one-time automations is among the highest-ROI uses of self-discipline.

## Temporal Discounting Problem

Commitment devices exist because of **temporal discounting**: we naturally devalue future rewards relative to immediate ones. The future benefit of not eating the junk food is abstract; the immediate pleasure is concrete.

A commitment device makes the future consequence **immediate** (social cost, sunk cost, physical barrier) — this rebalances the discounting.

## Design Rules

1. **Set the device when calm and rational** — not in the moment of temptation
2. **Make reversal effortful** — a commitment device you can easily undo is not a commitment device
3. **Combine with positive reward** — removing the bad option is more effective when paired with an easy good option (see [[Two-Minute Rule]])

# Values Framework (Manson)

From *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck* (Ch4), Mark Manson's central claim is that the quality of your life is determined by the quality of your values — not your circumstances, relationships, or success. Pain is inevitable; what determines wellbeing is what you believe makes that pain worthwhile.

## The Core Principle

> "If you want to change how you see your problems, you have to change what you value and/or how you measure failure/success."

Good values make good problems worth solving. Bad values make problems feel meaningless or unsolvable regardless of outcome.

## Good vs Bad Values

| Dimension | Good Values | Bad Values |
|---|---|---|
| **Basis** | Reality-based | Superstition / fantasy |
| **Control** | Internally controlled | Externally dependent |
| **Effect** | Constructive | Destructive or zero-sum |
| **Examples** | Honesty, creativity, humility, contribution, curiosity | Popularity, material success, pleasure, always being right, "being liked" |

### Why Bad Values Fail

Bad values are externally controlled — you cannot reliably achieve them because they depend on other people's responses, market conditions, or luck. Building your self-worth on them means your sense of success or failure is always outside your hands. This creates permanent anxiety and the [[Hedonic Treadmill]].

Bad values that appear neutral or positive:
- **Pleasure**: is a byproduct of living well, not a goal. Chasing pleasure directly produces the [[Backwards Law]] effect
- **Material success**: above a baseline, does not improve wellbeing; see [[Hedonic Treadmill]]
- **Always being right**: prevents learning; requires defending wrong positions; see Ch6 of the book
- **Staying positive**: toxic when used to suppress valid negative information; see [[Toxic Positivity]]

## The Self-Awareness Onion

Manson's model of self-knowledge has three nested layers. Most people only access the outer layers:

```
Layer 1 (surface): What emotion am I feeling?
  └── Layer 2 (middle): Why am I feeling it?
        └── Layer 3 (core): What is my measure of success/failure?
```

**Layer 3 is where your actual values live.** It is also the hardest to access because it requires questioning the assumptions you built your identity on.

### Layer 1 — Emotion Identification
Basic emotional awareness. "I feel anxious." Many people stop here, treating the emotion as an unexplainable fact. Overlaps with [[Emotion Granularity]] (Susan David's tool for precise labeling).

### Layer 2 — Causal Awareness
Why is this feeling present? "I feel anxious because I'm worried about the presentation going badly." This is narrative level — the story you tell about the feeling.

### Layer 3 — Values Audit
What standard am I using to judge whether this is a success or failure? "I feel anxious because my metric for success is 'everyone likes me and nothing goes wrong' — and I can't control that." This is where change actually happens.

> Most people never reach Layer 3. They manage Layer 1 symptoms without addressing the Layer 3 cause.

## Internal vs External Values

The critical axis is **locus of control**:

- **Internal values** (e.g., "Did I show up honestly?", "Did I do my best work?") — you can always achieve these regardless of outcome
- **External values** (e.g., "Did they like me?", "Did I win?") — always partially outside your control; failure is always possible no matter how hard you work

This maps to the Stoic distinction between what is "up to us" (prohairesis) and what is not. Manson arrives at the same place through a different frame.

## Practical Application

To audit your values, ask Layer 3 questions:
1. What counts as "winning" for me in this situation?
2. Is that metric something I can actually control?
3. If I can't control it, what *internal* metric could replace it?
4. What would I need to believe about myself and the world for that replacement to feel real?

# Paradox of Choice

The empirical finding that more options produce *less* satisfaction after choosing, not more. First systematically documented by psychologist Barry Schwartz in *The Paradox of Choice* (2004). Applied by Mark Manson in *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck* (Ch8) to argue for the value of commitment.

## Statement

> More options → more anxiety before deciding, more second-guessing after deciding, less satisfaction with the chosen option.

The mechanism: when you have few options, you commit fully to the best one. When you have many options, you remain half-committed to what you chose, perpetually wondering whether one of the unchosen alternatives would have been better (**counterfactual regret**).

## Schwartz's Original Research

Barry Schwartz showed that in the era of consumer abundance, more choice produces:
1. **Decision paralysis** — people often make worse or no decisions when overwhelmed with options
2. **Post-choice regret** — the more alternatives foregone, the more the chosen option is measured against an imagined "best" rather than evaluated on its own merits
3. **Diminished satisfaction** — achieved outcomes feel less good when other options existed

The paradox: choice is supposed to maximize welfare (classical economics), but beyond a threshold it consistently reduces it.

## Manson's Application: Depth vs Breadth

Manson extends this from consumer choices to life choices:

> "The avoidance of rejection in a relationship comes at the cost of intimacy. To commit to one relationship requires rejecting all others."

The same dynamic applies to:
- **Relationships**: keeping options open prevents the full commitment that produces deep connection
- **Careers**: dabbling in multiple fields prevents the expertise that produces mastery and recognition
- **Values**: holding many possible value systems prevents full commitment to any, which prevents the depth of experience those values can produce

The insight: **to value X, you must reject non-X.** Commitment is not a constraint on freedom — it is the mechanism by which meaning is created.

## The Commitment Paradox

| Approach | Experience | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Keep all options open | Perpetual low-level dissatisfaction; grass-is-greener thinking | Breadth without depth; chronic mild regret |
| Commit to one path | Initial loss of alternatives; freedom from second-guessing | Depth, mastery, genuine satisfaction possible |

This is a version of the [[Backwards Law]]: the more you try to preserve optionality (freedom), the less free you feel. The more you commit (restrict options), the more satisfaction you can access.

## Application to Relationships

The **Victim/Saver dynamic** Manson describes in Ch8 is a failure to commit to honest relationship values:
- The Saver cannot commit to saying no (keeping the option to be needed/important open)
- The Victim cannot commit to responsibility (keeping the option of blame open)

Both maintain optionality — and both avoid the depth that genuine commitment and clear boundaries produce.

Good relationship model: own your problems, let your partner own theirs, help where you genuinely can. This requires *committing* to a boundary rather than keeping all relationship options open.

## Application to Career and Skills

The Paradox of Choice is why specialist expertise produces career leverage that generalism cannot:
- The EE who specializes in SiC power electronics is less replaceable than the EE who "knows a little of everything"
- Depth requires excluding other skill trees to invest in one

# Do Something Principle

From *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck* (Ch7, p.155). The Do Something Principle is Mark Manson's inversion of the conventional motivation model. It states that **action comes first** — not inspiration, not motivation.

## The Conventional (Wrong) Model

> Inspiration → Motivation → Action

The cultural assumption: you need to *feel* inspired or motivated before you can act. You wait for the right mood, the right moment, the right energy level. This produces chronic procrastination, because motivation is unreliable and often never arrives.

## The Do Something Principle

> **Action → Inspiration → Motivation**

Act first — even poorly, even just a small step. The action itself generates feedback. Feedback generates engagement. Engagement generates inspiration. Inspiration generates motivation to do more.

Manson: "If you lack the motivation to make an important change in your life, do something — anything, really — and then harness the reaction to that action as a way to begin motivating yourself."

## Why It Works

1. **Momentum**: a body in motion stays in motion; the first action lowers the threshold for the second
2. **Feedback breaks paralysis**: action produces real-world feedback that imagination cannot; feedback is what actually generates emotional engagement
3. **Identity shift via behavior**: doing the action, even imperfectly, begins to make you the kind of person who does that action — overlaps with [[Identity-Based vs Goal-Based Habits]]
4. **Limbic friction decreases**: [[Limbic Friction]] is highest at the start; the first action overrides the anxious/lethargic state by brute force; subsequent actions cost less

## Contrast with Motivation-First Assumption

| Frame | Starting Point | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Motivation-first** | Wait to feel ready | Readiness often never arrives; procrastination loop |
| **Do Something Principle** | Act regardless of readiness | Action itself creates readiness and momentum |

The motivation-first model is a version of the [[Backwards Law]] applied to productivity: the more you wait to feel motivated, the less motivated you feel.

## Application

The Do Something Principle is most powerful when:
- You are procrastinating on something important (career change, difficult conversation, new project)
- You are waiting to "feel ready" before starting
- Perfectionism is preventing first drafts or first attempts

The antidote is to commit to the smallest possible first action — not the whole task:
- Writing a book: open the document and type one sentence
- Exercising: put on your shoes and walk to the door
- Learning a skill: open the resource for five minutes

The minimum viable action breaks the paralysis. The rest follows from momentum.

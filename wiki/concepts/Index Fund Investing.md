# Index Fund Investing

> The mathematically dominant investing strategy for the vast majority of people: own the whole market at minimal cost and hold forever.

## What Is an Index Fund?

An index fund is a fund that tracks a market index — a predefined basket of securities — instead of relying on a human manager to pick stocks. It is fully systematic: when a stock joins the S&P 500, the fund buys it; when a stock leaves, the fund sells it.

**Key properties:**
- No active management decisions
- Very low costs (0.03%–0.05% expense ratio vs. 0.5%–1.5% for active funds)
- Instant diversification (VTI owns 3,900+ US companies)
- Tax-efficient (low turnover = fewer taxable events)

---

## Why Index Funds Win

**The math is definitive:**
- Over any 15-year period, 92% of actively managed large-cap funds underperform the S&P 500 (S&P SPIVA 2025)
- Over 20-year periods, the underperformance figure approaches 95%+
- The average active fund charges 0.8–1.2% annually; VTI charges 0.03%

**The compounding cost of fees:**
| Annual Expense Ratio | $100k for 30 years at 10% gross return |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------|
| 0.03% (VTI/FXAIX) | $1,744,940 |
| 0.50% (many 401k funds) | $1,467,160 |
| 1.00% (average active fund) | $1,244,100 |
| 1.50% (expensive active fund) | $1,055,320 |

The 1.5% vs. 0.03% difference = **$689,620 lost to fees on one $100k investment**.

## Recommended Portfolios by Simplicity Level

### Level 1 (Maximum Simplicity)
One fund: **VTWAX** or **VT** — entire global stock market. Rebalancing is automatic. Zero decisions required.

### Level 2 (Classic 2-Fund)
- 70% **VTI** (US stocks)
- 30% **VXUS** (International stocks)

Rebalance annually. Matches global market capitalization weighting.

### Level 3 (3-Fund Portfolio — Bogle Model)
- 60% **VTI** (US stocks)
- 30% **VXUS** (International)
- 10% **BND** (US bonds — appropriate as you age; less so at 19)

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Invest the same fixed dollar amount at regular intervals (every paycheck, every month) regardless of market conditions.

**Why it works:**
- Automatically buys more shares when prices are low
- Prevents paralysis from trying to "time the market"
- Time in market > timing the market: missing the 10 best S&P days over 2000–2020 cut returns from 6% to 2.4%

**Implementation:** Set up automatic recurring investment in brokerage — $X on the 1st and 15th of every month. Never touch it.

## Compound Interest: The Core Math

At 10% average annual return (approximate S&P 500 long-run average):

| Years Invested | $1,000 Grows To | $10,000 Grows To |
|---------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 10 | $2,594 | $25,937 |
| 20 | $6,727 | $67,275 |
| 30 | $17,449 | $174,494 |
| 40 | $45,259 | $452,593 |
| 46 (19 → 65) | $84,000 | $840,000 |

**Age matters more than amount.** $1 invested at 19 is worth 5.5× more at 65 than $1 invested at 35. This is why the Roth IRA at 19 is the most powerful financial tool available to Joe right now.

## Opening a Roth IRA and Buying Your First Index Fund

**Step-by-step (Fidelity):**
1. Go to fidelity.com → Open an Account → Roth IRA
2. Complete identity verification (SSN, ID)
3. Fund with bank transfer ($25 minimum; no account minimum at Fidelity)
4. Go to Trade → search "FZROX" (0% expense ratio) or "FXAIX" (S&P 500)
5. Select "Buy" → Dollar amount → Market order → Confirm
6. Set up recurring monthly investment under "Automatic Investments"

**2026 Roth IRA Rules:**
- Contribution limit: **$7,500/year** (under age 50)
- Income limit (single): phase-out begins at $153,000 MAGI; irrelevant at student income levels
- Requires earned income (W-2 or 1099) equal to or greater than contribution amount
- Deadline: April 15 of the following year (can contribute for 2026 until April 15, 2027)

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| Waiting to invest until you have "enough money" | Time in market is the variable; $25/month starting at 19 beats $500/month starting at 30 |
| Trying to pick individual stocks | Requires beating 92% of professional managers who fail; don't try |
| Selling when the market drops | You only lose if you sell; downturns are sales on shares |
| Watching the portfolio constantly | Quarterly check-ins max; daily watching drives emotional decisions |
| Owning too many funds | VTI + VXUS = 10,000+ companies; adding more doesn't diversify, it confuses |

## The Mindset Shift (Behavioral Finance)

From *The Psychology of Money* (Housel):
- **"Save for nothing in particular"** — accumulated savings is optionality, not a number
- **Wealth is what you don't spend** — the Porsche signals spending capacity, not wealth
- **Long tails dominate** — most of your lifetime investment returns will come from a handful of great years; staying invested through bad years is the only way to capture them
- **Reasonable beats rational** — a strategy you can emotionally sustain for 40 years beats an optimal strategy you abandon in the first crash

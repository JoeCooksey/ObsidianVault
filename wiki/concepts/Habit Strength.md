# Habit Strength

Habit strength is a measure of how deeply a behavior is embedded in the nervous system. It is determined by two independent axes:

## Axis 1: Context Independence

Does the habit occur regardless of environment, time of day, or circumstance?

- **Context-dependent habit** (weak): only executes in familiar settings; disrupted by travel, schedule changes, or novel environments
- **Context-independent habit** (strong): executes consistently whether at home, traveling, or under stress

Context independence is the result of the behavior migrating from hippocampal-dependent procedural memory into basal ganglia circuits via [[Task Bracketing]]. Once that migration is complete, the habit no longer relies on environmental cues to trigger it.

**Test for context independence**: deliberately perform the habit at a different time of day or in a different location. If it still happens naturally, it is context-independent.

## Axis 2: Limbic Friction Required

How much activation energy is needed to initiate the habit?

- **High limbic friction** (weak): requires deliberate conscious override; fragile under poor conditions
- **Low / zero limbic friction** (strong): initiates near-automatically; executes even when tired, stressed, or distracted

See [[Limbic Friction]] for the two sources of friction (over-arousal and under-arousal).

## Combined Assessment

| Context | Limbic Friction | Habit Strength |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Dependent | High | Shallow — newly forming |
| Dependent | Low | Moderate — partially consolidated |
| Independent | High | Moderate — familiar but effortful |
| Independent | Low | Strong — fully consolidated, automatic |

## Relationship to Automaticity

A fully formed habit — one that has reached **automaticity** — scores low on limbic friction and high on context independence simultaneously. This is the functional definition of a habit that has been successfully wired into the nervous system. See [[Neuroplasticity and Habit Formation]].

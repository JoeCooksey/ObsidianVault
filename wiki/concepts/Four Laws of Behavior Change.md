# Four Laws of Behavior Change

[[James Clear]]'s design framework from *Atomic Habits* (2018) for building good habits and breaking bad ones. Each law corresponds to one stage of the [[Habit Loop]].

## The Four Laws

| # | Law (Building) | Inversion (Breaking) | Loop Stage |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Make it **Obvious** | Make it **Invisible** | Cue |
| 2 | Make it **Attractive** | Make it **Unattractive** | Craving |
| 3 | Make it **Easy** | Make it **Difficult** | Response |
| 4 | Make it **Satisfying** | Make it **Unsatisfying** | Reward |

---

## Law 1 — Make It Obvious (Cue Design)

The habit must be noticed. Most habits run below conscious awareness — the cue must be surfaced.

**Techniques:**
- **Habits Scorecard**: list all daily habits; label + / – / =; verbalize them. Pointing and Calling (Japanese railway technique) raises error rates by 85%
- **[[Implementation Intention]]**: "I will [Behavior] at [Time] in [Location]." Makes the cue explicit in advance
- **[[Habit Stacking]]**: "After [Current Habit] I will [New Habit]." Chains new cue to an existing, reliable signal
- **Environment Design**: place cues in the visual field (book on pillow, guitar on stand, fruit on counter)

**Inversion (Make It Invisible):**
- Remove the cue from the environment entirely
- The battle against bad habits is won at the environmental level, not through willpower
- Example: put the phone in another room to stop doom-scrolling

---

## Law 2 — Make It Attractive (Craving Design)

Habits are dopamine-driven. The brain craves the *anticipated* state change, not the habit itself.

**Techniques:**
- **[[Temptation Bundling]]**: pair something you WANT with something you NEED — dopamine from the "want" bleeds into the "need"
- **Social environment**: join communities where your desired behavior is normal; "The Many" is the most powerful social influence (see [[Atomic Habits - James Clear]] Ch9)
- **Motivation Ritual**: do something enjoyable immediately before a hard habit — transfers dopamine anticipation

**Inversion (Make It Unattractive):**
- Reframe: highlight the benefits of *not* doing the bad habit
- Identify the underlying need (reduce uncertainty? gain status?) and find a better way to satisfy it

---

## Law 3 — Make It Easy (Response Design)

The response only fires if friction is low enough. Reducing friction is more reliable than increasing motivation.

**Techniques:**
- **[[Two-Minute Rule]]**: start every new habit as a <2 minute action; master the art of showing up
- **Environment Priming**: reduce activation steps — lay out gym clothes, pre-load the app, keep instruments accessible
- **Law of Least Effort**: design the path of least resistance toward the good habit
- **Decisive Moments**: identify the small choice-forks in each day (ordering food, sitting down after work) and pre-decide

**Inversion (Make It Difficult):**
- **[[Commitment Device]]**: remove future optionality — uninstall apps, delete accounts, use website blockers
- Increase friction: put junk food in a high cabinet; put the TV remote in another room; log out of social media

---

## Law 4 — Make It Satisfying (Reward Design)

The brain encodes behaviors that produce immediate satisfaction. Good habits typically have delayed rewards — this must be corrected.

**Techniques:**
- Add immediate reward: track a habit and mark it complete; use a visual chain (don't break the streak)
- **Never Miss Twice**: the streak rule — one miss is an accident, two is a new habit. This makes recovery automatic
- **Habit Tracking**: provides three things — (1) visual cue to start, (2) motivation to continue, (3) immediate satisfaction on completion
- **Cardinal Rule**: never reward a good habit with something that reinforces a bad one

**Inversion (Make It Unsatisfying):**
- **Habit Contract**: written social agreement with consequences; accountability partner adds social cost of failure
- Make the cost of bad habits visible and immediate

---

## Applying the Framework

**For a new habit (e.g., daily studying):**
1. Obvious: stack it after coffee; open the book before sitting down
2. Attractive: use [[Temptation Bundling]] — favorite playlist only during study sessions
3. Easy: [[Two-Minute Rule]] — start with just opening the textbook for 2 minutes
4. Satisfying: track it on a habit tracker; mark the day; aim to not break the chain

**For breaking a bad habit (e.g., phone checking):**
1. Invisible: leave phone in another room
2. Unattractive: notice how it makes you feel (anxious, time-lost)
3. Difficult: use an app lock with a delay
4. Unsatisfying: track the habit negatively; enlist an accountability partner

# Stoicism Self-Study Roadmap

> A sequenced reading and practice plan for Stoic philosophy — from zero to fluent practitioner in 6 months. Extends [[Book List — Stoicism and Philosophy]] with a specific sequence, YouTube resources, and practice schedule.

---

## Why This Reading Order Matters

Most people start with *Meditations* and bounce — the Marcus Aurelius quotes feel abstract without context. The correct sequence installs the vocabulary and mental models before the primary texts. Ryan Holiday → Epictetus → Marcus Aurelius is the fastest path to actual practice.

---

## Month 1 — Foundation (Modern On-Ramp)

### Week 1–2: *The Daily Stoic* — Ryan Holiday & Steve Hanselman (2016)
- One 1-page entry per day; begin on any date
- Provides the interpretive vocabulary (dichotomy of control, premeditatio, amor fati, memento mori) before encountering them in the primary texts
- **Action**: buy the physical book; keep it on your nightstand; read one entry per morning

### Week 3–4: *Ego Is the Enemy* — Ryan Holiday (2016)
- Targeted at ambitious young people (19yo with research + engineering ambitions = exactly the target)
- Three stages where ego destroys: Aspiring, Succeeding, Failing — maps perfectly to where Joe is now (aspiring)
- **Key chapter**: "Becoming a Student" — why beginners must stay perpetual students; directly applicable to FURI and research lab culture
- **Action**: read in 3–5 sessions; take notes on which ego pattern you recognize in yourself

---

## Month 2 — Epictetus (The Rigorous One)

### The Enchiridion — Epictetus (~108 AD, translated by Robert Dobbin or George Long)
- 30 pages; the most concentrated practical Stoicism ever written
- Free online at Project Gutenberg or Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy
- "Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things" — the precursor to CBT (Albert Ellis explicitly cited Epictetus)
- **Action**: read in one sitting; then reread paragraph 1 ("Some things are in our power, some are not") every morning for a week

### *The Obstacle Is the Way* — Ryan Holiday (2014)
- Three disciplines: Perception, Action, Will
- Historical case studies make Stoic principles concrete and memorable
- **Best chapters**: Rockefeller (Chapter 1, Perception) and Marcus Aurelius (throughout)

---

## Month 3 — Marcus Aurelius (The Emperor)

### *Meditations* — Marcus Aurelius (~170 AD, Gregory Hays translation, 2002)
- Gregory Hays (Modern Library) is the best modern English translation — contemporary, no archaic language
- This is a private journal, never meant to be published — it's the most honest document of a person trying to live up to their philosophy
- **Reading strategy**: 1 entry per day; Books 2, 4, 5, and 7 are highest density — start there
- **Don't read linearly**: Meditations has no narrative arc; dip in anywhere after the first read-through
- **Action**: pair with *The Daily Stoic* practice; after reading an entry, write one sentence about how it applies to today

---

## Month 4 — Seneca (The Practical One)

### *Letters from a Stoic* (Epistulae Morales) — Seneca (65 AD, Robin Campbell translation)
- 124 letters; Seneca is the most witty, accessible, and practical of the three core Stoics
- Read these letters (in order of importance): 1 (on time), 7 (on crowds), 12 (on aging), 47 (on masters and slaves), 77 (on death), 80 (on freedom)
- **Letter 1** is the most important: "Claim time for yourself, Lucilius; gather and save it."
- **Action**: read 2–3 letters per week; treat each as a mentor writing to you specifically

---

## Month 5–6 — Deepening

### *How to Be a Stoic* — Massimo Pigliucci (2017)
- Former evolutionary biologist turned Stoic philosopher; most intellectually rigorous modern treatment
- Uses Epictetus as a Socratic guide through practical scenarios (health, illness, relationships, death)
- Best for a scientific mindset — connects Stoicism to evolutionary biology and empirical ethics

### *A Guide to the Good Life* — William Irvine (2008)
- Most accessible academic treatment; structured around Stoic exercises with modern commentary
- Best chapter: on negative visualization — the practice of imagining worst cases to cultivate gratitude

## The Daily Practice Stack

Books without practice are tourism. The Stoics were insistent on daily exercise (*askesis*):

**Morning (5 min)**
1. Read one entry from *The Daily Stoic*
2. Write one sentence: "What could go wrong today, and how will I respond?" (premeditatio malorum)
3. Write one sentence: "What virtue do I want to exercise today?"

**Evening (5 min)**
1. Write one sentence: "Where did I fail to live up to my principles today?"
2. Write one sentence: "Where did I hold them under pressure?"
3. Ask: "Did anything happen today that I let disturb me that was outside my control?"

**Weekly**
- Negative visualization: 5 minutes deliberately imagining losing something you value (health, a relationship, a goal) — not to be depressed, but to deepen gratitude and reduce complacency

## Key Mental Models (Quick Reference)

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Dichotomy of Control** | Only your judgments, impulses, desires, and aversions are yours; everything else is not | Before reacting to anything: ask "is this mine to control?" |
| **Premeditatio Malorum** | Premeditate the bad; imagine worst cases in advance | Removes the shock value of setbacks; convert surprises to plans |
| **Amor Fati** | Love of fate; embrace what happens as if you had chosen it | Converts suffering into data and resistance into energy |
| **Memento Mori** | Remember you will die | Makes the present serious and precious; cures procrastination |
| **Sympatheia** | Interconnectedness of all things | Reduces tribalism; increases perspective-taking |

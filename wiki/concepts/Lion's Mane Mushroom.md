# Lion's Mane Mushroom

Lion's mane (*Hericium erinaceus*) is a functional mushroom studied for nerve growth factor (NGF) stimulation and potential cognitive and mood benefits. Human evidence is early and limited.

## Mechanism

Hericenones and erinacines (bioactive compounds) cross the blood-brain barrier and may stimulate NGF synthesis — a protein involved in neuron growth and maintenance. Theoretical benefit for neuroplasticity over time.

## Evidence Quality: Low (in young healthy adults)

**Best available RCT in young adults (2023 pilot study):**
- Design: Double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel groups, 41 healthy adults aged 18–45 (mean 26 years)
- Dose: 1.8g/day for 28 days
- **Acute (single dose)**: Significantly faster on Stroop task at 60 min (p=0.005); reduced accuracy on word recall
- **Chronic (28 days)**: Trend toward reduced subjective stress (p=0.051); null/negative findings on most cognitive measures
- Authors: results "should be interpreted with caution" due to small sample size

**Key caveat from Examine.com:** "Lion's mane may not improve cognitive function" — summarizing a null result in a different trial.

## Honest Assessment

The animal data is compelling; the human data is thin. Most positive human studies used older adults with mild cognitive impairment. In healthy young adults, the acute effects on processing speed are tentative (one small pilot), and chronic cognitive benefits are unconfirmed.

## Practical Details

**Dosage:** 500–3,000 mg/day fruiting body extract; or 1–1.8g/day as tested in the pilot study
- **Extract type matters**: Look for "fruiting body" extract, not mycelium on grain (lower active compound content)
- Lion's mane dual-extract (hot water + alcohol) has best bioavailability

**Timing:** Consistent daily use — acute effects are minor; chronic NGF stimulation is the proposed mechanism.

**Safety:** No known significant side effects at studied doses. Generally well-tolerated.

## Verdict

Low certainty, low risk, moderate cost. Reasonable to try for a semester as part of a stack — but should not be high on the priority list vs foundational supplements with stronger evidence.

# Food Health Tier List — Vegetables

Ranking criteria: **pure healthiness** — CDC Powerhouse nutrient density scoring system (17 qualifying nutrients per 100 kcal), anti-cancer phytochemical content, anti-inflammatory properties, fiber, and disease prevention evidence.

CDC Powerhouse definition: provides ≥10% daily value per 100 kcal across 17 nutrients (protein, fiber, potassium, calcium, iron, zinc, vitamins A, B6, B12, C, D, E, K, folate, thiamin, riboflavin, niacin).

## S Tier — Nutritional Royalty

| Vegetable                      | CDC Score | Why                                                                                                                                                       |
| ------------------------------ | --------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Watercress**                 | 100/100   | Perfect nutrient density; more calcium than milk gram-for-gram; more vitamin C than oranges; 50+ vitamins and minerals; strongest overall in CDC analysis |
| **Chinese Cabbage / Bok Choy** | 91.99     | Cruciferous; glucosinolates → cancer cell inhibition; high calcium for a vegetable; vitamin C, folate                                                     |
| **Swiss Chard**                | 89.27     | Vitamin K (bone health), magnesium (muscle/nerve function), iron, potassium; extremely high mineral density                                               |
| **Beet Greens**                | 87.08     | Vitamin K, potassium, iron; often thrown away — the green tops are more nutritious than the beet itself                                                   |
| **Spinach**                    | 86.43     | Iron, folate, vitamins A/K, lutein + zeaxanthin (eye protection); versatile; one of most studied vegetables                                               |

## A Tier — Daily Driver Powerhouses

| Vegetable | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| **Kale** | Glucosinolates (indole-3-carbinol); calcium (more bioavailable than dairy calcium per calorie); vitamin K, C; cancer-protective phytochemicals |
| **Collard Greens** | High CDC score; most calcium per serving of any cruciferous; vitamin K, folate |
| **Arugula** | Cruciferous; dietary nitrates → blood pressure reduction; vitamin K; top-10 CDC vegetable |
| **Broccoli** | Sulforaphane — most well-researched cancer-fighting phytochemical; vitamin C (one serving = 135% DV); folate; fiber |
| **Brussels Sprouts** | Glucosinolates; vitamin C, vitamin K; high fiber; shown to reduce LDL oxidation |
| **Mustard Greens** | Top-20 CDC score; folate, vitamins A/C/K; cancer-protective |
| **Romaine Lettuce** | High CDC score (63.48); vitamins A, K, folate; far more nutritious than iceberg |
| **Garlic** | Did not meet CDC powerhouse threshold, but allicin → demonstrated cardiovascular and antibacterial benefits; used medicinally for 5,000 years |

## B Tier — Solid Contributions

| Vegetable | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| **Bell Peppers (Red/Orange)** | Vitamin C: red bell pepper has 3× the vitamin C of an orange; vitamin A (from beta-carotene); antioxidants; low calorie |
| **Asparagus** | Folate (critical for DNA synthesis), prebiotic inulin fiber (gut microbiome), vitamin K; diuretic properties |
| **Tomatoes** | Lycopene — strongest association with reduced prostate cancer risk; vitamin C; cooked tomatoes have more bioavailable lycopene than raw |
| **Carrots** | Beta-carotene → vitamin A; fiber; eye health; GI is low in whole form (~39) |
| **Mushrooms** | Only plant source of vitamin D (especially when sun-dried); selenium; ergothioneine antioxidant; B vitamins |
| **Cauliflower** | Cruciferous; glucosinolates; vitamin C; low carb; choline (often deficient in plant-based diets) |
| **Cabbage** | Inexpensive cruciferous; vitamin K, C; fermented cabbage (sauerkraut) adds probiotic value |
| **Onions** | Quercetin (anti-inflammatory flavonoid); prebiotics; allicin (similar to garlic); did not meet CDC powerhouse threshold but strong phytochemical evidence |
| **Beets** | Dietary nitrates → vasodilation → blood pressure reduction and athletic performance; folate; betalain antioxidants |
| **Peas** | Plant protein (8g/cup); fiber; vitamins A/C/K; lutein; one of the more protein-dense vegetables |

## C Tier — Good But Not Stars

| Vegetable | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| **Sweet Potato** | Lowest CDC powerhouse score (10.51) despite reputation as "superfood"; beta-carotene and potassium are genuine, but higher sugar/GI than most vegetables (~63) |
| **Potato (plain)** | Potassium, vitamin C, B6; but high GI when baked (~85); resistant starch when cooled improves this; toppings are the real danger |
| **Zucchini / Squash** | Low calorie, hydrating, vitamin C; but low nutrient density overall |
| **Eggplant** | Nasunin antioxidant; fiber; but relatively low across most nutrient categories |
| **Green Beans** | Vitamin K, C, fiber; but low protein and moderate nutrient density overall |
| **Corn** | Often considered a vegetable but is starchy; lutein and zeaxanthin are positives; but mostly simple starch, low in most nutrients |

## D Tier — Minimal Nutritional Value

| Vegetable | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| **Iceberg Lettuce** | 96% water; very low nutrient density across all 17 CDC nutrients; essentially filler |
| **Celery** | Mostly water and fiber; minimal vitamins/minerals; low calorie but also low payoff |
| **Cucumber** | Hydrating; some vitamin K; very low nutrient density — more of a hydration tool than nutrition |
| **Radishes** | Low calorie, some vitamin C; but negligible nutrient density |

## Key Principles for Vegetables

1. **Cruciferous = cancer-protective**: Broccoli, kale, bok choy, Brussels sprouts all contain glucosinolates → sulforaphane — the most evidence-backed food compound for cancer prevention
2. **Dark green = more nutritious**: Color intensity correlates with nutrient density. Romaine > iceberg. Kale > cabbage.
3. **Eat greens daily**: 6 of the top 10 CDC powerhouse vegetables are dark leafy greens
4. **Cooking matters**: Light steaming preserves more nutrients than boiling; roasting concentrates flavor but may degrade some water-soluble vitamins; raw retains enzymes
5. **Garlic and onions** missed the CDC powerhouse threshold but have strong mechanistic evidence — include them

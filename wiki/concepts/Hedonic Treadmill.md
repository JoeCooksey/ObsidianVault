# Hedonic Treadmill

Also called **hedonic adaptation**. The empirically well-supported phenomenon where people return to a stable baseline level of happiness after major positive or negative life events. Working hard to change your situation produces temporary emotional gains or losses that fade as you adapt.

## Definition

> You work hard, achieve something, feel better for a while — then return to roughly the same baseline happiness level you started at.

The "treadmill" metaphor: no matter how fast you walk (how hard you pursue better circumstances), you stay in the same place emotionally.

## Origins

The concept was formally introduced by Brickman and Campbell in *Hedonic Relativism and Planning the Good Society* (1971) and expanded by psychologists Michael Eysenck and others. It is also called **adaptation-level theory**.

Mark Manson uses it in *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck* (Ch2) to support his argument that choosing goals by the reward they promise is a fool's errand — the reward adapts away.

## Key Evidence

- **Lottery winners** return to baseline happiness within one year (Brickman et al., 1978 — the original landmark study)
- **Paraplegics** return to near-baseline within two years in the same study
- **Income and happiness**: above ~$75k–$100k household income (US), additional income produces diminishing marginal happiness gains (Kahneman & Deaton, 2010); updated research suggests the relationship continues but flattens
- **Material purchases**: new cars, houses, clothes generate a spike of satisfaction that fades within weeks to months
- **Status**: promotions and social recognition produce temporary mood lifts that adapt

## What Doesn't Adapt (or Adapts Slowly)

Research identifies categories of experience that produce more durable wellbeing — i.e., where the treadmill effect is weaker:

1. **Meaningful relationships**: social connection is the most robust predictor of long-term wellbeing (Harvard Study of Adult Development, 80+ years)
2. **Autonomy**: control over your time and work resists adaptation longer than income gains
3. **Engagement / flow**: activities that challenge skill produce sustained wellbeing because they change as you grow
4. **Health behaviors**: sleep, exercise, nutrition — their wellbeing benefits are constant and non-adapting because they are ongoing physiological inputs, not one-time events

This last point connects directly to [[Biohacking Tier List]] — the S-tier interventions (sleep, Zone 2 cardio, morning sunlight) produce their benefits continuously, unlike purchases or status upgrades.

## Manson's Use of the Concept

Manson's conclusion from the Hedonic Treadmill is the pivot of Ch2: **choose goals by what pain you're willing to endure, not the reward you're seeking.** If the reward adapts away, then the only thing that actually differentiates life outcomes is what you were willing to suffer for.

This reframes the entire self-help question:
- Wrong question: "What do I want?"
- Right question: "What am I willing to endure to get it?"

## Interaction with the Backwards Law

The [[Backwards Law]] and the Hedonic Treadmill describe the same phenomenon at different timescales:
- **Backwards Law**: in the short term, chasing positive experience produces negative experience
- **Hedonic Treadmill**: in the medium/long term, achieving positive circumstances produces adaptation back to baseline

Both suggest that the pursuit of better feelings/circumstances is not the right frame for building a good life.

## Practical Implications

1. **Do not choose careers primarily for salary** above a reasonable baseline — the income gain adapts; the daily work reality does not
2. **Do not buy things to feel better** — the feeling adapts; the expense does not
3. **Invest in what resists adaptation**: relationships, autonomy, skills, health behaviors
4. **Choose suffering deliberately**: Manson's reframe — what is worth the ongoing cost? That is more durable than any reward
